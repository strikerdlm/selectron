# 04 — Existing Astronaut and Analog-Astronaut Selection Frameworks

Research agent **A2** comparison brief, Selectron project. Compiled 2026-05-18. Ten frameworks reviewed; one body-paywalled (ASTRA/AAD).

---

### ASTRA / AAD (Apollonio, Kring, Berry, Sawyer 2026)

- **Source(s)**: AIAA 2026-3000 abstract, https://doi.org/10.2514/6.2026-3000 (paywalled — abstract only).
- **Selection stages**: Not specified in the abstract. The paper positions AAD as a *standardized infrastructure* sitting inside the broader Analog Selection, Training, Reporting, and Analysis (ASTRA) framework — i.e. selection is one of four modules but the abstract does not enumerate stages.
- **Criterion families used**: Not enumerated. The authors argue that the field "lack[s] standardized qualification criteria and consistent certification processes, resulting in variability in participant readiness."
- **Instruments named**: None disclosed in the abstract.
- **Pass-rates**: None reported.
- **Notable quirks**: The Analog Astronaut Database (AAD) itself is the quirk — a cross-station registry intended to "improve accessibility, reduce fragmentation, and enable safer, and more consistent outcomes across HSA environments." It is the only framework in this review that targets *the analog sector as a whole* rather than a single station's crew. Authors are from Interstellar Performance Labs / Fort Hill Group.

### ESA Astronaut Selection (2021–2022 campaign)

- **Source(s)**: https://www.esa.int/About_Us/Careers_at_ESA/ESA_Astronaut_Selection/Astronaut_selection_2021-22_FAQs; Mittelstädt et al. 2016, doi:10.3357/amhp.4548.2016 (https://elib.dlr.de/109178/).
- **Selection stages**: six. "Stage 1: Screening … Stage 2: Test Phase 1 consists of cognitive, technical, motor coordination and personality tests. Stage 3 (Test Phase 2): psychological tests including group exercises as well as practical tests. Stage 4: Test Phase 3 is a medical selection … Stage 5: Interview Round 1 is a panel interview … Stage 6: Interview Round 2 is the last selection stage." (ESA FAQ.)
- **Criterion families used**: medical (European Part-MED Class 2, visual acuity 20/20, hearing ≤25 dB better ear, free from psychiatric disorders), psychological (cognitive, personality, group), professional (Master's plus three years' experience), language (English ≥ CEFR C1), age (< 50 at application).
- **Instruments named**: "European Part-MED, Class 2 medical examination" (Private Pilot certificate); DLR psychological battery includes **Temperament Structure Scales** and **Big Five** (Mittelstädt et al. 2016 keywords).
- **Pass-rates**: > 23,000 applications received; "more than 80%" of applications passed pre-screening (ESA FAQ). Final class size announcement not in the scraped FAQ — re-verify from a separate ESA news URL before use.
- **Notable quirks**: First major agency to run a parallel "Astronaut with a physical disability" vacancy (Parastronaut Feasibility Project). The medical and physical fitness components are "either pass or fail. It is not a graduated system" (ESA FAQ).

### NASA Astronaut Selection

- **Source(s)**: https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/astronaut-selection-timeline/; Slack, Holland & Sipes (NASA NTRS 20140006498), https://ntrs.nasa.gov/api/citations/20140006498/downloads/20140006498.pdf.
- **Selection stages**: USAJOBS vacancy → Qualifications Review → Highly Qualified review → Interviewees (initial interview at JSC) → Finalists → Final interview → Astronaut Candidate Class announcement (NASA 2024–2025 timeline). Internal Behavioral Health & Performance flow: Astronaut Selection Board (ASB) Round 1 → Astronaut Medical Board → ASB Round 2 → Team Exercises → Competency Based Summary → final ASB.
- **Criterion families used**: medical (NASA Astronaut Medical Board, NPR 1800.x), psychiatric / behavioral (BHP "Select-Out" for medical psychopathology, "Select-In/Suitability" for fit), competency / team performance, professional / academic.
- **Instruments named**: "clinical process" (Slack et al.); explicit competencies listed are **"Mental/Emotional stability, Performance under stressful conditions, Group living skills, Teamwork skills, Family issues."** Behaviourally-Anchored Rating Scales (BARS) tablet app used to score team exercises. Future-state additions called out: **Situational Judgment Test** and **Biodata**.
- **Pass-rates**: 2013 class — 6,113 applications → 4,552 basic-qualified → 483 rating-panel → 120 initial interview → 49 final interview → 8 selected (Slack et al. NTRS 20140006498). 2016 campaign drew "more then 18,000 applications" (Space Review). 2017 class: 12 selected (NASA timeline photo caption).
- **Notable quirks**: NASA's framework explicitly distinguishes a **binding "Select-Out"** medical-psychopathology decision from a graded **"Select-In / Suitability"** clinical judgement; "Once DQ always DQ" is an explicit slide in the BHP deck. Lower-fidelity team-task simulations are scored on tablets in real time.

### JAXA Astronaut Selection (5th campaign, 2008)

- **Source(s)**: Inoue N, Tachibana S. *An isolation and confinement facility for the selection of astronaut candidates.* Aviat Space Environ Med. 2013 Aug;84(8). PMID 23926666, doi:10.3357/asem.3188.2013 (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23926666/); JAXA reports https://iss.jaxa.jp/en/astro/report/2008/.
- **Selection stages**: three. "The final stage involved 10 of 963 applicants undergoing the first and second examination stages based on physical checks, written tests, and interviews. The third (final) stage focused on evaluating the behavioral attitudes required for ISS crewmembers."
- **Criterion families used**: medical (physical checks), cognitive (written tests), behavioral (longitudinal observation in isolation), professional (interviews).
- **Instruments named**: Methodology proper rather than a named scale: "10 examinees stayed in the isolation and confinement facility for 1 wk. During their stay, several group and personal tasks were assigned along with predetermined daily schedules covering aspects of leadership, teamwork ability, productivity (performance)". Assessors: "psychologists, psychiatrists, and JAXA officials/managers." Press-known: the **thousand-origami-cranes task** for monotony tolerance.
- **Pass-rates**: 963 applicants → 10 finalists → 3 selected (Inoue & Tachibana 2013).
- **Notable quirks**: The week-long isolation-chamber confinement is JAXA's signature: "Since it is difficult to assess these attitudes by paper testing or interview alone, JAXA has established an environment in which the assessors could observe the behavior of examinees on a continuous basis."

### D-MARS / AMADEE-20 Israeli Selection (Vizel-Parnas et al. 2020)

- **Source(s)**: Vizel-Parnas N, Barnett DL, Rubinstein H. *Insights from the analog astronauts selection and team building.* IAC-20,B3,9-GTS.2,3,x60789. Local copy at /root/repos/exports/2026-05-17_paper_vizel-parnas-2020-analog-astronaut-selection.md.
- **Selection stages**: five. (a) Publication of the Call → (b) Medical evaluation → (c) Professional, technical, scientific assessments → (d) Personality and behavioral evaluations (BBI) → (e) Psychological and mental evaluation.
- **Criterion families used**: medical, mental stability, professional (technology/engineering/science background or pilot), academic (PhD or working toward), behavioral (BBI), demographic (Israeli citizen, age 25–45, height 165–190 cm, weight > 55 kg, fluent English + Hebrew).
- **Instruments named**: **Behavioral Based Interview (BBI)** as the principal psychometric tool — "candidates were asked to provide examples of complex and challenging circumstances, which could demonstrate 'select-in' or 'select-out' measures." Standard medical exam: ECG, vital signs, visual acuity & colour-blind screening, cerebellar neurological test.
- **Pass-rates**: AMADEE-20 funnel — 83 applicants → 68 (medical) → 22 (academic/professional) → 9 (phone interview) → 5 (BBI + self-presentation) → 4 (medical + mental health) (paper Table 3).
- **Notable quirks**: The "select-in/select-out" language is borrowed directly from NASA BHP. Diversity in gender and minority representation is explicitly engineered into final crew composition.

### OEWF AMADEE / Class of 2019 (Austrian Space Forum)

- **Source(s)**: https://oewf.org/en/2019/01/analog-astronaut-selection-the-view-from-inside/ (candidate first-person account, Adam Crellin); Vizel-Parnas et al. 2020 (Innsbruck training schedule).
- **Selection stages**: Application → A-Selection → B-Selection (≈ 30 candidates, weekend in Innsbruck) → C-Selection (final 15, deeper testing) → final 6 + 2 non-European.
- **Criterion families used**: medical (sports medical evaluation at ISAG Sportmedizin Hall), psychological, fitness, knowledge / technical (knowledge test), motor-skill (hand-eye coordination, tactility test), team behaviour.
- **Instruments named**: not formally named; described as "basic fitness tests, administrative interviews, psychological evaluations, a hand-eye coordination test, and a medical examination" (Crellin 2019). C-selection adds a **tactility test** and a **knowledge test**.
- **Pass-rates**: Class 2019 — "≈ 30 candidates to proceed to the next round … down to the final 15 candidates" → 6 European + 2 non-European graduates (Class of 2019 included 13 individuals from 8 countries per Vizel-Parnas Table 3 narrative).
- **Notable quirks**: The **6 a.m. shorts-and-t-shirt run in –8 °C** Innsbruck winter is the most cited oddity ("freezing 45-min run before sunrise" in the Vizel-Parnas account), followed by the **chopsticks-and-rice grain-sorting task** (separating white and brown grains of rice with chopsticks). Current Analog Astronauts sit on the selection panel.

### HI-SEAS (University of Hawai'i / International MoonBase Alliance)

- **Source(s)**: https://www.hi-seas.org/about-hi-seas; LPI/USRA analog briefing https://www.hou.usra.edu/meetings/analogs2013/pdf/4044.pdf.
- **Selection stages**: researcher-curated (HI-SEAS explicitly notes "**We select the crew to meet our research needs (in serendipitous analogs, such as Antarctic stations, crew selection criteria are not controlled by researchers)**"). Application → eligibility verification → research-fit selection → crew slot.
- **Criterion families used**: research-fit (the dominant driver), professional / academic, demographic eligibility (no published numeric medical or psychological standard).
- **Instruments named**: None published on the public site; HI-SEAS leverages NASA-style behavioural-health protocols when running NASA-funded missions, but the about-page lists no specific scale.
- **Pass-rates**: not publicly reported per crew (six per mission; mission I–VI run 2013–2018; lunar simulations 2020–2022).
- **Notable quirks**: Selection criteria are **explicitly researcher-controlled** for experimental purposes — the team uses crew composition as an experimental variable in confinement/isolation studies. The 8,200-ft Mauna Loa site with 20-minute Mars-like one-way comms latency is the platform's defining feature.

### MDRS (Mars Desert Research Station, Mars Society)

- **Source(s)**: https://reports.marssociety.org/apply-to-become-a-crew-member/; MDRS 2023–2024 Applicant Instructions PDF (https://reports.marssociety.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/11/MDRS23-24Applicationplusinstructions.pdf).
- **Selection stages**: Pre-contact with Director Shannon Rupert → invitation to apply → role-specific application (4 parts) → committee review → conditional acceptance → fee payment → rotation.
- **Criterion families used**: role-fit (Commander, XO, HSO, Crew Engineer, GreenHab Officer, Scientist/Geologist/Biologist, Astronomer, Journalist/Artist), self-attested medical fitness, professional / academic, language (ILR scale; "at least three of the crew must speak English well. At least one must be fluent"), commercial (US$3,500 professional / US$2,000 student per 2-week rotation).
- **Instruments named**: HSO requires "**16 hours of in person training that resulted in current certification in first aid and CPR**"; ILR self-assessment for English; **self-attestation** statements for fitness and communicable disease ("I attest that I am sufficiently able-bodied …").
- **Pass-rates**: not publicly published; crews of 6 per 2-week rotation.
- **Notable quirks**: Selection is **fee-paying and self-attesting** — there is no agency-style medical exam. Commander qualification requires prior MDRS rotation or equivalent field-expedition leadership. Crews can be invited as self-organised teams with a faculty advisor letter.

### CSA Canadian Astronaut Selection (2017 campaign)

- **Source(s)**: https://www.asc-csa.gc.ca/eng/astronauts/how-to-become-an-astronaut/2017-recruitment-campaign.asp.
- **Selection stages**: Application → Public Service Entrance Exam + logic + preliminary medical → top 72 (aptitude weekend, Saint-Jean-sur-Richelieu) → top 32 (emergency-simulation week, Halifax) → top 17 (robotics, comms, full medical) → final interview → 2 selected.
- **Criterion families used**: medical, cognitive / logic, physical fitness, behavioural (judgment, resourcefulness, teamwork), emergency-response performance, communications & public-engagement, professional / academic, civil-service eligibility (Public Service Entrance Exam).
- **Instruments named**: **CSA fitness test** (later released as "First Steps: Training for Space" public protocol); medical battery — "**blood tests, respiratory tests, eye and dental exams, psychological evaluations, scans and MRIs**"; "Dexterity, logic and critical reasoning tests; Swimming, fitness and cardio tests."
- **Pass-rates**: 3,772 applicants → 72 → 32 → 17 → 2 (Kutryk, Gibbons).
- **Notable quirks**: Halifax Stage 2 — candidates "**solve problems, take part in emergency and rescue simulations, fight fires and contain floods**." Communications skills are explicitly assessed via media interviews, school visits, and social-media production. Active-duty CAF astronauts must also complete the annual CAF FORCE fitness evaluation.

### Roscosmos Cosmonaut Selection (2017–2018 open call)

- **Source(s)**: Quine T. *So, you want to become a cosmonaut? Inside the 2018 cosmonaut selection process.* The Space Review, 15 Oct 2018, https://thespacereview.com/article/3585/1.
- **Selection stages**: four. "Screening of initial applications" → "Full-time Stage" (2 weeks at Yuri Gagarin Cosmonaut Training Center, ≈ 20% advance) → Medical Commission → Selection Commission credentials review.
- **Criterion families used**: demographic (≤ 35 years, Russian citizen, ≥ higher education in exact/natural/aviation sciences), psychological, physical fitness, intellectual / language (Russian + English), operator skill, medical (extreme — "ideal health" required), professional review.
- **Instruments named**: "tests with **250 questions**, then immediately you have a logical test including some calculations, and after that the interview when you sit alone surrounded by several psychologists and superiors of the Training Center"; **drawing test** ("draw some unreal creature, as your personality is visible from your drawing"); **Rumberg [Romberg] test**, **trampoline 3-m fork-jump head-first entry**, **25 m breath-hold underwater swim**, hand-rider bike, treadmill; **operator's test** (constructing an electronic device or aiming a camera as on ISS); centrifuge and swivel-chair (vestibular).
- **Pass-rates**: 420 applicants → ≈ 80 invited to Full-time Stage (≈ 20%) → ≈ 16 to Medical Commission (≈ 20%) → 13 reached credentials commission → **8 selected** in August 2018.
- **Notable quirks**: Passing score threshold for advancement to Medical Commission was reported as **55 points** in 2017–18. Reported select-out causes included a **deviated nasal septum, enlarged tonsils, an "over-trained heart"** in an elite footballer, and "a high pulse" on the cycling test. Roscosmos's "ideal health" bar is notably higher than NASA's per candidate report.

## Methodological caveats

1. **Quote sourcing.** Phrases in bold or quotation marks are verbatim from the cited URL or paper. Any paraphrasing is explicit (no quotes).
2. **OEWF C-selection vs. D-MARS reporting.** The "chopsticks-and-rice" and pre-dawn freezing-run quirks are described in the D-MARS paper (Vizel-Parnas et al. 2020 §4.3) reporting on the **AMADEE-20 Innsbruck training week** (OEWF-run). They are attributed to the OEWF row in the matrix.
3. **JAXA quirk.** The folded-cranes / origami isolation-chamber task is widely cited in popular press; the peer-reviewed Inoue & Tachibana 2013 paper documents the 1-week confinement methodology without naming origami specifically. Treat the cranes claim as press-level, the confinement-week protocol as primary.
4. **Roscosmos.** Best English-language primary source is The Space Review's first-person interview with a 2018 candidate (Drozhzhova); medical and sport details should be re-verified against the official Roscosmos / GCTC handbook before publication.
5. **ASTRA paper body is paywalled.** Only the AIAA abstract was readable; A1 / A4 should obtain the full PDF via institutional access before any direct comparison with the proposed Selectron framework.
