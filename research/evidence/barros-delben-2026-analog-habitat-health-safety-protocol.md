## Abstract

As space exploration emerges as the next frontier for humanity, challenges related to health and safety in isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE) environments become increasingly relevant. This study examines the development of integrated monitoring protocols to establish a replicable standard for collecting and analyzing human factors indicators in both analogous and real space missions, adapting existing approaches from polar environments. A one-month analog mission was conducted with a six-member crew, during which protocols were designed for pre-, during-, and post-mission phases. The methodological approach combined Soft Systems Methodology (SSM) for modeling complex systems with a comparative analysis of ICE technologies developed for polar expeditions, particularly in Antarctica, alongside direct observations of existing systems. The resulting system adopts an interdisciplinary framework, integrating biological, physiological, medical, and psychological data. A key outcome of this study is the proposal for an automated adaptive response mechanism, enabling the creation of individualized profiles and standardized scores that evolve throughout the mission. The findings underscore the necessity of flexible

---

protocols for crews in prolonged isolation while maintaining standardized indicators for comparative analysis. These protocols can be adapted for two-week missions, broadening their applicability across various analog space environments. This research contributes to the development of a health and safety monitoring model aligned with the requirements of future manned missions to the Moon and Mars, as well as expeditions in remote and challenging environments on Earth. Future studies should assess the proposed model using the Delphi method.

## 1. Introduction

Space exploration represents the final frontier for humanity. In the 21st century, five decades after the first human footprint was left on the Moon as a historic milestone (Cromwell et al., 2021; Zivi et al., 2020) a renewed investment in space exploration is underway. This new space race is driven by governments and private companies aiming to establish a sustained human presence beyond Low Earth Orbit (LEO) - region of space between approximately $160\mathrm{km}$ and $2.000\mathrm{km}$ above Earth's surface -, including on the International Space Station (ISS) and in planned Lunar and Mars missions (Melamed et al., 2024). Building on the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) five hazards of human spaceflight (i.e. space radiation, isolation and confinement, distance from Earth, gravity fields, hostile/close environments), the main challenges of long duration human spaceflight involve developing mechanisms to better understand and predict emerging risks for astronauts health and crew dynamics, space infrastructures, and spacecraft design and habitability, while ensuring the necessary conditions for humans to thrive in space (Fisher et al., 2020).

The astronaut's health and safety guarantee against the adverse effects of microgravity, pressure variations, ionizing radiation, and isolation—among other factors—is a priority. Continuous monitoring and preventive measures are essential to mitigate undesirable events (Wadhwa et al., 2024), typically through standardized protocols and checklists. Monitoring astronaut health and safety indicators in space is inherently complex, costly, and constrained by a

---

significantly small sample size, limiting both research and intervention strategies (Cromwell et al. 2021; Wadhwa et al. 2024; Zivi et al. 2020). As a result, simulations in space-analog environments have emerged as logistically viable alternatives, playing an increasingly critical role in space exploration (Agaptseva et al. 2024; Heinicke & Arnhof, 2021).

Key areas of investigation in both space and space-analog environments related human factors include medical aspects such as emergency response, immunological changes, biological aging, bone and muscle performance, and pharmacological interventions (Diak et al. 2024; Muramatsu et al. 2025; Robertson et al. 2020). In the psychological domain, cognitive function, mood regulation, behavioral patterns (De la Torre et al. 2024), and stress responses—mediated by molecular markers such as cytokines and hormones—are of particular scientific interest (Feiveson et al. 2022), demonstrating different areas of knowledge cooperating.

Interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary collaborations, particularly in cross-cultural studies, attract researchers worldwide (Barros-Delben, 2025; Mendes et al. 2024), fostering novel approaches to space science, including investigations into post-mission biological aging (Campisi et al. 2024). These studies have grown significantly in the so-called space-analog environments.

Space-analog environments serve as controlled settings for simulating space activities (Alexander & Bannova, 2021). These environments - such as isolated research stations in polar regions, high-altitude deserts, underwater laboratories, and confined artificial habitats—replicate physical, psychological, and operational conditions similar to those encountered in space missions. They provide ideal platforms for testing technologies and methodological frameworks for future extraterrestrial applications (Bensch et al. 2024; Sauro et al. 2021) offering significant cost savings and overcoming logistical barriers (Agaptseva et al. 2024; Marques-Quinteiro et al. 2023), although they also present typical difficulties in navigating this environment.

One of Earth's natural space-analog environments is Antarctica, where similar studies on health and safety are conducted among individuals operating in extreme conditions (Barros-Delben & Cruz, 2023; Barros-Delben et al. 2019). In polar regions such as Antarctica and the Arctic, research focuses on the effects of isolation and confinement on groups, aiming to develop strategies to mitigate adverse health and safety outcomes while enhancing performance and adaptation (Walotek et al. 2024). Although artificial analogues offer greater control over variables and lower costs for their implementation, aspects such as urgency and a more realistic requirement

---

for planning and logistical execution and self-sufficiency, similar to what could be found in space, should be introduced.

Investing in astronaut health and safety reflects both social responsibility and strategic foresight. Maintaining crew well-being can reduce the likelihood of illness, human error and accidents. This inherently enhances mission success and crisis prevention, especially in high-cost space expeditions. The present study aims to adapt and refine replicable health and safety protocols (Barros-Delben et al. 2024) for space mission crews, drawing from existing experiences and proposed interventions in isolated, confined and extreme (ICE) environments. It deliberately excludes constraints encountered in space that are not reproducible in natural laboratories, such as low gravity, reduced pressure conditions and intense UV light incidence.

### Current Space Exploration

Space exploration is part of the roots of human history. It is at the forefront of the present, and it is an inevitable path for the future of our civilization. In direct comparison with the rising events of the Cold War, this modern competition is more a manifestation of technological prowess than a demonstration of superior ideology (Bains, 2022).

Investments by each nation or continent are growing and reaching billions, with estimates that they will exceed trillions by the middle of this century (Spector & Higham, 2025). Promising areas include space tourism, satellites, extraterrestrial infrastructure, conservation of the planets for science purpose, and the health and safety of those involved in the missions.

## 2.2. Main risks and impacts observed and predicted

Space exploration has undergone significant transformations since the first flight with humans in 1961. Following the Apollo program, which first landed two people on the Moon in 1969, space agencies shifted focus toward human presence beyond Earth. Establishing the ISS marked a new era, enabling long-duration missions and critical scientific research in microgravity (Antonsen et al., 2023). However, human spaceflight beyond low Earth orbit presents unprecedented challenges. A Mars mission, for example, is projected to last between two and three years—far exceeding the most prolonged continuous human stay in space, lasting 370 days, 21 hours and 22 minutes (Agar et al., 2024; Landon et al., 2018).

Long-duration spaceflight exposes astronauts to various physiological and psychological risks (e.g. harassment, loneliness and psychosis episodes), including microgravity-induced muscle and bone loss, immune system dysregulation, radiation exposure, and cognitive decline (Basner et al., 2014; Freeman, 2000). The increased distance from Earth introduces significant communication delays, requiring astronaut crews to operate more autonomously (Landon et al., 2018; Quélen, 2024). Additionally, behavioral and mental health concerns (i.e. isolation, confinement stress, and interpersonal conflicts) pose significant threats to both mission success and astronaut well-being (Kanas et al., 2001; Sandal et al., 2007).

---

As many authors (Antonsen et al. 2023; Krittanawong et al. 2023; Oluwafemi et al. 2021) emphasize that while advances in life-support systems and spacecraft design have mitigated some hazards, the human factor remains the most unpredictable element of deep-space missions. To mitigate risks, extensive research is being conducted in terrestrial analog habitats that replicate conditions of isolation and confinement similar to those encountered in space, facilitating the study of psychological and physiological adaptation (Barnett & Kring, 2003; Cromwell et al. 2021), although limitations of these artificial or natural environments are recognized.

### Habitats and their relevance for space exploration

### Summary of Space Analogs

On Earth, significant aspects related to human adaptation to space conditions can be studied in controlled, artificial, and / or natural environments: space analogues. Their importance lies in their ability to offer cost-effective, safer, logistically feasible, and highly controlled conditions for research that would be challenging or impossible to conduct in space. Additionally, some analogs provide conditions surpassing the ISS capabilities, such as the opportunity to simulate longer communication delays (e.g. Martian and Lunar delays) that closely resemble deep-space missions (Stuster, 2016). While certain conditions—such as radiation—cannot be fully recreated in existing analogue facilities, studies conducted in analog environments have provided valuable insights into cognitive, emotional, and physiological challenges comparable to those experienced in space (Paganini et al. 2023). This is why analogue studies are part of the current human and robotics exploration research strategy from national space agencies such as NASA and ESA, where standardized research protocols are an important source of information to further understand how to better prepare astronauts for long-duration space missions (De la Torre et al. 2024; Van Ombergen et al. 2022).

Space analogues fall into two main categories: physiological analogues and isolation and confinement analogues (Cromwell et al. 2021). Physiological analogues set the primer on the effects of artificially induced microgravity on the human body (including the nervous system), with emphasis on biological and medical studies. Two stark examples of such analogues are bed-rest studies using a head-down tilt to simulate the changes in fluid flow as in human spaceflight (e.g. see the BRACE study, a joint effort between the French and European space agencies), and parabolic flights (De La Torre et al. 2024).

---

Different from microgravity and space radiation, confinement and isolation are more reproducible characteristics of space habitats and infrastructures that have a direct effect on individuals and groups (De La Torre et al., 2024). Isolated, confined and extreme (as natural) analogue environments include Polar research stations, submarines, and underwater habitats. Likewise, ground-based and spaceflight isolated, confined, and controlled analogues are specialized facilities designed to simulate the conditions of the space environment under controlled conditions, enabling research on human performance, psychological well-being, crew dynamics, and technological solutions for future missions. Currently, there are over twenty-two operational habitats worldwide, which vary in size, crew capacity, location, and equipment⁸. Initially developed during the space race in the 1960s, they were primarily constructed by space agencies such as NASA and the Soviet Union (USSR). However, in recent years, private initiatives have increasingly contributed to their development (Mintus et al., 2022). Among the existing habitats, five permanent installations—FMARS, HI-SEAS, HMP, Mars Base 1, and MDSR—are located in geologically analogous environments to extraterrestrial surfaces, such as deserts or volcanic slopes (Heinicke &amp; Arnhof, 2021). Other habitats, like HERA at NASA's Johnson Space Center, the NEK habitat chamber in Moscow (which has hosted the MARS 105, Mars 500/520, SIRIUS 19, and SIRIUS 21 missions), and DLR's Envihab in Cologne are examples of existing analogue infrastructures that provide an isolated, confined, and controlled environment for the performing of human related studies focusing on short duration (e.g. HERA) and long duration (e.g. Envihab) missions. What is more, some analogue environments such as Poland's Lunares research base, and the LUNA facility at the European Astronaut Center include enclosed environments allowing controlled Extra-Vehicular Activity (EVA) simulations (De La Torre et al., 2024; Mintus et al., 2022).

## 2.4. Health and safety challenges in space missions and ICE environments

## 2.4.1. Psychosocial stressors

Human space exploration presents significant health risks to astronauts, causing physiological, cellular, and molecular changes that accelerate biological aging, impacting their well-being and the success of missions (Camera et al., 2024; Mason et al., 2024). Health risks include factors such as disruptions in circadian rhythm, sleep, mood, and hormonal balance, along with the exposure to cosmic radiation that can increase susceptibility to DNA damage, promoting

---

cellular mutations and long-term diseases (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020; McDonald et al. 2024), combined with increased stress from workload are common causes of concern. These aspects directly affect overall health, making continuous and comprehensive monitoring necessary (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020; Camera et al. 2024; Tomsia et al. 2024).

### Environmental conditions

In addition to psychosocial stressors, environmental conditions during space missions pose significant physiological challenges (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020). Isolation and confinement during spaceflights stand out for increasing psychological, behavioral, and physiological health risks (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020; Basner et al. 2014). Although they do not always represent immediate threats to the mission, these effects compromise health and individual and collective performance, affecting long-term objectives (Stuster, 2016). Studies in 520-day analog missions have confirmed these patterns (Basner et al. 2014). The reduced cabin pressure, for example, compared to standard Earth levels (Tomsia et al. 2024), can induce mild hypoxia, impairing cognition, sleep, and cardiovascular health, with cumulative effects similar to accelerated aging (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020; Colon, 1997; Ferrucci & Fabbri, 2018). These impacts add to the environmental risks typical of closed systems, such as air and water quality, which require constant monitoring (Madrigal et al. 2022).

Continuous air recirculation favors the accumulation of carbon dioxide (CO_{2}) and other toxic compounds (Camera et al. 2024). These factors are associated with headaches, fatigue, and cognitive impairment (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020). Altered pathogen dispersion in microgravity increases the risk of contamination and infections (Madrigal et al. 2022). Recycled water may contain pharmaceutical residues excreted by the crew, such as antibiotics, with impacts on inflammatory biomarkers (Camera et al. 2024). These biomarkers persist in purification systems, as demonstrated in studies conducted aboard the International Space Station (ISS), requiring new degradation methods and machine learning-based monitoring (Madrigal et al. 2022).

The limited diet and nutritional monotony also pose risks, potentially leading to subclinical malnutrition, sarcopenia, and mood changes (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020), with metabolic implications documented in long-duration missions (Tomsia et al. 2024). The influence of food on astronaut health is also critical and cannot be overlooked, especially considering that space nutrition differs markedly from that on Earth. Meals in space are pre-packaged, often dehydrated,

---

and limited in variety. These meal selections can compromise not only nutrient intake but also appetite, satiety, and psychological well-being. Dietary constraints can influence gut microbiota composition, immune system function, hormonal regulation, and metabolic balance. The combination of these cumulative effects can adversely harm an astronaut during a long-duration mission (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020; Tomsia et al. 2024).

In long-duration missions, astronauts are exposed to stressors like microgravity, cosmic radiation, and isolation. Stressors have a negative impact on the body that accelerates biological aging. This is characterized by molecular and cellular deterioration in comparison to chronological aging, which merely reflects the passage of time (Barros-Delben et al. 2021; Bisserier et al. 2022; Rivolier et al. 1991; Sabry et al. 2022). In space, this accelerated aging manifests as bone density loss, muscle mass reduction, increased inflammation, and changes in cardiovascular health, as well as on people's cognitive and mood effects (Camera et al. 2024). Monitoring health during space missions is therefore essential, especially for long-duration missions, such as future Mars exploration (McDonald et al. 2024; Romero & Francisco, 2020), in order to develop and evaluate effective countermeasures that support human adaptation (Tomsia et al. 2024).

However, strict astronaut selection criteria (Landon et al. 2018) and validated countermeasures, such as regular physical exercise, blue light exposure to adjust circadian rhythms, and emerging therapies with microRNA antagonists and AI-based interventions, have proven effective in mitigating these effects (Pagnini et al. 2023). Countermeasures promote the maintenance of physical and mental health in extreme environments. Long-duration missions also produce long-term physiological consequences—including accelerated biological aging, chronic inflammation, immune dysregulation, genomic instability, and metabolic disturbances—which extend beyond the immediate environmental stressors described above (Camera et al. 2024; Mason et al. 2024). Evidence from ICE environments indicates similar molecular and psychosocial pathways, reinforcing the importance of developing integrated monitoring frameworks capable of capturing multisystem interactions (Pagnini et al. 2023; Stuster, 2016). However, historical spaceflight data remain scarce and heterogeneous, highlighting a need for replicable, standardized, and longitudinally oriented health-monitoring systems.

### Technologies in health assessments

---

To ensure astronauts' health during missions, assessments need to be conducted. Through questionnaires, interviews, observations, tests, imaging exams, instruments, and wearable monitoring devices, one is able to assess the body's electrical activity (Mason et al. 2024) along with bone density, muscle strength, cardiovascular, pulmonary, psychological patterns and food intake (Camera et al. 2024; Tomsia et al. 2024). Additionally, biological samples (i.e. blood, saliva, and urine) provide crucial information about cytokine levels, epigenetic modifications, and oxidative stress, ensuring ongoing immunological, nutritional, and metabolic monitoring (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020; Colon, 1997; Sabry et al. 2022).

Technologies such as wearable devices, integrated with AI-driven analyses hold promise for early detection of health impairments, enabling rapid and precise interventions (Camera et al. 2024; Madrigal et al. 2022; Sabry et al. 2022). Such interventions depend largely on preparing astronauts for self-sufficiency, considering that they may remain for several months on a journey away from Earth's support. Limited support happens in some regions on Earth as well. For example, in the polar stations during the winter period in Antarctica, in certain areas. As result, most current studies are Earth-based in extreme environments like polar stations and submarines, they provide valuable insights that can be adapted for future space missions. In this context, simulations conducted in Antarctica have been instrumental in developing countermeasures and refining personalized health strategies for application in spaceflight (Bisserier et al. 2022; Rivolier et al. 1991).

Space exploration also poses significant challenges to astronauts' biosafety and bioprotection (Afshinnekoo et al. 2020) - respectively, focusing on the protection of the health of space mission participants, as well as the protection of the general public, even if not directly related to the activity, and the protection or preservation of the environment. The microgravity environment and prolonged confinement in spacecraft may favor the proliferation of resistant microorganisms, increasing the risk of infections and health complications (Bayot et al. 2025; Ubaniak et al. 2018). The implementation of biosafety protocols, coupled with the development of bioprotection strategies, is crucial to minimize risks to crew health and ensure the viability of prolonged missions (Urbaniak et al. 2018). The use of advanced monitoring systems for the early detection of biological agents and the creation of physical, chemical, and behavioral protective barriers will further minimize risks.

---

The integration of strategies with cutting-edge technologies, such as artificial intelligence and extreme environment simulations, not only improves mission performance but also has the potential to bring significant advances to human health on Earth, providing solutions applicable to longevity and well-being in adverse environments (Camera et al. 2024; Madrigal et al. 2022; Scarpa et al. 2023).

### Prevention and Countermeasures for Incidents in Long Duration Space Missions

As space missions extend beyond low Earth orbit, adopting personalized strategies to address astronaut health becomes critical (Wang et al. 2024). Right here on planet Earth, hard-to-reach areas already face challenges related to emergencies and the need for countermeasures for incidents, as well as prevention strategies. At certain locations in the poles, evacuation or access by specialists to provide assistance can be impossible during some seasons (Oertle & Liggett, 2025). For this reason, expedition members - like astronauts - need to be equipped with sufficient skills and knowledge to provide self-assistance when necessary, increasing their autonomy (Barros-Delben & Cruz, 2023). Remote support technologies, such as telehealth, become an ally, however, they may also be unfeasible due to environmental constraints and time--distance communication delays (Kathiravelu & Moxley, 2024), as the same in space, highlighting the long-duration missions.

Long-duration space missions present unique challenges for human life, especially in preventing incidents that could compromise the health of astronauts and the success of the mission (Krittanawong et al. 2022). Preventative measures, such as rigorous pre-flight and post-flight medical evaluations, psychological assessments, and physical and mental fitness programs, are important to ensure that astronauts are fully prepared for the harsh conditions of space and confinement and limited society (Krittanawong et al. 2022; Marques-Quinteiro et al. 2023). Countermeasures to mitigate the impact of these risks include the development of advanced technologies ranging from psychological/medical to infrastructure to protect astronauts from the diversity of space (Marques-Quinteiro et al. 2023). Customized protocols integrating artificial intelligence can also be used to monitor individual astronauts' responses to space conditions, enabling targeted interventions to address specific one-off concerns. These strategies contribute to the long-term success and safety of space missions, mainly through an inter and transdisciplinary approach, based on analysis of the requirements of the specific context. Methods that consider

---

human factors are increasingly used as alternatives, such as the safe behavior model for ICE environments (Barros-Delben, 2025), which offers low-cost mechanisms, requiring only the training of individuals and groups, and also includes raising people's awareness.

## 2.6. The current study

This study evaluated the effectiveness of an interdisciplinary approach to health indicator management designed for use in space simulation scenarios. The tool, originally developed for use at Antarctic research stations, was later adapted to the analog environment of the LunAres Habitat. The month-long mission at LunAres generated critical feedback for technological development, with the prototype being analyzed using the System Soft Methodology (SSM) framework (Checkland, 2000). The analysis incorporated both the expeditioners observations and suggestions regarding the proposed technology—based on a previous functional interface—and the professional and research expertise of the team as a whole. The main hypothesis was that an integrated system supporting interdisciplinary health management could increase the autonomy of analog astronauts, optimizing daily routines and facilitating indicator monitoring. Thus, the objective was to validate the tool with potential users, streamline operational processes, and reduce the time and cost of its technological improvements, which would be finalized in future simulations. Evaluation criteria included users' ability to understand and interact with the system within the mission-defined conditions, and the potential for reducing time spent on routine simulation tasks.

## 3. Material and methods

## 3.2. Sample characteristics

This study was conducted during a one-month space-analog mission involving a six-person crew representing diverse scientific disciplines and cultural backgrounds from the United States, India, Poland, Canada, Brazil, and Slovenia. It is important to highlight that the sample size of the current study is small. Although large-sample studies are often considered methodologically stronger, the disregard of small samples can lead to overlook many teams and team-level phenomena in relevant and specific work contexts (Maynard et al., 2025). Small-sample research has generated some of the most influential insights in human space flight research—for example,

---

Atlis et al. (2004) research about decision making processes during a two-women antarctic transverse, and Landon et al. (2025) recent findings on the importance of space farming for astronauts performance and mental health.

Furthermore, teams operating in analogue environments such as Lunares are also rare populations (Christman, 2009), where small Ns are unavoidable and can still accurately represent the broader population. Such studies have revealed unique dynamics—such as reduced interaction processes during the third quarter of Antarctic winter-overs, salutogenic patterns, and shifts in ingroup–outgroup relations—that would be difficult to capture otherwise (Pagnini et al., 2023). Given these constraints, small-sample research often benefits from qualitative methods that enable fine grained comparisons across multiple individuals and groups.

## 3.3. Research Design and Context

The research followed and extended a protocol originally developed by Barros-Delben (2025) across seven space-analog missions, which was used here to enable an in-depth examination of the experiences of potential system users and managers. Observations, comparative analyses, and protocol reviews were performed to evaluate how this program could be integrated and adapted to meet the operational and human-factor requirements of human spaceflight.

The research was supported by an interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary international team, which had previously collaborated to consolidate various protocols into a unified framework, considering their professional experiences. This integrated protocol, covering pre-, during-, and post-mission phases, was designed to evaluate a broad range of human factor indicators. The technology was presented at the LunAres Habitat in Poland, a facility designed for lunar (Lun) and Martian (Ares) mission simulations (Heinicke &amp; Arnhof, 2021; Mintus et al., 2022), as part of the research tasks assigned to one of the study authors during a one-month simulation. The project's scope was expanded to include additional researchers, incorporating mechanisms for replication, standardization, and improved data collection, storage, and analysis, depending on interest in indicators and previous successful experience. One of the paper's authors fully tested the system, simulating its daily use under space-analog conditions during the LunAres mission, improving access to tools through the prototyped conceptual instrument.

---

## 3.4. Development Methodology

The methodological framework was based on SSM. The SSM is a qualitative systemic approach aimed at solving complex and poorly structured problems, being particularly effective in the development of technologies, such as protocols and systems (Checkland, 2000). Additionally, the SSM integrates multiple human perspectives in the design of adaptive solutions (i.e. professional and research experiences, individual experiences in Antarctica, and individual experiences in spaceflight analogs). SSM guarantees a mechanism to develop an integrated Health and Safety Management Program (PROGSS), a set of protocols that had been initially designed for polar expeditions (Barros-Delben, 2025) and were adapted to the context of a space analog habitat. The study considered both the existing simulation systems and the demands identified by participants, researchers, and mission control.

The proposal developed here consists of an adapted version of PROGSS, initially designed for the Antarctic environment as part of a doctoral research (Barros-Delben, 2023), based on 7 expeditions to Antarctica over 7 years and contributions from experts in the field, combining existing protocols and system tests, now applied to an analog space simulation for its standard replicability structure. The system was implemented and refined during the execution of the one-month mission in an analog habitat, and its formulation occurred in parallel with the practical experience in the simulation scenario - a confined environment hosting six volunteer participants under controlled restrictions of sugar, caffeine, and sunlight exposure, with limited social interaction and external support, funded by the University of Zurich. The simulation reproduced operational and psychological conditions of isolation and confinement analogous to those experienced in space missions and Antarctic stations, aiming to observe behavioral, physiological, and procedural responses under prolonged stress and deprivation.

Subsequently, the project was combined with protocols from partners and collaborators in the areas of medicine, biology, human physiology and medical engineering. Based on the principles of SSM, the adaptation of PROGSS was structured in four main phases, aligned with Checkland's methodological steps (Checkland, 2000):

A. Problem Exploration (Rich Picture and Diagnosis)

An assessment of existing resources was carried out, with a survey of available protocols specific to analog spaceflight missions, direct observations in the LunAres habitat, and interviews

---

with participants and support staff. This diagnosis sought to understand the critical elements of the simulated experience, both operational and subjective.

## B. Definition of Relevant Systems

The data collected in stage A were systematized and integrated into the original PROGSS structure, respecting the mapping of typical risks in polar environments and adapting them to the spatial context - simulation in analog spaceflight. The coexistence of different areas of health and safety (psychological, medical, physiological and operational) was considered. In this context, the relevant systems included, for example: (a) the psychological support system, focused on stress management, motivation, cognition, and interpersonal dynamics under confinement; (b) the medical monitoring system, responsible for tracking vital signs, sleep, and nutrition; (c) the physiological adaptation system, addressing the effects of restricted sunlight, diet, and circadian rhythm disruption; and (d) the operational safety system, ensuring task performance, communication routines, and emergency procedures.

These examples were defined based on the expertise of the multidisciplinary team involved in the simulation and on existing health and safety protocols commonly applied in polar and analog spaceflight contexts, as part of the SSM proposal. The interaction among these subsystems provided the basis for PROGSS adaptation and validation during the mission.

## C. Conceptual Modelling and Comparison with Reality

The analysis and adaptation of existing subprotocols were carried out, with refinement of instruments and schedules to ensure compatibility with the actual flow of the mission. This stage aimed to avoid data fragmentation and improve the fluidity of collection, reducing the burden on participants who would access PROGSS.

## D. Implementation and Actions to Improve the System

Finally, the combined protocol was formalized, with the consolidation of an integrated preparation and monitoring system.

In-depth interviews were conducted in a non-structured, convenience-based format, allowing flexibility to explore participants' perceptions, suggestions, and criticisms regarding the proposed integrative technology for managing health and safety indicators. Although no questionnaire was applied, the interactions followed a pre-defined thematic focus, each topic illustrated by an example question:

---

1. Usability – “How would you assess the tool’s ease of use in the mission context, especially compared with currently available mechanisms?”
2. Operational efficiency – “To what extent would the system help reduce the time spent collecting and recording indicators?”
3. Individual progress monitoring – “Do you consider the alerts and tracking features provided by the system relevant to your daily performance?”
4. Data integration – “Does the system successfully consolidate the necessary information in a clear and accessible way?”

Responses were recorded in field notes, emphasizing functionality, applicability, and user acceptance. Given the small sample size (five participants) and the exploratory nature of the study, no statistical analysis or formal thematic categorization was performed; feedback was synthesized descriptively to capture practical considerations and direct user impressions.

The system was also evaluated outside the LunAres mission by 16 domain experts through similar in-depth interviews, providing complementary perspectives on potential applications and areas for refinement, as long as the doctoral thesis phase (Barros-Delben, 2023), which also involved seven additional experts, including co-authors and specialists who contributed their observations during study group discussions of the proposal. In accordance with the SSM, these contributions were not based on standardized quantitative instruments, but rather on reflective and iterative analyses of professional and field experiences. This approach emphasizes learning and conceptual modeling rather than statistical inference, generating representative insights that guided the system's adaptation and validation process. This methodological choice reflects an exploratory, mixed-design approach aimed at gathering rich, context-specific insights from a small, specialized participant group under operational constraints, prioritizing descriptive, experience-based feedback to guide future iterations of the technology.

In parallel with the SSM-based system design, PROGSS incorporates an analytical decision-support layer aimed at transforming multidimensional health and behavioral data into actionable risk assessments. This layer integrates a range of computational methods, including decision trees for classifying risk states, logistic regression for estimating probabilities of adverse events, K-means clustering for identifying similar psychophysiological profiles, and artificial neural networks for detecting non-linear patterns across heterogeneous data sources.

---

Input variables are derived from both self-report instruments (digital questionnaires, psychometric scales, and interviews) and physiological and medical signals, such as heart rate, electrodermal activity, biochemical markers, and eye-tracking metrics collected through wearable sensors and cameras. These data streams are continuously enriched by critical incident reports and field observations, forming a cooperative and evolving database that supports iterative model refinement.

In line with the principles of Soft Systems Methodology, model validation follows an evolutionary and safety-oriented approach, prioritizing human well-being and operational integrity over computational efficiency or predictive aggressiveness, particularly in the generation of prescriptive outputs for emergency and risk management scenarios.

## 4. Results

The main result of this study is the adaptation of a development of a health and safety support system in extreme contexts (PROGSS), originally built for ICE environments. The analytical functioning of the PROGSS system—conceived as a continuously evolving system rather than a closed solution—is schematically presented in Figure 1. The system was designed to enable progressive interdisciplinary action, consistent with the complexity inherent to extreme environments and emergency scenarios. The figure details the input, processing, and output layers, as well as the types of data mobilized at each stage or phase, highlighting the adaptive logic that sustains the system throughout the operational cycle and its feedback loops. This architecture explicitly accounts for the limitations associated with small datasets, which are common in isolated environments, and for their potential analytical implications. The use of AI techniques is implemented in a responsible and incremental manner, emphasizing transparency, continuous human oversight, and the explicit acknowledgment of the system's operational limits. Machine learning models and rule-based engines are employed in a complementary fashion for clustering, risk stratification, and trend prediction, avoiding automated decision-making while providing assertive decision support rather than substituting specialized human judgment and interdisciplinary health and safety assessment.

It is structured based on an interdisciplinary and transdisciplinary architecture that is aimed at the integration, analysis, and automated response to multiple human and environmental data in polar missions and analog spaceflight simulations. The implementation of PROGSS during the analog mission resulted in an integrated operational platform composed of two functional steps and five mission phases, applicable to both polar and simulated space environments. In Step A, a contextualized integration platform was established, allowing interdisciplinary protocols (psychological, physiological, biological, and medical) to be prepared, standardized, and deployed across analog and digital devices. This enabled continuous data collection and storage even under limited communication conditions. Step B operationalized this platform across five temporal phases: pre-mission, early mission, mid-mission and critical events, end-of-mission, and post-mission. Baseline profiles were generated before exposure to environmental stressors, followed by continuous individual and group monitoring throughout the mission and structured follow-ups at one and six months post-mission. As a result, PROGSS enabled the systematic collection, storage, and analysis of behavioral, physiological, and psychological indicators, and the delivery of structured feedback to participants, researchers, and operational staff, supporting mission replicability and comparative analysis across analog environments.

The system was designed to collect data only once per indicator, eliminating redundancies between areas and favoring the interoperability of information between technical and scientific teams. For example, it is not necessary to repeatedly collect basic information across psychological, medical, or physiological studies. Each indicator - such as motivation level, sleep quality, or sociodemographic data, as sex, age, etc. - is collected once and then integrated into the shared system, allowing specialists from different fields to independently cross-analyze the information. In stages A and B, the system allows significant gains in terms of clarity in longitudinal monitoring and fluidity in the mission schedule, reducing tasks for scientific studies.

The viability of the system was validated through the execution of the simulation by the developer herself, presented to participants to express their perception for improvement activities, as planned by the SSM, with field observations and practical application of the protocols on a specific route, designed in Google Sheets as a prototype, including the schedule in a daily progress status for mission participants and supervisors. The results indicate that the model is applicable both in space simulations and in polar expeditions. The model was tested as a decision-support

---

system designed to anticipate potential critical events, support operational and clinical decision-making, and contribute to crew well-being through early warning and adaptive feedback mechanisms.. The system also provides mechanisms to check the daily schedule of activities and tasks, with a progress bar. Layered data collection and storage structure facilitate efficient data sharing, the addition of the use of hybrid digital and analog instruments, including offline/online tools, wearables (e.g., smartwatches, eye-tracking devices, movement sensors), and physiological sensors (e.g., temperature, perspiration) can enhance data accuracy and minimize respondent burden.

During the analog mission, the analytical layer of PROGSS was used to integrate physiological signals (e.g., heart rate and sleep quality) with self-reported emotional and behavioral data, allowing the system to generate dynamic risk profiles for individual crew members. These profiles made it possible to detect emerging patterns of fatigue, stress, and reduced performance over time. Based on these combined indicators, the system produced rule-based adaptive suggestions, such as recommendations to adjust workload, introduce additional rest periods, or activate targeted psychological support routines. Although implemented in a simplified prototype format, these automated outputs demonstrated how the integrated data streams could be translated into actionable decision-support information during mission operations. In addition, the aggregation of individual and group-level data allowed the system to capture collective behavioral trends, supporting the identification of periods of increased psychosocial risk associated with confinement and workload dynamics in the analog habitat. # 5. Discussion

The present study suggests that an integrated monitoring system such as PROGSS can help streamline operational processes, reduce the time required for daily health- and safety-related tasks, and enhance crew autonomy during analog missions. User feedback indicated that consolidating psychological, physiological, medical, and operational indicators into a unified platform improved clarity and reduced redundancy. These findings highlight the practical benefits of interdisciplinary system design in ICE environments and the potential for scaling such frameworks to future spaceflight contexts.

Protocols that enable continuous and integrated monitoring of human factors, such as the one proposed in this study, can support enhanced safety and strategic risk management mechanisms in remote and extreme environments. These include difficult-to-access regions, such as polar stations, simulated spaceflight habitats analog, and underground environments (Barros-Delben, 2025; Sauro et al., 2021), although they are also suitable in more routine contexts.

By systematically mapping and updating key indicators, these protocols contribute to greater crew autonomy and self-sufficiency, which are essential for the success of missions where external assistance may be delayed or even unavailable (Robertson et al., 2020). The protocol's

---

preventive characteristics are particularly relevant for mitigating undesirable events (Wadhwa et al. 2024). In this study, the system was used to generate calculation-based options to support responses to anticipated incidents. . This was observed to reduce the perceived difficulty of decision-making for the countermeasure and optimizing response time.

An integrated approach that consolidates diverse scientific and technical methodologies can support the standardization of safety measures and the reduction of mission-related risks. Such a systematic and holistic framework may also foster innovations applicable to terrestrial contexts, such as hospitals and remote sites (Palacios et al. 2021). Despite these advances, the study has clear limitations. The small sample size limits statistical power and generalizability, a common constraint in polar, space, and analog research (Zivi et al. 2020). This limitation can be partially addressed through the standardization and repetition of data collection across different missions and crews. Operational costs also restrict long-term and large-scale investigations in space analog environments. Complementary approaches, such as virtual and augmented reality simulations, may help mitigate these constraints (Bensch et al. 2024). Given these limitations, it is also worth considering the use of machine learning approaches under small-sample conditions (Vabalas et al. 2019). In this context, the learning component of the system was designed to operate under data-limited conditions, using transfer learning and synthetic data generation to support initial model calibration.

Natural ICE environments, such as Antarctica and caves, present both unique opportunities and challenges for space analog research (Sauro et al. 2021). Antarctica, for instance, has been instrumental in identifying immunological changes among long-term station residents, which resemble those observed in space crews. This highlights the potential of polar environments as space analogs to validate proposals (Diak et al. 2024). Moreover, these environments provide controlled settings for testing countermeasures to safeguard human health during space missions.

Nevertheless, factors such as prolonged isolation, limited privacy, social deprivation, and constrained resupply logistics introduce additional complexities (Barros-Delben, 2025). Understanding how these variables impact human adaptation is crucial for refining strategies that will be transposed to future space missions. Proposed countermeasures—such as pharmacological interventions to mitigate immune system dysregulation—are currently under investigation (Muramatsu et al. 2025), and further research should assess how these and other interventions could be effectively integrated within long-duration spaceflight protocols to enhance astronaut

---

resilience, and minimizing the aging impact of the space elements exposure. It is worth remembering that despite the advantages of various types of analog environments, they will never fully reflect the conditions faced by astronauts during space journeys. Real-space cabin conditions, including factors such as cabin noise, vibration, microgravity effects, and radiation exposure, are crucial for crew performance and health. However, they are not present in analog studies. In the future, the framework can even better reflect the conditions to which individuals and teams will adapt in future space missions, such as taking into account restrictions due to Planetary Protection and Environmental Protection requirements, such as prohibitions on material or gas release and the mandate for re-use (Coustenis et al. 2023; Galli & Losch, 2019). However, following van Omberg's et al. guidelines (Van Ombergen et al. 2022), the most important thing is a reliable description of the conditions that prevail during exploration in a given analog environment.

The system developed in this study, for subsequent implementation and improvement testing, offers a practical response to several challenges faced in health and safety management in isolated, confined, and extreme environments (ICE). The main result was the creation of an integrative protocol that consolidates interdisciplinary approaches into a unified operational framework, compatible with critical subsystems such as the Life Support System (LSS), and based on both the literature and practical experience in space-based analog habitats (e.g. HERA, envihab) (Barros-Delben & Cruz, 2023; Marques-Quinteiro et al. 2023; Walotek et al. 2024).

The implementation of the integrated monitoring framework in the LunAres mission demonstrated the feasibility of generating real-time, multidimensional situational awareness, suggesting its potential to support decision-making in long-duration missions. The proposed protocol was perceived by users as contributing to participant autonomy, operational efficiency, and reduced cognitive load in routine monitoring tasks. The main focus of the analysis was on the system's functionality and applicability from the perspective of its potential users in real simulations, highlighting the protocol's ability to optimize repetitive tasks, reduce redundancies, and improve time management in simulation environments. The proposal shows promising replication potential in other missions, particularly its ability to circumvent the limitation of small sample sizes—typical of ICE studies—by standardizing procedures, allowing for the overlapping of data from different groups and strengthening statistical analyses. The interdisciplinary structure of the framework facilitated data triangulation across psychological, physiological, and operational indicators in the LunAres mission, improving the consistency of cross-domain comparisons.

---

Among the crucial elements of the developed technology, the following stand out briefly:
(1) Task automation and the centralization of multiple protocols in a single digital system;
(2) Methodological standardization that expands the statistical potential of studies with small samples;
(3) Promoting self-sufficiency in remote contexts through a system that facilitates integrated and continuous monitoring of health and human performance.

## 6. Standardization, replicability, and future validation plan

As a strategy for progressive standardization, the definition of shared minimum evaluation criteria is proposed to ensure methodological replicability and comparability across studies, without compromising sensitivity to local contexts or the autonomy of individual investigations. This approach enables both cross-sectional and longitudinal analyses across different groups and environments, gradually expanding the model’s potential for generalization while preserving the complexity of the extreme scenarios under assessment.

The continuous validation and refinement plan for PROGSS involves its systematic application over the next five years across different environments. To date, three strategic contexts have been addressed:
(i) the Mars Habitat located in Rio Grande do Norte (Brazil), focused on prolonged confinement simulations, in addition to other habitats interested in replicating the protocols;
(ii) high-altitude mountain expeditions (above 5,000 meters), conducted in collaboration with experienced mountaineers;
and (iii) polar missions in Antarctica and the Arctic, as well as forest environments, with emphasis on the Amazon region. These contexts allow for testing the robustness, adaptability, and sensitivity of the system under diverse environmental, operational, and psychosocial stressors.

Microgravity scenarios represent an important aggregating field for future research, and the project remains open to collaborative initiatives with researchers operating in such contexts. This cooperative strategy aims to enable more robust quantitative studies through the progressive accumulation and consolidation of data—both longitudinally within the same group and comparatively across different groups in comparable environments, with appropriate contextual weighting. These developments contribute to mitigating inherent limitations of the employed algorithms, particularly with respect to sample size, potential biases, generalizability, statistical robustness, and the ongoing need for continuous validation.

# 7. Conclusions

This research aims to contribute to the development of a comprehensive health and safety monitoring model, aligned with the requirements of future manned missions to the Moon and Mars, as well as expeditions in difficult-to-access environments on Earth. By employing SSM as both a methodological framework and an organizational learning tool, this study facilitated the iterative construction and validation of the final model. The addition of the Delphi method, a structured consultation technique that uses multiple rounds of questionnaires with experts to reach consensus on complex topics, for protocol evaluation will further strengthen this process by incorporating expert input and a participatory approach controlled.

The enhanced health and safety protocols designed for spaceflight habitats analog represent a significant advancement in monitoring strategies for analog space missions, addressing growing demands for interdisciplinary and holistic models. The integration of biological, medical, physiological, psychological, and ergonomic indicators underscores the necessity of multifaceted approaches to ensure crew well-being and operational efficiency in ICE environments. Importantly, the protocol suggests dual applicability, functioning effectively both in spaceflight contexts and in terrestrial extreme settings such as Antarctic stations. Furthermore, a prototype automated adaptive response framework, that was used to generate dynamic profiles and scores, which demonstrated its scalability and replicability potential during the analog mission.

Comparisons with historical Antarctic expeditions highlight the potential for inter-environmental learning, suggesting that space-derived technologies and protocols could have direct applications for polar research and other extreme terrestrial operations. This bidirectional exchange of knowledge reinforces the broader implications of this research beyond space exploration.

Moving forward, further validation is necessary through testing in diverse analog habitats, including controlled training simulations for space and extreme environment expeditions. The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) platforms could enhance predictive analytics and real-time decision-making, although such developments must consider organizational and ethical constraints.

The present study follows ethical and regulatory principles aligned with the Brazilian General Data Protection Law (LGPD), in accordance with international guidelines widely adopted in analogous contexts, including operational references used by the European Space Agency (ESA)

---

and the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA). The ethical approach prioritizes risk minimization for participants, clarity of research objectives, and transparency regarding the use, processing, and purpose of the collected data.

With regard to participant confidentiality and information security, procedures include informed consent, anonymization through encrypted identifiers, and continuous support for participants to clarify doubts and ensure responsible data management. Data confidentiality is a fundamental requirement of the system, with disclosure permitted only in situations involving significant risk to the participant or to third parties, in accordance with widely accepted ethical principles. Information security is structured through multiple layers of protection, with explicit and transparent recognition that no system is entirely immune to failures or breaches, although all recommended best practices in data security and data governance have been incorporated into the proposed framework.

A critical next step involves testing the proposed protocols under simulated microgravity conditions and under real spaceflight habitat hardware and conditions to ensure that the findings from this study are applicable to real spaceflight scenarios. By addressing these next steps, this research can serve as a cornerstone for the continuous improvement of health and safety monitoring in space missions, ultimately supporting the success and sustainability of future human exploration beyond Earth.
