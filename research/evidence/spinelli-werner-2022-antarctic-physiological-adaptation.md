## Abstract

The Antarctic environment induces adaptive metabolic and neuroendocrine changes associated with survival, as well as increased risks to physical and mental health. Circadian disruption has been observed in Antarctic expeditioners. The main consequences appear in quality of sleep, which can affect physical and cognitive performance. Physiological adaptation to cold is mediated by the norepinephrine and thyroid hormones (T₃ and 3,5-T₂ metabolite). The observed changes in the hypothalamic-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis of expeditioners varied according to temperature, photoperiod, time spent in the cold environment and stress level. The decrease in T₃ levels has frequently been associated with mood swings. Psychological and physical stressors cause disturbances in the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, with consequent maintenance of high cortisol levels, leading to memory impairment, immunosuppression, and cardiometabolic and reproductive disorders. Preventive measures, such as provision of adequate food, well-established eating times, physical activity and even the use of phototherapy, can all help maintain the circadian rhythm. In addition, the use of high-tech clothing and room temperature control in research stations provide greater protection against the effects of intense cold. However, psychological stress requires a more individualized approach based on the crew's sociocultural characteristics, but it can be mitigated by mental healthcare and training in coping strategies.

# 1 | INTRODUCTION

Antarctica still poses challenges for human occupation, despite its magnificently beautiful landscapes. Since 1958, most research in Antarctica has focused on geoscience, ecology, physics, and astronomy. Medical research has also progressed, although to a lesser extent. Olson (2002) conducted a review of medical research in Antarctica, indicating that



---

the areas under study reflect the impact of extreme conditions on explorers' health and addressing such aspects as physiology at low temperatures, effects of ultraviolet light, endocrine changes, alterations in immune function, circadian changes, sleep disturbances, and mood swings. Most of these changes, while adaptive to the extreme conditions of Antarctica, can have implications for human health, especially in more extreme conditions and during longer periods.

Besides these biological aspects, psychological issues should be considered, as they can affect the neuroendocrine system. The need for shared lodging and forced cohabitation eliminates privacy and can generate interpersonal tensions. Basic affective needs can become difficult to resolve. The impossibility of rescue during the winter and other logistic issues can increase the team's concerns and tensions. According to Palinkas and Suedfeld (2008), the way expeditionary team members are affected by these conditions depends largely on the meanings they attribute to each of them, so that camping in Antarctica during the summer can be either fun or disturbing. However, for military personnel assigned to research stations, sometimes with long periods of isolation, the overarching meaning is one of fulfillment of duty, which does not rule out psychological impact with physical and mental consequences (Wang et al. 2020).

The purpose of this integrative review is to understand how the Antarctic environment leads to adaptive metabolic and neuroendocrine changes essential for the survival of expeditionary team members, but that may represent susceptibility or factors contributing to the appearance of pathological changes, with both physical and psychological influences. The review also focuses on ways that monitoring biomarkers for stress and circadian rhythm disturbances has helped demonstrate the effectiveness of countermeasures to minimize the adverse effects of the Antarctic environment on crew members' health.

## CIRCADIAN ALTERATIONS

Alterations in circadian rhythm have been studied in Antarctic crews, especially in individuals that stay on the continent over the winter and undergo significant changes in duration of the light/dark cycle. These changes have also been associated with alternating work shifts (Koshy et al. 2019), frequent changes in time zones from long airplane trips, leading to jet lag (Vosko et al. 2010), or current social jet lag caused by modern lifestyle in large cities (Mota et al. 2021).

The circadian biological clock participates in regulation of sleep/waking periods, eating behavior, hormonal release, blood pressure, and body temperature, among others, indicating its importance for human health (Reinke & Asher, 2019). According to Cha et al. (2019), circadian rhythm dysregulation has been associated with the development of several neuropsychiatric disorders and neurodegeneration. Circadian misalignment leads to sleep problems, hormonal imbalance, inflammatory processes, impaired glucose metabolism, and cell cycle dysregulation. These outcomes are associated with various clinical conditions, including obesity, metabolic syndrome, type 2 diabetes, intestinal dysfunction, impaired immune function, cardiovascular disease, excessive daytime sleepiness, social and mood disorders, and increased risk of cancer (Baltatu et al. 2017; Cardinali & Vigo, 2017; James et al. 2017; Proietti et al. 2017; Reinke & Asher, 2019).

The discovery of the molecular mechanism that controls circadian rhythm gave Jeffrey Hall, Michael Rosbash, and Michael Young the Nobel Prize in 2017. Cells transcribe genes termed clock genes. According to Deng and Yang (2019), clock genes regulate 43% of the genes that encode proteins. The CLOCK gene codes the CLOCK transcription factor. Cells constantly transcribe this gene, maintaining cellular levels of CLOCK relatively constant. Another clock gene, transcribed at regular intervals, is called BMAL1 (brain and muscle Arnt‐like protein 1) generating cycles of its respective protein. When CLOCK and BMAL1 proteins meet, they form a heterodimer. Once formed, this heterodimer interacts with DNA and can activate the transcription of hundreds of other genes. Within hours, their respective proteins begin to be produced and accumulate throughout the day. These hundreds of genes are termed clock‐controlled genes (CCGs). In the suprachiasmatic nucleus of the hypothalamus, the NPAS2 protein (neuronal PAS domain protein 2) replaces the CLOCK protein in its functions. Some genes among the CCGs participate in metabolic regulation. Thus, changes in the transcription rhythm of these genes may lead to metabolic changes. The pace of CCG transcription is maintained by a negative feedback control mechanism (Gnocchi et al. 2015; Koshy et al. 2019; Vosko et al. 2010). The feedback loop is performed by some clock genes and respective proteins, such as the PER (period) and CRY (cryptochrome) proteins, which are produced and exported to the cytoplasm, where they combine, forming a heterodimer that returns to the nucleus at dusk and binds to the CLOCK‐BMAL1 complex, inhibiting it. This inhibition disrupts its own transcription and that of hundreds of other CCG genes. During the night, PER and CRY are gradually phosphorylated and degraded. Another negative feedback loop is performed by the REV‐ERB (reverse‐erythroblastosis virus receptor) and ROR (retinoid‐related orphan receptor) proteins, which exert opposite actions on BMAL1 transcription, inhibiting and stimulating it, respectively, thus checking the rhythm of their production, the cycles. The rhythm synchronization system (reset system, zeitgeber) comprises two main components, one central and one peripheral. The central clock is the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus, considered the biological rhythmic pacemaker. Its synchronization with the Earth's rotation is performed in two ways, through the photic path (retinal stimulation by light, leading to stimulus of the SCN) and the nonphotic path (hormones and neurotransmitters associated with eating, behavior, and social contact). The synchronization of peripheral clocks also occurs through the photic path, through both neural and hormonal signals emanating from the SCN, and through the nonphotic path, through feeding (ghrelin and leptin levels, for example) and body temperature (Gnocchi et al., 2015).

Studies on jet lag syndrome and shift work showed that the central clock adapts faster than the peripheral clocks. Difficulty sleeping, excessive daytime sleepiness, general malaise, impaired performance, and gastrointestinal upset are temporary symptoms associated with circadian disruption. In general, individuals take 1 week to adapt to a new work shift or new meridian. Individuals can behaviorally accelerate realignment to the environment through appropriately timed feeding and exercise (Vosko et al., 2010).

# 2.1 Biomarkers of circadian rhythm disruption

The biomarker most frequently applied to diagnosis of central circadian rhythm disorders is urinary melatonin (6-sulfatoxymelatonin, the major metabolite), while salivary cortisol is the most widely used to diagnose peripheral circadian rhythm disorders.

# 2.1.1 | Melatonin

Melatonin production occurs in the pineal gland and begins to increase at sunset, reaching a peak throughout the night and decaying at dawn. Many different types of receptors have been found in the pineal gland, demonstrating that it is controlled by several neurotransmitters released by fibers from different nervous system regions, from both photic and nonphotic pathways. The time of salivary melatonin onset was shown to exhibit low intraindividual variability and was significantly correlated with plasma onset. Figure 2 shows the photic pathway in the control of melatonin production. The pineal gland produces various substances during the day, including serotonin, which is converted to melatonin during the night. This conversion is performed by enzymes whose synthesis is activated by norepinephrine when the latter stimulates pineal  $\beta$ -adrenergic receptors. This norepinephrine originates from postganglionic fibers of the cervical ganglion. This is an important control route, in that  $\beta$ -blocking agents decrease melatonin levels (Simonneaux &amp; Ribelayga, 2003).

Melatonin plays several roles in the body at night. This hormone's importance can be better understood by verifying the distribution of its membrane receptors MT1, MT2, its nuclear receptor RZR/ROR $\alpha$ , and the MT3 receptor, which comprises enzyme quinone reductase 2, present in almost all tissues (Barrenetxe et al., 2004; Emet et al., 2016; Tosini et al., 2014). Melatonin performs feedback control on the synthesis of these receptors. In addition to actions mediated by receptors, melatonin also exhibits antioxidant action, as it reacts with free radicals and reactive oxygen species and promotes an increase in glutathione levels. Melatonin also interacts with calmodulin in the cytosol, modulating its physiological actions. Melatonin levels in the mitochondria are higher than in plasma, corroborating its importance in the antioxidant mechanism (Baltatu et al., 2017; Cardinali &amp; Vigo, 2017; Mayo et al., 2017; Proietti et al., 2017; Tan et al., 2015). Delaying or advancing the production of melatonin or increasing or decreasing the amount of produced melatonin both have consequences for normal body physiology. The biotransformation of melatonin by cytochrome P-450 with subsequent conjugation leads to production of the urinary metabolite 6-OH-melatonin sulfate (6-sulfatoxymelatonin or 6sMT). This metabolite's urinary concentration correlates with the amount of melatonin present in the blood in each period and can be used to assess clinical conditions related to disturbances in the rhythm or amplitude of melatonin production (Saksvik-Lehouillier et al., 2015).

Cortisol is the most robust peripheral synchronizer. Cortisol production exhibits a circadian rhythm, with maximum values in the morning (peak 6:00--10:00 AM) and minimum around midnight (10:00 p.m. to 2:00 a.m.). In addition, oscillations formed by pulses every 45 to 60 min are also observed. When cortisol binds to its intracellular receptor, it undergoes dimerization and migrates to the cell nucleus, where it interacts with the promoter zone of clock genes such as PER, printing the transcription rhythm of several CCGs. Cortisol regulates the expression of 3--10% of the human genome (Nicolaides et al. 2000). As examples, activation occurs for metabolism‐regulating proteins and anti‐inflammatory proteins, whereas repression occurs for pro‐inflammatory cytokines (Duque & Munhoz, 2016; Stahn & Buttgereit, 2008).

Clock‐controlled genes are also associated with glucocorticoid receptor (GR) acetylation, decreasing its activity. When acetylation is maximum in the morning, the tissue is less responsive to high cortisol levels that occur during this period, with the opposite happening at dusk. The period of greatest receptor activity occurs at night (10:00 p.m. to 2:00 a.m.), when the receptor is more responsive, or during stress, when cortisol remains elevated. Synchronization between the activity of tissue glucocorticoid receptor and peak serum cortisol is highly regulated. Disturbances in this synchronism can contribute to several pathologies (Dickmeis, 2009; Nicolaides et al. 2000).

Morning cortisol participates in mobilizing energy stores and stimulating appetite, preparing the body for daytime activities. Cortisol releases an additional amount of energy by promoting gluconeogenesis, release of free fatty acids, and protein catabolism. Cortisol leads to both positive and negative effects on bone tissue and has pro‐apoptotic activity in some types of cells, including neuronal cells, both neuronal and glial, mediating organization, plasticity, or degeneration. Cortisol also participates in the control of food intake, body temperature, and nociception. Its vasoconstrictor action increases blood circulation and oxygen distribution to muscles and brain (Dickmeis, 2009; Nicolaides et al. 2000).

Sleep onset occurs when cortisol levels are extremely low. Two to three hours after the onset of sleep, cortisol begins to rise, coinciding with REM sleep. Nighttime awakenings coincide with nighttime pulses of cortisol and are followed by temporary inhibition of their secretion. At the end of sleep, cortisol rises rapidly for 1 h, generating the morning peak (Chung et al. 2011; Miller et al. 2017).

Only 10--15% of plasma cortisol circulates freely, consisting of the bioactive fraction, which can be quantified in oral fluid. Castro and Moreira (2003) reviewed the usefulness of this biomarker in the diagnosis of diseases related to the hypothalamic--pituitary--adrenal (HPA) axis. Oral fluid samples can be collected several times a day, allowing for assessments concerning the dynamics of free cortisol secretion. Deregulation of the circadian cortisol cycle is associated with fatigue, weight loss, insomnia, coronary artery disease, immune deficiency, and tumor progression (Mavroudis et al. 2012).

### Circadian changes in Antarctic expedition members

Arendt (2012) conducted a review of studies on changes in circadian rhythm in individuals living and working in the polar regions. Variations in 6sMT urinary excretion were reported, both for long nights and long days, with changes in the circadian rhythm confirmed by analysis of 6sMT, electrolytes, cortisol, and other hormones in biological samples. The main consequence appeared in quality of sleep, with difficulty initiating sleep (sleep latency), decreased restorative efficiency of sleep, decreased slow wave sleep (stages 3 and 4), more awakenings, and decreased total sleep time. Physical and cognitive performance were impaired under these conditions.

Longer melatonin production time was observed during the winter and correlated with seasonal affective disorder (SAD), the symptoms of which remitted after photic stimulation, shortening the melatonin production period. Phototherapy has been used to treat individuals with circadian disorders (Leger et al. 2018). Photic stimulation for 0.5--1 h in the early morning improved sleep patterns and performance in expeditioners who wintered over in Antarctica (Palinkas et al. 2010). According to the authors, depressive and angry symptoms were often associated with changes in thyroid hormones (decreases in free T3), and when photic treatment was compared with thyroxine supplementation, the former was more efficient in normalizing TSH and free T3 levels, improving mood.

Mottram et al. (2011) investigated the effect of exposure to daytime, natural, or blue‐enriched light on certain sleep parameters at the British Antarctic Station. The best sleep timing, duration, efficiency, and quality were observed in summer, with natural light conditions. During the winter, circadian phase was 45 min earlier on average when the crew was exposed to white light. However, an increase in the maximum amount of light exposure per 24 h, irrespective of spectral composition, was the most important factor for sleep timing and sleep quality.

Najjar et al. (2014, apud van Ombergen et al. 2021) investigated the effect of 2‐week exposure to daytime, blue‐enriched light on sleep of expeditioners at Concordia Station during winter. A small but significant delay (30 min) was observed during weeks under standard white light. No circadian phase delay of melatonin was detected during use of blue‐enriched light, and an increase in cognitive performance and mood levels was observed. When solar light is not available for extended periods, as normally occurs in Antarctic winter, this strategy could be used to adjust circadian rhythms and minimize health impacts.

Moraes et al. (2020) evaluated some hormones including salivary melatonin in 10 individuals during 26‐day ship travel followed by 24‐day camping in the Antarctic field during the summer season at the Brazilian Antarctic Station. A typical day on the ship was characterized by low levels of physical activity and exposure to low luminosity and warm environment. In contrast, a typical day in the Antarctic camp was characterized by high levels of physical activity and exposure to high incidence of natural light and cold environment. An increase in salivary melatonin at night (72.3%) was observed during ship travel, although the rhythm was preserved. During camping, some individuals returned to initial values (like the second day of ship travel) while other crew members maintained pre‐camp values. Increases in depression score were significant on the ship but not in the camp.

## PHYSIOLOGICAL ADAPTATION TO COLD

Working in a cold environment (<10° C) for more than 2 h is associated with negative impacts on physical and cognitive performance and is considered a significant stressor for individuals that need to work under such conditions. Attention is diverted to the discomfort of cold, affecting the performance of tasks that require attention and vigilance. Although safety protocols and personal protective equipment (PPE) aim to minimize the risk of hypothermia, thermal discomfort remains an important cause of accidents, the number of which increases with decreasing temperatures (Morris et al. 2017). In addition, the most frequent signs and symptoms in workers exposed to cold involve the respiratory tract, cardiovascular system, musculoskeletal system, and skin. The most common respiratory symptoms are shortness of breath, wheezing, mucus production, and cough, with the development of chronic bronchitis, stress asthma, and chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. In the musculoskeletal system, reports of muscle pain are common, especially in the back, shoulders, neck, and knees. Cold‐induced hypertension and exercise‐induced hypertension are additive effects, where individuals with certain cardiovascular diseases or those more sensitive to these effects are at greater risk of developing cardiac complications. Diabetic individuals display a deficiency in the adaptive mechanisms to cold due to peripheral neuropathy and vasculopathy, with increased risk of health harms. Some types of skin lesions are common in the cold environment and mainly affect workers' feet, hands, and face. Changes in finger color may occur (reddish or bluish), as well as pain, paresthesia, decreased skin sensitivity, manual dexterity, and even cases of frostbite (Mäkinen & Hassi, 2009). There are regulations in the labor area that limit the time of exposure to cold environments according to the temperature, alternating time between these environments and others with milder temperatures.

### Thermoregulation

According to Dougherty (2010), human body temperature is controlled by neurons located in a region of the anterior hypothalamus called the preoptic area (POA). This region receives stimuli from nerve endings in the skin and abdomen for thermoception, which pass the stimulus to the spinal cord and from there to the brainstem, reaching the hypothalamus. Three types of neurons in the POA participate in thermoregulation, namely those sensitive to heat (30%), sensitive to cold, and insensitive to temperature variations (60%). The neurons responsible for the perception of warmth are WSN (warm‐sensing neurons). The warm sensation begins to be perceived at temperatures >34 °C, mediated by activation of TRPV‐type ion channels. Different subtypes of this receptor have different perception thresholds, some of which are activated only at temperatures >45 °C. POA neurons activated by the perception of cold are CSN (cold‐sensing neurons). They represent only 5% of the neurons in the POA but are more abundant in the posterior hypothalamus. They are actively inhibited by WSN (McKemy, 2013; Refinetti, 2010). Neurons insensitive to temperature variations cooperate in activating CSN neurons in the absence of inhibition exerted by WSN neurons. The cold sensation begins to be perceived at temperatures <31 °C, mediated by activation of TRPM‐type ion channels. Different subtypes of this receptor display different perception thresholds, some of which are only activated in intense cold (<12 °C) (McKemy, 2013). Once activated, warm-sensitive and cold-sensitive neurons send stimuli to other regions of the hypothalamus (paraventricular nucleus, lateral hypothalamus, and posterior hypothalamus) that command the sympathetic and parasympathetic autonomic mechanisms related to heat loss/containment (vasomotor action) and heat production (metabolic rate) (Refinetti, 1992; Szymusiak, 2017).

Rectal temperature is the gold standard for estimating body temperature resulting from thermoregulation, ranging from  $36.1^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  around 5:00 a.m. to  $37.7^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  around 6:00 p.m., with gradual increases and decreases throughout the day and night, respectively. The circadian rhythm of body temperature is under control of the suprachiasmatic nucleus of the hypothalamus, which also innervates the POA (Refinetti, 2010).

Exposure to cold is correlated with sleep disorders. Neural control of sleep and thermoregulation are co-located in POA. Neuronal populations that participate in sleep regulation are WSN. When WSN are activated, they trigger mechanisms to lower body temperature, that is, reduction in the metabolic rate and relaxation of the vessels for heat loss in the periphery. The decreasing body temperature phase then begins, when sleep usually starts. In this phase, sleep latency is low and sleep duration is longer. Conversely, increased heat production and heat conservation accompany increases in body temperature and promote awakening. The increases in melatonin normally associated with the onset of sleep activate WSN. Disorders in circadian cycle control may not affect the amount of sleep, but can affect the period of their occurrence, and decreases in body temperature persist during this period, when the person is sleeping. Moderate to intense temperatures, both cold and hot, cause sleep fragmentation or sleep suppression (Szymusiak, 2017).

During cold adaptation, the initial event comprises the contraction of peripheral blood vessels, with activation of  $\alpha$ -adrenergic receptors in the vessels to minimize heat loss. In addition, the hypothalamic thermoregulatory center sends a signal to the skeletal musculature to initiate involuntary contractions (tremors). At the peak of shivering, heat production increases up to five times the baseline (Kaikaew et al., 2018). Serum norepinephrine levels were found to be elevated in several experiments exposing humans to cold air or cold water, but without significant changes in epinephrine levels, which demonstrates the nonparticipation of the adrenal medulla at this initial cold adaptation stage (Paakkonen &amp; Leppaluoto, 2002). These changes in peripheral vasoconstriction and thermogenic tremors tend to decrease with exposure time to cold (Keramidas et al., 2018).

The sympathetic stimulus sent by the hypothalamus activates adrenergic receptors in adipose tissue and muscle. In white adipose tissue, activation of β‐receptors leads to an increase in lipolytic activity, which in turn increases the concentration of free fatty acids and glycerol, used as fuel. In brown adipose tissue, stimulation of β3‐adrenergic receptors leads to the expression of genes in the thermogenesis program. Among the several events that occur, mitochondrial biogenesis and increased expression of type 2 deiodinase (D2) are the most noteworthy, with a consequent increase in the rate of thyroid hormone tissue activation (T_{4} → T_{3}). There is also an increase in the amount of beige adipose tissue, which displays intermediate characteristics between white and brown adipose tissue and contains β_{3} receptors and similar physiology to brown tissue, contributing significantly to thermogenesis (Phillips, 2019; Yau & Yen, 2020). Increased lipolysis and type 2 deiodinase activity are also observed in skeletal muscle (Louzada & Carvalho, 2018), in addition to increased glucose uptake (Shiuchi et al. 2017).

In the pancreas, adrenergic stimulation leads to increased glucagon release. After activation of glucagon receptors in the liver, glycogen breakdown occurs, releasing glucose into the blood, converting amino acids to glucose and activating the breakdown of lipids into fatty acids to supply fuel to tissues. In the liver, activation of β‐adrenergic receptors produces the same effects as glucagon (Yagami, 1995).

Intracellular T_{3} hormone interacts with its nuclear alpha type (TRα) receptors, enhancing β‐adrenergic signaling, and interacts with its nuclear beta type (TRβ) receptors, increasing the expression of several proteins, including uncoupling proteins (UCP) (Maushart et al. 2019; Phillips, 2019). UCPs are a subset of the mitochondrial carrier proteins located on the inner mitochondrial membrane. By dissipating the proton gradient, they decouple oxidative phosphorylation, and the energy, no longer conducted to form ATP, dissipates as heat. The expression of UCP‐3 isoform in skeletal muscle as well as in brown adipose tissue makes these tissues important adaptive thermogenesis mediators (Depieri et al. 2004; Iwen et al. 2018). UCP activation is a highly regulated process that prevents collapse in the production of ATP. With UCP activation, cold‐induced shivering decreases (Phillips, 2019). In addition, T_{3} increases membrane permeability to sodium and calcium ions and activates the Na/K‐ATPase pump, increasing heat dissipation by ATP hydrolysis, while also controlling the expression of proteins involved in lipid metabolism and increasing glucose uptake and enzyme expression in the glycolytic pathway.

Fekete and Lechan (2014) conducted an extensive review on central regulation of the hypothalamic--pituitary--thyroid (HPT) axis, indicating that cold stimulates the production of TRH (thyrotropin‐releasing hormone) via β‐adrenergic receptors in hypophysiotropic neurons of the paraventricular nucleus (PVN) in the hypothalamus. After its synthesis, TRH is stored in granules at nerve endings in the median eminence, and its release into the pituitary system occurs via stimulation of α‐adrenergic receptors (Joseph‐Bravo, Jaimes‐Hoy, Uribe, & Charli, 2015).

TRH‐producing neurons are densely innervated by adrenergic nerve endings originating from the brainstem. Other regulatory stimuli for TRH synthesis are received by these neurons, originating from the suprachiasmatic nucleus (circadian rhythm), medial dorsal nucleus (MDN), and arcuate nucleus (ARCN). The latter receives peripheral signaling via ghrelin, leptin, glucose, insulin, and free fatty acid levels and sends regulatory signals via orexigenic and anorexigenic peptides, inhibiting or stimulating TRH synthesis, respectively. Leptin also plays a regulatory role via MDN that also receives innervation from the POA. The integration of this information determines the pace and intensity of TRH production. In the median eminence, TRH is partially degraded by the enzyme PPII, whose activity modulates the amount of TRH that reaches the pituitary (Joseph‐Bravo, Jaimes‐Hoy, & Charli, 2015).

The stimulating effect of cold on TRH expression, biosynthesis, and processing does not depend on regulation of TRH synthesis by feedback loops (Fekete & Lechan, 2014). Joseph‐Bravo, Jaimes‐Hoy, Uribe, and Charli (2015) also reported this characteristic in their review of central control of the HPT axis. They state that human exposure to cold for more than 60 h activates the HPT axis. Increased TRH levels in the hypothalamus are then followed by an increase in serum TSH within about 30 min and an increase in T_{4} after a few hours.

Expression of cold‐induced TRH is independent of serum thyroid hormone levels or nutritional status (leptin levels), in a mechanism that has still not been elucidated. Cold stimulation, however, is not effective in the presence of chronic stress or corticosterone injections. Unlike the acute stress produced by cold, which stimulates TRH production via the β‐adrenergic pathway, chronic stress prevents the efficient response of the HPT axis to cold, decreasing lipolysis and leading the body to stimulate food intake and decrease physical activity (“feeling of fatigue”). The increased levels of orexigenic neuropeptides (NPY and AGRP) inhibit TRH synthesis in the PVN (Fröhlich & Wahl, 2019; Joseph‐Bravo, Jaimes‐Hoy, & Charli, 2015). This interaction between the two axes (HPA and HPT) may explain the discrepancies in thyroid hormone levels in different studies conducted in Antarctica. Stress levels may differ between research stations, depending on the intensity and duration of stressors such as cold, altitude, confinement, sociocultural factors, and type of prior training received by the expeditionary team (coping strategies). These factors can determine the quality of the

adaptive process (Sadock, 2017) or the phenotypes of thyroid allostatic load (Chatzitomaris et al. 2017). In type 1 phenotype, TSH and free T4 are low or normal, total T3 and T4 are low, free T3 is low, and reverse T3 is high. This phenotype reflects a decreased TSH release, decreased binding to thyroglobulin, downregulation of deiodinases D1/D2, and upregulation of D3. This phenotype is common in fetal life, depression, starvation, and exhausting physical exercise. Type 2 phenotype is the opposite, with TSH and free T4 high or normal, high levels of total T3 and T4, low levels of free T3, and high levels of reverse T3. This type 2 phenotype reflects an increased TSH release, increased binding to thyroglobulin, upregulation of D1/D2, and downregulation of D3, and is common in pregnancy, obesity, endurance exercise, adaptation to cold, acute psychosis, and posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD). However, a mixed phenotype can be observed when energy deprivation is associated with another stressor, like altitude sleep deprivation or confinement (Alhazmi et al. 2018; Hackney et al. 1995).

### Changes in the HPT axis during polar expeditions

The first observations of changes in the HPT axis in both the Arctic and Antarctica date to the 1960s (Eastman et al. 1974). Several researchers since then have included the evaluation of thyroid hormones in populations exposed to prolonged cold (Eastman et al. 1974; Leppäluoto et al. 1998; Levy et al. 2013; Moraes et al. 2020; Reed, 1995; Reed et al. 1990; Savourey et al. 1994; Solter et al. 1989; Woods et al. 2015).

Polar T_{3} syndrome was originally considered an adaptive change in the thyroid, characterized by low free T_{3} levels in the presence of normal TSH levels. However, some discrepancies appear between the various studies. In general, free T_{4} levels are either decreased, slightly increased, or unchanged, while TSH is unchanged or slightly increased (Pääkkönen & Leppäluoto, 2002). The apparent discrepancies may result from different factors, with variations in temperature, photoperiod, time spent in the cold environment, stress level, and the methodology used to collect and analyze samples. TSH presents some isoforms which may display different biological activities, despite having the same immunoassay reactivity. Thus, even if an individual preferentially produces a certain isoform of TSH with low biological activity, the immunoassay will show normal TSH values, not distinguishing whether the form is active or inactive (Estrada et al. 2014)

Levy et al. (2013) studied changes in T_{3}, T_{4}, and TSH levels in a Siberian population during the summer (10--32 °C) and winter (-20 to -50 °C) months, noting a TSH increase of 17--18% and decreases in free T_{4} and T_{3} of 12--16% and 25--26%, respectively, for winter values compared with values detected in summer. In eight other studies cited by the authors, TSH and free T_{4} responses varied greatly, but free T_{3} was mostly reduced.

Chen et al. (2016) compared variations in thyroid hormones in two groups of expeditioners who spent a year in Antarctica, one remaining at the station located on King George Island, with milder temperatures (annual average of -2.5 °C), without polar days or nights, and with several bases nearby, and another located further east on the continent, with lower average annual temperature (-10 °C), with polar days and nights and no nearby bases in winter. Larger decreases in free T_{4} and gradual TSH increases were observed in more extreme conditions, with the increase only statistically significant in the 12th month. The authors concluded that changes in the HPT axis are consistent with central hypothyroidism, associated with intensity of cold, time spent in the cold environment, and photoperiod. According to Üstündağ et al. (2020), Melatonin can affect the thyroid gland directly or indirectly. However, the underlying mechanisms are not yet fully elucidated. Melatonin has typical inhibitory effects on the thyroid gland and T_{4} secretion. According to Kovaničová et al. (2020), shivering induced by cold exposure was associated with increased TSH and decreased T_{3} and T_{4} levels after 15 min of ice‐water swimming. However, nonshivering from cold exposure failed to regulate PTH and free T_{4}, while TSH decreased. The authors concluded that PTH and TH regulation during cold exposure in humans varies by cold acclimatization level and/or cold stimulus intensity.

Thom and Lugg (2002) report widespread scientific and anecdotal evidence of heavy alcohol consumption in Antarctica in modern‐day over‐wintering expeditions. Interestingly, ethanol consumption increases TRH expression in PVN, but blocks this expression when induced by cold. Ethanol consumption thus interferes with the adaptive response of the HPT axis (Joseph‐Bravo, Jaimes‐Hoy, & Charli, 2015).

The 3,5‐T_{2} metabolite produced by type 2 deiodinase from T_{3} displays thermogenic activity, in contrast to other inactive T_{2} metabolites, but the exact mechanism of its action is still unclear. # 3.3 | The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis

As the sympathetic component of the autonomic nervous system, cortical and medullary activation of the adrenal system has been well-established as pivotal in adaptation to physical and psychological stress. Alteration in the HPA axis has been investigated in Antarctic expeditioners (Bishop, 2004; Harinath et al., 2005; Harris et al., 2010; Moraes et al., 2020; Strewe et al., 2018). We now present a summary of HPA axis regulation to help understand the results of these surveys.

The highest concentration of CRH (corticotrophin-releasing hormone) neurons occurs in the hypothalamic PVN. The set of hypophysiotropic neurons that synthesize CRH comprise the primary regulators of the HPA axis and are projected towards the median eminence, where CRH and vasopressin (VP) are stored for later concurrent release in the pituitary portal system. The stimulation input for CRH synthesis comes from adrenergic and glutamatergic neurons. Glutamatergic terminal signaling is modulated by the endocannabinoid presynaptic receptor  $(\mathrm{CB}_1)$ . By contrast, the inhibitory input comes from GABAergic neurons. Circadian information from the SCN reaches the hypothalamus (PVN) via vasopressinergic stimulation, synchronizing the production of CRH. Physiologic stressors (e.g., cold) and psychogenic stressors (e.g., confinement) can similarly stimulate the PVN to synthesize CRH (Joseph-Bravo, Jaimes-Hoy, &amp; Charli, 2015), stored in the median eminence (ME). Secretion from the ME depends on glutamatergic neuronal signaling. Once released, CRH and VP interact with their receptors in the pituitary, which begins the synthesis and release of ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone). MC2R (melanocortin receptor type 2), specific to ACTH, are present

in the adrenal cortex. In response to the activation of these receptors, the adrenal synthesizes and releases glucocorticoids to the bloodstream, consisting mainly of cortisol in humans (Herman et al. 2016). In the adrenal medulla, glucocorticoids promote the conversion of norepinephrine into epinephrine for release into the bloodstream (Wurtman & Axelrod, 1965). Adrenal sensitivity to ACTH (receptor production) varies in a circadian fashion, and the SCN has a direct influence on the adrenal through the autonomic nervous system (Herman & Tasker, 2016).

According to Karin et al. (2020), there are two phases during the stress pulse. The initial phase occurs after de onset of the stressor and before adaptation to the stressor. It lasts several weeks and causes elevation of CRH, ACTH, and cortisol levels. However, over weeks of stress input, the pituitary corticotroph and adrenal masses grow (hyperplasia). This is an example of dynamical compensation in physiological systems. A larger functional mass means that less ACTH is needed to maintain high cortisol levels, driving ACTH down to baseline. This also happens to hypothalamic CRH, and such a return to baseline is called “exact adaptation.” When the prolonged stress input is over, the glands return to baseline over several weeks.

Upon reaching a serum threshold, cortisol acts on both the hypothalamus and the pituitary gland, inhibiting its own release (negative feedback mechanism) (Herman & Tasker, 2016). Two feedback mechanisms are recognized for the HPA axis—one fast, nongenomic, probably mediated by the putative membrane GR receptor, and the other slow, genomic, mediated by the classic intracellular GR receptor. In the hypothalamus, the mechanism underlying the fast inhibitory effect involves cortisol‐induced endocannabinoid release in the PVN, inhibiting the glutamatergic neurons, in addition to negative regulation in other brain regions that innervate the PVN. The underlying mechanism in the slow inhibitory effect involves protein--protein interaction between GR and CREB, suppressing CRH transcription. In the pituitary, the mechanism underlying the fast inhibitory effect involves inhibition of ACTH release by interference in voltage‐activated potassium channels and impairing annexin 1 translocation. Meanwhile, the slow mechanism involves inhibition of ACTH synthesis (Gjerstad et al. 2018). Figure 5 summarize the feedback control of HPA axis.

Exposure to stressors can increase cortisol levels acutely by rapid activation of neural pathways afferent to the PVN, but 24‐h cortisol remains both in phase and in amplitude in these situations. Activation patterns and neural circuitry vary according to stressor type, intensity, and duration. However, in persons chronically exposed to stressors, cortisol levels are generally elevated throughout the day, overshadowing normal circadian variations. Maintenance of high cortisol levels is a poorly adaptive process that can cause memory impairment, immunosuppression, central obesity, and cardiometabolic and reproductive disorders (Chung et al. 2011; Oster et al. 2017).

In animal studies, psychogenic stressors associated with desensitization of ACTH responses to repeated stimuli induce transient CRH synthesis, which returns to baseline levels after few exposures to the stimulus. However, no desensitization of ACTH responses was seen in other situations of repeated homotypic stress (severe immobilization or foot shock for 14 days), with increased CRH synthesis after each stress exposure or when a new (heterotypic) stressor was introduced, suggesting the development of plasticity changes that facilitate the response to stress (Aguilera & Liu, 2012).

### PVN plasticity under chronic stress

CRH‐producing PVN neurons show remarkable plasticity after repeated exposure to stressors. The excitatory input to PVN is enhanced by the increase in the density of adrenergic/noradrenergic and glutamatergic terminals, both in the neuronal body and in dendrites, and glutamate receptors become more sensitive to stimulation. The inhibitory input via GABA decreases by reducing the frequency of opening of chlorine channels and decreasing GABA_{A} receptor expression. A decrease in the endocannabinoid system's inhibitory effect in the glutamatergic terminals has also been observed in chronic stress. The neural influence is high enough to overcome the negative feedback control of glucocorticoids, which also contributes to the reduction in glucocorticoid receptor expression in the PVN. In the pituitary gland, the synthesis of CRH and VP receptors and consequent sensitivity to them increases to maintain high ACTH levels. Noradrenergic innervation and estrogen also increase adrenal sensitivity to ACTH, which may increase cortisol release regardless of ACTH levels. The opposite effect is produced by testosterone. The consequence is maintenance of high cortisol levels (Herman & Tasker, 2016).

According to McEwen (2000), glucocorticoids, catecholamines, and cytokines, the primary hormonal mediators of the stress response, have both protective and damaging effects on the body, depending on the amount and duration of stress. The allostatic mechanism of adaptation to acute stress involves the output of stress hormones which act to restore homeostasis in the face of a challenge. ### Cortisol as a stress biomarker

A 24‐h urine sample can be used to measure daily cortisol release. Alternatively, other tests can be useful to show an excess or deficiency in cortisol production, such as salivary cortisol at night (11:00 p.m. to midnight) or in the morning (8:00 to 10:00 a.m.). Meanwhile, to check for rhythm disturbances in cortisol release, salivary or serum cortisol can be determined in multiple samples, collected at different times of the day (Oster et al. 2017). Egawa et al. (2018) conducted a review of the use of oral fluid cortisol as a biomarker, assessing it along with other parameters to measure stress levels in individuals working in a confined environment, reporting higher values associated with psychological stress. The author accessed salivary cortisol at 7:00, 12:30, 15:00, and 21:00 h, during 14 days of confinement.

CRH test, dexamethasone suppression test (DST), and dexamethasone/CRH test are challenge tests used to evaluate HPA axis dysfunction. These tests are more usual in clinical settings, for diagnosis of Cushing's syndrome, differentiation between Cushing's and pseudo‐Cushing conditions, and diagnosis/differentiation of neuropsychiatric disorders. These tests were well described by Dogra and Vijayashankar (2021). CRH test is used to test the responsiveness of the pituitary in producing ACTH and, by consequence, cortisol. ACTH and cortisol peak occur 15--30 min and 30--60 min, respectively, after IV administration of CRH. No more than a 35% increase in plasma ACTH is expected in healthy individuals. The DST is based on the administration of dexamethasone (DEX), a synthetic glucocorticoid that binds with high affinity to glucocorticoid receptor (GR). This simulates the molecular cascade for negative feedback with the resulting suppression of cortisol release. Therefore, DES can assess the lack of suppression of the HPA axis by cortisol. Blood is collected from the subject to measure cortisol response, with the expectation that most individuals will have diminished serum cortisol levels. Various protocols exist for this test (Du & Pang, 2015; Pivonello et al. 2015). DEX/CRH test is based on the rationale that glucocorticoid suppression of the HPA axis can be overcome by CRH stimulation. Two hours after DEX administration, CRH is infused, and serum cortisol is evaluated after 15 min. The cumbersomeness of this test limits its application in the ambulatory setting (Dogra & Vijayashankar, 2021). According to Watson et al. (2006), this test presented greater sensitivity and specificity than the DES test for diagnosis of neuropsychiatric disorders.

### Changes in the HPA axis during polar expeditions

Harinath et al. (2005) investigated salivary cortisol in two Indian Antarctic expeditions to Maitri Station (sea level). The summer team was evaluated at Delhi, during the stay on the ship, and on days 7, 30, and 60 of their stay in Antarctica. The winter team was evaluated in a similar way. Epinephrine and norepinephrine were also evaluated in 24‐h urine samples. Salivary cortisol was higher on days 7 and 30 but gradually returned to baseline levels on day 60 in both the summer and winter teams. Urinary excretion of epinephrine and norepinephrine on days 7 and 30 was higher than the Delhi values but decreased by day 60 of stay. However, in the winter team the urinary excretion of epinephrine and norepinephrine was higher than in the summer team. The authors suggested that regular cold exposure at the Antarctic summer camp led to cold tolerance and attenuation of sympathetic tone, unlike the occasional cold exposure observed in the winter team, who spent more time inside the station due to the extreme outdoor conditions. According to Aguilera and Liu (2012), another characteristic of chronic stress is that regardless of habituation to the repeated homotopic stressor, there is generally increased CRH synthesis and HPA axis responsiveness to a novel or heterotypic stressor, suggesting the development of plasticity changes that facilitate the response to stress.

Harris et al. (2010) investigated the diurnal rhythm of salivary cortisol in personnel (n = 55) over‐wintering at two British Antarctic stations (sea level). Evaluations were performed immediately after arrival at the station, midwinter, and the last week before departure, consisting of saliva sampling at awakening, 15 and 45 min after awaking, and at 3:00 and 10:00 p.m. on three consecutive days. The three morning samples were used to calculate cortisol awakening response (CAR). Although daily rhythm was preserved, CAR level was higher at arrival. The authors explained the results, considering arrival the most stressful part of the stay due to high activity levels at the research stations where routines need to be established in a novel and challenging environment, with low outdoor temperatures and constant daylight. As observed by Aguilera and Liu (2012), different responses can be displayed according to the stressors' type, intensity, and exposure frequency.

Strewe et al. (2018) studied three expeditionary crews at Germany Neumayer III Station (sea level) and two at the French‐Italian Concordia Station (high altitude) to measure the levels of catecholamines, glucocorticoids, and

---

endocannabinoids over an 11‐month period including winter confinement. Endocannabinoids were 10 times higher at sea level, reflecting a possible downregulation of hypobaric hypoxia on endocannabinoid response. The increase in endocannabinoid signaling can reduce glutamatergic stimulation and neuroendocrine response to stress, as the norepinephrine and cortisol levels increase less at sea level than at high altitudes.

In Moraes et al. (2020), salivary cortisol was assessed at 7:00 a.m. and 7:00 p.m. in 10 individuals during 26‐day ship travel followed by 24‐day camping in the Antarctic field during the summer season at the Brazilian Antarctic Station (sea level). Data were collected on days 2, 16, and 26 aboard the ship and days 4, 11, and 23 of camping. The difference between cortisol in the morning and in the afternoon was used to characterize circadian rhythm and calculate cortisol range. Cortisol rhythm was preserved, but range varied according to place. Inside the ship, with a temperature‐controlled environment, a decrease in salivary cortisol was observed between days 2 and 26. In the Antarctic camp (a cold environment), salivary cortisol increased by 72.1% over pre‐camp values. The effect of cold and other stressors associated with camping was decisive for the increase in cortisol.

## CONCLUSION

Prolonged periods of daylight or darkness, intense cold, confinement, and isolation are characteristic of the Antarctic winter. To survive in these extreme conditions, the body undergoes several physiological adaptations. Although they are not permanent changes, as they are restricted to the period the individual remains in Antarctica, they can represent a susceptibility factor for the development of physical and psychological dysfunctions. The biochemical mechanisms that mediate these changes overlap at some point and undergo mutual influence, making it difficult to establish a single causal factor. The knowledge accumulated during years of Antarctic research indicates that certain preventive measures can minimize these dysfunctions, including the provision of adequate food, discipline with well‐established mealtimes, physical activity, and even the use of phototherapy and vitamin D supplementation during the winter, all of which help maintain circadian rhythm and adequate physical and mental functioning. High‐tech clothing and room‐temperature control at research stations provide greater protection against intense cold effects. The perception of isolation, because of the region's geographic and climatic characteristics, may be reduced by the availability of internet connectivity at stations, which can additionally allow for implementation of telemedicine. An issue that is still difficult to solve is the confinement and forced coexistence of diverse people, who must deal with the lack of privacy and cultural and gender differences, generating interpersonal problems, considered the main cause of psychological stress, which has a negative influence on physical and mental health through chemical mediators (e.g. norepinephrine and cortisol). The introduction of mental healthcare with training in coping strategies and remote monitoring adds to the preventive measures, aiming to mitigate the health problems of Antarctic expedition members.
