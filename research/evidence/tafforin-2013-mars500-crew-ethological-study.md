# ABSTRACT

A Mars mission is a new challenge for scientific investigators in the space field. What would be the behavioral profile of an interplanetary crew with long-duration social isolation and spatial restriction? The current study addresses this question with the first ethological data from the Mars-500 experiment. It took place in Moscow, Russia from June 3, 2010 to November 4, 2011. It was designed to simulate the living and working conditions of an isolated and confined crew over 250 days for reaching Mars, 30 days for Mars orbiting with Mars landing and 240 days for returning to Earth. The Mars-500 crew was composed of three Russians, two Europeans and one Chinese. The Mars-500 facilities comprised four hermetically sealed, interconnected modules and a Martian surface module. We applied the ethological method based on observation, description and quantification of the individual and inter-individual behaviors in terms of personal actions, visual interactions, object interactions, body interactions, facial expressions and collateral acts. These events were scored on the Observer XT® software, from video recordings made every two weeks at breakfast time inside the habitat module. We found the following results: a diminishing collective time from the first phase corresponding to the 250-day trip to Mars to the second phase corresponding to the 240-day return to Earth; 35-day cycles then 70-day cycles of high duration of personal actions within these phases; periodic oscillations of duration of inter-personal actions; decreasing then increasing occurrences of facial expressions with temporal points of decrements, around day 159 and day 355, after 6 months and one year of simulation; increasing occurrences of collateral acts over the full 520-day journey. We discuss the findings with regard to a Mars mission scenario. Time has a major impact on the behavioral profile, as shown by indicators of physical and psychological states of fatigue, stress, well being and good spirit. The interplanetary crew follows phasic, cyclic, periodic and punctual behavioral changes in a temporal dynamic as a positive adaptation.

# 1. Introduction

The future challenges of human Mars exploration lead to new goals for investigations in space life sciences. For 20 years, ethological work in this field has dealt with behavioral strategies of men and women in new, peculiar and unusual environments, both real situations (orbital flights and parabolic flights), analogs (polar missions) and simulations (bedrest, confinement and isolation). Our model of adaptation is built on interconnected systems that implement



---

neuro-physiological mechanisms, sensory-motor mechanisms, socio-psychological mechanisms [1] manifested by the subjects' behaviors. Their behaviors are dynamic and change from day to day, week to week and month to month, over the course of a Mars mission scenario for more than 500 days. With that perspective, the scientific goal is to analyze the effects of time in isolated and confined environments. What would be the behavioral profile of an interplanetary crew with long-duration social isolation and spatial restriction? The behavioral profiles observed during interplanetary travels will expand the knowledge of crews' adaptation in terms of their psychosocial strategies and their physical capabilities, in space and over time. A mission to Mars would last 520 days, when considering a 250-day Earth to Mars trip, one month of orbiting with landing on the surface and a 240-day Mars to Earth trip.

Until now, preparation for long-duration space missions was performed for average 150-day stays onboard the International Space Station (ISS) and beyond, onboard the Russian orbital station (Mir) which lasted 439 days. The primary emphases were on psychosocial interactions [2], cultural differences [3] and verbal communications [4] among crewmembers. A recent survey with respect to the inter-personal and organizational issues during long-duration space missions [5] indicates that time has a major impact on behavioral profiles. It is the prime hypothesis in our study.

Space travel analog environments have been performed in Antarctic polar stations. During summer campaigns (lasting 2--4 months) and winter-overs (lasting 8--9 months), the winterers' behaviors were studied in terms of their adaptive abilities to cope with sensory and social deprivation. During the French missions at the Dumont d'Urville polar station, typical symptoms of winter-over mental syndrome were initially described in three successive phases: alarm reaction, stage of resistance and stage of exhaustion, from the psychological point of view [6]. More recently, we showed behavioral adaptive strategies based on periods of organization, disorganization and reorganization of the group, from the ethological point of view [7]. During the first Franco--Italian mission at Concordia, the behavioral observations confirmed periodic changes in social grouping [8]. Our second hypothesis is that the crew's non-verbal behavior during a simulated 520-day journey from Earth to Mars would follow cyclic or periodic changes as a dynamic process of adaptation.

Simulations of extended periods of time in isolation and confinement are experimental conditions of crewmembers' stays in closed and reduced habitats. Ethological investigations performed during the Isolation Study of European Manned Space Infrastructure (ISEMSI), the EXperimental campaign of European Manned Space Infrastructure (EXEMSI) and the Human Behavior in Extended Spaceflight (HUBES) simulation showed changes in social behavior over time with a decrease in the use of personal space. This was balanced by an increase in the use of public space, with a high level of body mobility through the initial period to the final period of the confinement campaigns of 28-day duration, 60-day duration and 135-day duration [9]. The mid-periods were found to be a critical time. Our third hypothesis is that within the main phases of adaptation, specific temporal points could appear in crew's behavior profile.

Today, a 520-day experimental paradigm to promote further advances in our understanding of the time factor in isolated and confined environments is the Mars-500 experiment. It is a unique simulation of highly autonomous conditions with two-way communication with Earth for up to 40 min total duration. From a previous 105-day pilot study, different tendencies in communicative behavior [10] and distinctive sub-groups based on personal values [11] have been identified. With a focus on a daily life activity, we expected that the individual and inter-individuals manifestations would follow increments or decrements in their occurrence during the dynamic process of adaptation.

## Materials and methods

### Mars-500 experiment

The Mars-500 program began on June 3, 2010 and ended on November 4, 2011. The full 520-day experimental paradigm simulated different phases of a mission to Mars: a 250-day interplanetary flight from Earth to Mars, a 30-day orbital stay that included the Mars landing and a 240-day interplanetary flight from Mars to Earth. A 20-min delay communication was simulated.

The Mars-500 facilities were located at the Institute of BioMedical Problems (IBMP) in Moscow, Russia. They were composed of four hermetically sealed, interconnected modules: the habitable module, the medical module, the storage module and the Mars landing module (Fig. 1). The total volume of the modules was 550 m^{3} and the volume of the Martian surface was 1200 m^{3}.

The Mars-500 international crew was composed of three Russians, two Europeans and one Chinese (n=6). The subjects were all males, aged between 26 years and 38 years. Their functions during the experiment were one commander, two physicians and three engineers. They were selected on the basis of motivation, health and professional background. They were trained on payloads, simulated spacecraft systems, coping techniques and Baseline Data Collection. Food testing and team-building activities were an important part of training. All subjects were required to provide their informed consent to participate in the Mars-500 experiment. They reported that cameras did not represent an annoyance [12].

### Protocol of observations

The advanced logistic support was the subject's video recording incorporated into the observation protocol (Fig. 2). Two fixed-focus pan/tilt Charge-Coupled Device (CCD) cameras allotted for the surveillance coverage (no audio) were used and activated by the outside personnel at the experiment control center.

Twice a month, on Tuesday, video recordings were performed during breakfast time in the kitchen of the habitat module. The duration of the activity was not strictly limited. Breakfast was scheduled mainly between 9:00 and 10:00 a.m.. Evaluation lasted from when the first

---

subject sat at the table until the last subject left the table. The whole video files cover 1330 min for 36 temporal points of observation. Ethological data were collected on the same timeline as psychological questionnaires and sociometric measurements (Wireless Group Structure--WLGS) for complementary analyses (investigation #1.20). The questionnaires were based on verbal methods for investigating the subjects' perception of the situation (environmental evaluation) as well as their auto-perception (state of stress, coping and recovering processes). The WLGS tool provided with time measures based on time spent together for two subjects (crew cohesion time). Psychological and sociometric data are not available for the present analysis. They will be a part of future studies.

### Ethological analysis

The ethological method is based on a quantitative description of the spontaneous personal and social behavior observed in daily life activities (i.e. breakfasts). The analysis was supplied with a software based-solution, the Observer XT®, designed to collect, organize and process observational data from synchronized video files [13]. The observed actions and interactions were visually decrypted by the experimenter. The reproducibility of such a method has been shown in several space simulators and analog environments [14].

The non-verbal behavioral repertoire for the descriptive step at breakfast focused on the individual manifestations by coding the personal actions (any body segment movements, postural changes or global displacements), the collateral acts (any small movements with no manifest functions) and the facial expressions (any face movements as positive manifestations). The observations also focused on the inter-individual manifestations by coding the visual interactions (any look directed to one subject), the body interactions (any body contacts between two subjects) and the object interactions (any manipulation of one subject by another). In the quantitative step, we measured these behavioral events according to the descriptive statistics of duration of occurrence or frequency of occurrence. We present the results on the summed subjects (i.e. the whole crew).

# 3.2. Personal actions

The duration of personal actions (Fig. 4) varies according to the mission day for the whole crew. We observed alternating short and long durations within the range of 20–40 min (from day 19 to day 243, with a peak on day 159) then within the range of 30–60 min (from day 271 to day 513, with a peak on day 355). The results show cyclic changes that could be associated with 35-day cycles in one phase and 70-day cycles in the other phase. This result confirms that there are two phases of the mission, before and after the crew separation at mid-mission. The crewmembers avoided behavioral stereotypies, such as performing the same actions, during the same task, every day. They are defined as repetition or lack of variation in patterns of actions. It is not the case in the observed cyclic variation of personal actions.

# 3.3. Inter-personal actions

The visual interactions, object interactions and body interactions between the crewmembers are presented as inter-personal actions. Their duration changes according to the mission day (Fig. 5). We observed different variations with high oscillation from day 33 to day 104, medium oscillation from day 117 to day 173, and low oscillation from day 187 to day 243 as periods on the first phase, then high oscillation during the full second phase (day 271 to day 513), with a maximum duration of 30 min on day 320 and a minimum duration of 6 min on day 355. The results show periodic inter-individual manifestations.

# 3.4. Facial expressions

The relative occurrence of facial expressions (Fig. 6) was calculated as a percentage of the Mars-500 crew's total behavioral repertoire. They are the major manifestations that indicate well being such as smiling and laughing. The results show decreasing and increasing occurrences according to the mission day. These manifestations undergo sub-periods within the ranges of 15–20 min, 10–15 min, 15–20 min and 20–25 min, respectively. Difficult periods have been reported by crewmembers after 6 months (around days 159 and 173) and after 1 year (around days 355 and 369). Low percentages of facial expressions are a behavioral indicator of such periods.

# 3.5. Collateral acts

The relative occurrence of collateral acts (Fig. 7) was also calculated as the percentage of the Mars-500 crew's total behavioral repertoire. They are small movements with no manifest functions, such as scratching the nose, rubbing the head, etc. The results show increasing occurrences of collateral acts according to the mission day. They are behavioral indicators of stress and fatigue.

## Discussion

This quantitative description of behavioral events during a daily life activity has to be integrated in their occurrence context and linked with psychological issues to support the findings. We define the context. In the experiment, daily life activities were leisure time, rest time, training time, cleaning time and meal time. They were part of timelines that included working time, communication time and sleeping time. Work tasks were the panel of investigations that the crewmembers had to perform and completed with logistic operations as life-support and safety control. For instance, a standard day starts at 7:30 with “self-regulation morning”; 7:55 with medical control and cardiopsychological test; 9:00 with breakfast and “coordination meeting”; 10:00 with psychological (Ps) or physiological (Ph) investigations; 14:30 with dinner; 15:30 with training or Ps/Ph investigations or routine operations; 19:30 with supper; 22:00 with medical control; 22:55 with “self-regulation evening” then end of the working day. Communications were contacts with the outside world via written, video and audio messages. They are important in the psychological support to help the crew in times where daily live is getting problematic. A daily life activity such as breakfast had no strict time requirement and no specific goal other than a social activity. Breakfast time was always scheduled after medical control and cardiopsychological test for the whole crew. We could expect that the crew was involved in the same collective dynamics afterwards. But time spent together at the breakfast table (i.e. collective time) varies and thus shows that there is no unique crew profile. The variations were expressed through changes of personal actions, facial expressions, collateral acts and visual interactions. They are indicative of what the crewmember feels individually and collectively. High level of facial expressions at the end of the mission could be interpreted being due to the good spirit of the crewmembers upon completing the mission goal. Low level of collateral acts observed on day 19 and high level observed on day 513 are indicative of stress and fatigue and could be explained, for instance, by the increasing feeling of loneliness at the end of the mission [15].

During the first phase, corresponding to the 250-day trip from Earth to Mars, collective time was higher than during the second phase, corresponding to the 240-day return trip from Mars to Earth. No obvious contextual differences are relevant in the timelines between the two phases. However, it was shown that most of the key events (e.g. the landing on Mars simulation) altered crewmembers' time perception, their emotional states and their communication [16]. During the first 250 days, the consumption of food was determined by a physiological investigation on long-term sodium balance. After meals, the crew was asked to write every type of food they consumed. They were eager to get it done [12] with no major impact on their behavior as a result. From day 19 to day 513, the increasing occurrence of collateral acts, as stress indicator, supports therefore our first hypothesis on the major impact of time, as stress factor, on the crewmembers' behavior. This also reflects the crewmembers physical and psychological states from the first half to the second half of the mission. Even if we did not monitor the behavioral activity 24 h a day, our finding might be considered with the physical activity measured over full days [17]. Reduction of physical activity over time could be stressful. This stress is expressed through the increasing occurrence of collateral acts.

Within the two main phases of the experiment, personal actions follow 35-day cycles of changes then 70-day cycles of increasing durations while inter-personal actions follow different periods of oscillating durations. This could be explained, for instance, through systematic changes in activity levels displayed by the crewmembers across the mission [18]. Thirty five-day durations have been identified in the pilot study of the 105-day experiment as cycles in which significant changes can be detected in the individuals, caused by environmental conditions. Our previous findings in polar mission with respect to shorter cycles also argue that case [8]. That confirms the second hypothesis on the crew's non-verbal behavior with cyclic and periodic changes in social isolation and spatial

They correspond, respectively, to 6 months and one year of the Mars-500 experiment. Decremented ranges of facial expression indicate difficult sub-periods, as verbally expressed by the crewmembers, while incremented ranges after the second half of the mission reflect the well being and good spirit of the crewmembers. This is also consistent with the personal account of the crewmembers [12] who reported that after their separation for the Mars landing, the crew was not divided at all and continued to work in the same spirit. On day 355, when facial expressions were not frequent, we observed the highest duration of personal actions and the lowest duration of inter-personal actions. This supports our third hypothesis that over extended periods of time, specific temporal points appear in the crew's behavioral profile.

To summarize, a Mars mission scenario could be divided into two main phases:

Phase 1 with decreasing collective time, decreasing facial expressions and increasing collateral acts;

Specific phase of the crew separation during Mars orbiting and Mars landing (not studied herein);

Phase 2 with decreasing collective time, increasing facial expressions and increasing collateral acts.

Over the course of the mission, cyclic changes of personal actions and periodic variations of inter-personal actions occur.

---

On the basis of behavioral observations, such scenario will be helpful in determining the crewmembers feelings, in anticipating emotional states and in compensating their reluctance to express them [20]. Knowledge on cycles of behavioral changes would help in the prevention of mood changes. The limitation of ethological studies is not to overlap the objective description of the behavior but the interest is to offer features of behavioral profiles for subjective analyses of the underlying psychological profiles. They can be applied to monitor the crew's mental health. As such, they can be considered as an additional tool for psychological support programmes [21].

To conclude, from one cycle to the other, from one period to the other and from one phase to the other, the Mars-500 crew adapted to isolated and confined conditions through a dynamic behavioral profile. From the ethological point of view, we state that any behavioral change is the expression of a positive adaptation to cope with personal and interpersonal problems that may occur over the mission. The phase of crew separation at mid-mission is a relevant time point that addresses a new question of what it was going on. Our finding gives new reference values for next human adaptation studies in isolation and confinement conditions.
