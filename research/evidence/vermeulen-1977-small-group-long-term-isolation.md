# SMALL-GROUP BEHAVIOUR IN LONG-TERM ISOLATION*

L.P. VERMEULEN
Dept. of Psychology, University of Pretoria

## ABSTRACT

This study is based on the measurement of psychosocial reactions in a group of seventeen men exposed to prolonged isolation and confinement at the South African National Research station (SANAE) in Antarctica. Under conditions of restricted stimulation and activity the participants reported an increased incidence and severity of hostility, depression, insomnia and anxiety. Among the most pronounced effects of group isolation and confinement were hostility and depression.

## INTRODUCTION

The purpose of this paper is to discuss the effects of prolonged isolation and confinement upon the sociopsychological adjustment of a small group of men at the South African Antarctic research station SANAE.

SANAE is built on an ice shelf approximately 130 km from the true coastline of the Antarctic continent. The mean annual temperature at SANAE (50 m above sea level) is about -17°C. The station, being a small community located under the ice, represents a physically and socially restricted environment.

During the summer months (November to February) the SANAE personnel are actively occupied with continuous repairs, storage of supplies and the collection of scientific data. For a period of approximately eight months (March to November) the station is totally independent and isolated from the outside world. There is no way of evacuating men or receiving supplies. The only contact with the outside world is by radio communication and even this is interrupted occasionally by unfavourable weather conditions. With the advent of the Antarctic winter (May to August), a period of approximately three months of darkness, the men are forced indoors for almost all their activities, and they live and work together in virtual confinement. The group is confined to the physical boundaries of their station and to the social boundaries defined by their particular associates (Gunderson, 1966).

Prolonged isolation in a restricted environment places unusual stress upon small groups. The effects of such stresses upon human effectiveness over long periods are largely unknown. Much of the available literature in this area is of an autobiographical nature and consists of reports of explorers, castaways and prisoners-of-war. Fortunately, laboratory and field research has been conducted on small groups in isolation and confinement (Appley and Trumbull, 1967; Burns et al., 1963; Radloff and Helmreich, 1968; Rasmussen, 1973; Zubek, 1969).

It is usually assumed that fewer problems exist for small groups in isolation than for individuals in isolation. Companions seem to enrich the environment, making it more tolerable. Confined groups, however, often face difficult emotional and interpersonal problems. The following reactions to group isolation and confinement are frequently stated: (i) social withdrawal from group activities; (ii) increased display of territoriality for areas and for possessions; (iii) deterioration of group morale and general motivation; (iv) pronounced irritability, hostility and personal conflicts; and (v) emotional and psychosomatic complaints.

Research conducted by Gunderson (1963, 1968) has shown that men who "winter over" in the Antarctic become increasingly susceptible to disrupted sleep patterns, depression, hostility, anxiety and somatic symptoms. Both Gunderson (1963) and Nelson (1963) have suggested that such symptoms are likely to sap a group's motivation and hinder social adjustment.

In another study, Gunderson and Nelson (1963) discussed the development of a set of measurements for attitudes dealing with individual reactions to and satisfaction with Antarctic life and the group's efficiency and work relationship. These sets revealed a general decline in work satisfaction, social relationships and group accomplishments as a result of prolonged isolation.

Palmai (1963) has undertaken the most thorough study of small group behaviour in Antarctica. His results indicate that during the year the men tended to become generally more responsible but less cooperative and conventional. Morale was at its lowest ebb in the third quarter of the year; during this quarter the incidence of negative social and emotional feelings increased significantly, as did the incidence of somatic symptoms.

Rohrer (1961) noted a phenomenon peculiar to men in the Antarctic which he labelled the "long eye syndrome", characterized by a "twelve-foot stare in a ten foot room". The long eye syndrome has been dichotomized into two distinct behavioural phenomena, 'staring' and 'drifting' by Popkin et al. (1974). They reported that staring is a transient and episodic phenomenon that appears to be associated with leisure activity in the evening, whereas drifting may appear as an ongoing process marked by memory difficulties, changes in verbal patterns, alterations in emotional control and a reduction in both the general level of attentiveness and motivation (Popkin et. al., 1974). Both these behavioural phenomena seem to be limited to the latter part of the Antarctic year.

Finally, of particular interest were the findings of Doll and Gunderson (1970) and Gunderson (1963, 1968) that the size of the group seemed to have no relation to the incidence of emotional and somatic complaints and that improved living and working conditions have not reduced the incidence of emotional complaints at Antarctic stations. The groups observed consisted of fifteen to twenty men and of eighty to one hundred men. The assumption that "the presence of other persons seems to enrich the environment" is obviously disproved by the above studies.

In view of all the preceding investigations the primary intent of this study was to obtain measures of sociopsychological reactions indicating

# PROCEDURE

## Subjects

The subjects were 17 men assigned to the South African Antarctic station SANAE for one year during January 1975 to January 1976. All were volunteers who had been selected for Antarctic duty primarily on the basis of occupational, medical and psychological competence. The mean age of the group was 26.88 years.

## Measurement of sociopsychological reactions

The questionnaire, 'A Checklist of Feelings', included 20 reactions and symptoms which had shown prevalence during earlier Antarctic expeditions. The 'feelings' pertained to five areas viz. depression, hostility, anxiety, insomnia and somatic complaints.

The questionnaire was administered by the medical officer assigned to the group on four occasions during the year. The first testing took place at the end of the Antarctic summer (April). The second testing occurred at mid-winter (June) after two months of confinement and the third at the end of the winter after the beginning of outdoor-activities (September). The final testing was administered during the active period in early summer (November). Responses were given on a four-point scale indicating the presence and intensity of the five main reaction areas.

# RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

The data reveal a general trend toward an increased incidence of negative feelings during this period. The incidence of almost all the symptom areas were higher during the mid-winter and post-winter periods than during the pre-winter period.

The mean incidence of sociopsychological reactions and somatic complaints for all the subjects over the four time periods is presented in Figure 1.

Figure 1 indicates that the three most pronounced effects of group isolation and confinement were hostility (feeling easily annoyed or irritated, feeling critical of others, feeling short-tempered or unkind, feeling impatient with others); depression (feeling blue, feeling lonely, feeling depressed, feeling downcast); and insomnia (difficult in falling asleep, and other somnial problems such as staying asleep, waking up at night, feeling weary or drowsy).

The reported percentages for insomnia, depression and anxiety were higher after two months of confinement at mid-winter than during the pre-winter period. Hostility did not reach a high percentage until the post-winter period.

The incidence of problems connected with sleep, anxiety (feeling afraid for no apparent reason, feeling nervous and tense, feeling uneasy or worried) and somatic complaints (headaches, pains in the heart or chest, pains in lower part of back) dropped after the beginning of outdoor-

activities during the post-winter period. Reported incidence of hostility, depression and anxiety appeared relatively stable for the last two periods of isolation. The increased severity of somatic symptoms in the last period of isolation was presumably the result of the large amount of strenuous physical work performed during early summer.

Generally, the subjective sociopsychological reactions reported by the group resemble the reactions often observed under conditions of laboratory manipulation in "sensory deprivation" experiments (Zubek, 1969). Many of the same reactions have been reported in men wintering in the Arctic (Boag, 1952), and in men confined in a concentration camp (Kral, 1951). Similar symptoms have been noted under confining conditions imposed by submarine duty (Weybrew, 1963).

It was not possible to identify the particular parameters of Antarctic station life which induced the increased negative sociopsychological reactions. It may include both physical and social factors. The following agents of change were presumably responsible for the symptoms reported: (i) the absence of interesting stimulus variations; (ii) restriction in physical activity, particularly at mid-winter; (iii) reduced cognitive and affective stimulation; (iv) social deprivation; (v) enforced socialization; (vi) status leveling, (vii) and reduced gratification of the basic human needs of security, affection and feelings of personal significance.

However it seemed clear that sociopsychological disturbances tend to increase even in healthy subjects when exposed to prolonged isolation in a restricted environment.

The results gained through this study of human responses to life in isolation and confinement may have direct application in other restricted environments. The knowledge acquired gives rise to numerous questions with regard to the sociopsychological effects of prolonged exposure to other environments characterized by isolation, monotony, confinement, enforced intimacy and sensory deprivation. Such environments include prisons, mental hospitals, concentration camps and institutions.

At the same time the results indicate that behavioural scientists should remain alert to the existence and possible development of physical and social conditions that could produce pathological responses.

## SUMMARY

This study was concerned with the measurement of psychosocial reactions in a group of seventeen men who were exposed to prolonged isolation and confinement at the South African National Research station (SANAE) in Antarctica. Under conditions of restricted stimulation and activity the participants reported an increased incidence and severity of hostility, depression, insomnia and anxiety. Among the most pronounced effects of group isolation and confinement were hostility (feeling easily annoyed or irritated, feeling critical of others, feeling short-tempered or unkind); and depression (feeling blue, feeling lonely, feeling depressed).
