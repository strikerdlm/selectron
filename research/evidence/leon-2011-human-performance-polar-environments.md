# ABSTRACT

An overview of the physical, psychological, social, and coping aspects of living and working in polar regions is presented, assessing findings from both expedition teams and work groups. A personal narrative describing the experiences of a polar expeditioner is interspersed in relevant sections to provide a deeper understanding of the challenges of performing in an extreme environment. The application of findings from polar environments as an analog for space missions is discussed. We conclude that further research is needed on the integration of psychosocial and neurobehavioral adaptation. Furthermore, it is important to consider both the positive as well as negative features of living and working in polar and other extreme environments, and develop training strategies and countermeasure tools to optimize performance in these conditions.

# 1. Introduction

The challenges of human performance in cold environments has been a topic of interest since the early history of polar exploration. While the biographies of early explorers such as Nansen, Cook, Dumont d'Urville, and Shackleton consist of many tales of heroism, bravery, and self-sacrifice (see Stuster, 1996; Palinkas &amp; Suedfeld, 2008), they also recount the costs in human suffering. Fritjof Nansen, who became the first person to cross Greenland and later led the first expedition attempt to reach the North Pole, understood that the psychological as well as physical status of his team were crucial factors in the success or failure of the endeavor. As he succinctly stated, "It is the man that matters." (Nansen, 1897, as cited in Stuster, 1996, p. 302). The focus on the individual as the key element in the polar environment represents the core of polar psychology.

In addition to the empirical study of expedition teams, recent efforts have focused on understanding and preventing psychosocial and mental health problems among personnel operating in the Arctic or Antarctica. Furthermore, because polar environments have been viewed as space analogs, the findings from these isolated, confined, and challenging environments have broad application (Lugg &amp; Shepanek, 1999). This paper presents an overview of such research conducted over the last few decades, examining findings on non-indigenous personnel temporarily performing in environments and situations quite different from their usual lives.

During the "heroic age" of polar exploration (~1895-1922), information on human performance in Antarctic regions was obtained primarily from the diaries and books written by expeditioners. Systematic research on polar expedition and work groups commenced in the 20th century during the 1956–1957 International Geophysical Year (Palinkas, 2003). However, in conducting empirical research, the voice of the expeditioner is often lost, although the challenges of human performance in polar environments can be informed by consideration of both personal experiences and the research literature.

In this paper, one of the authors (EL) recounts his experiences and observations leading up to and during two summer two-man expeditions to the North Pole, in 2005 and 2006. He was 33 years old at the time of the first North Pole trek, which was terminated after several weeks because of poor ice conditions and severe weather. In the second attempt, the teammates reached the North Pole but again encountered poor ice conditions and did not continue on to Greenland, their original objective. His narrative is interspersed in relevant sections of the paper to provide a deeper understanding of the normative research findings.

For nearly a month, my expedition team dog sledded through an area equal to the size of the state of Montana. It was an unending vastness of snow and ice that we had all to ourselves. The above excerpt attests to the fact that people are drawn to polar environments for multiple reasons, including the beauty as well as the challenge of the environment, and the strong attachment to the place that can form during time spent in polar regions. Similarly, the activities of non-indigenous people in polar environments have included expedition teams striving to attain a particular goal such as being the first to traverse an entire continent, civilian work groups conducting scientific research and establishing or maintaining a national presence, and military groups training in the Arctic. Polar work groups are active during both the summer and the dark winter-over period, although there is a significant downsizing of personnel during the winter. Currently, human presence in polar areas has become safer and more commonplace than in the “heroic age” of exploration through the availability of advanced technology such as the Internet. However, the living and work conditions still involve a number of potential physiological and psychosocial stressors.

### Types of groups

The environmental challenges in polar regions are different, depending on the tasks and goals of the particular group. Polar expeditions are the modern-day equivalent of the polar trek of the heroic era. Teams must cope with the severe cold and wind conditions while setting up tents and preparing food at the end of the day's trek, tending to equipment and possibly sled dogs, dealing with physical strains or injuries, and perhaps, communicating with the outside world. Participants of polar treks and traverses and, to a lesser extent, polar camps are also exposed to a number of hazards such as crevasses, blizzards, slippery ice, frozen lakes, and open leads (i.e. a channel of water through a field or flow of ice), which increases the likelihood of accidental injury and death (Palinkas & Suedfeld, 2008). A second type of polar activity involves personnel who spend a period of time at a polar research station. These polar work groups experience a different kind of environment than those on polar treks because most live in permanent housing or shelters, and there is substantially less physical exertion in the cold. In the stations and camps, there are a large number of other personnel in the summer and a relatively large number in winter as well, compared to the usual 2--4 expedition trekkers. For example, at the U.S. McMurdo station in Antarctica, there are over 1000 personnel in the summer and approximately 250 in the winter. However, a winter-over crew on a small station can consist of only 10 people, and a summer camp with personnel living in tents, Quonset huts, or permanent shelters may consist of only three people.

Expedition teams have varied in composition, in terms of mixed or single gender, and national or international teams. Because the team is smaller than is the case of personnel at work stations, this leads to a higher level of interdependence among participants. For a number of years, many polar work stations, each typically run by a single nation, have included both men and women. The Concordia station, run jointly by Italy and France, is an exception to the national structure.

In addition to traversing a specific area to reach a particular goal, expedition activities can include scientific studies such as measuring snow content, ice levels, pollution effects, or changes in the lives of indigenous populations as a result of global warming. A number of expeditions have a web-based, sometimes interactive, instruction component geared toward teaching school children about polar environments, and/or presenting a daily log of activities that is also targeted to adult audiences. At polar work stations, the personnel, their backgrounds, and work tasks are more varied, ranging from mechanics, cooks, scientists, physicians, surveillance, etc. In the Arctic regions of each country, there are also activities by military groups engaged in training in severe cold environments.

The commonality of polar environments is the severe cold and wind conditions, frequent blizzards, low humidity, total darkness during the winter period, and physical isolation from the rest of the world which makes rescue and evacuation highly difficult or impossible during the winter months, depending on the location.

## Environmental challenges

There are different levels of danger associated with any extreme situation. In the Arctic, the cold is a constant threat. So are white outs, polar bears, shifting ice and just about everything else. In fact, the key to dealing with any dangerous situation is relatively easy. First and foremost, it is important to simply avoid the hazard and minimize the risk. That may seem especially difficult in the context of such great risk.

Knowing yourself both physically and mentally, establishing an efficient daily routine, setting and achieving specific goals, breaking up larger problems in to smaller ones, relying on the support of others all increase your ability to survive. If these systems are second nature, you will tend to stay on the safe side of most situations.

My first polar trek - Day 42 on the ice: I have learned by first hand experience how physically and psychologically demanding polar expeditions can be. Up ahead the Arctic Ocean sea ice roughens into a jumble of rafted pressure, cracks and open water and I am forced to veer from our northward bearing. I recheck my compass. The needle spins for a while then settles. For the past 100 yards, I've been traveling southeast - the exact opposite direction we need to be traveling. It's my last shift skiing in lead and the last hour of the day. Physically, I'm exhausted but mentally I am stretched to my breaking point.

My head falls and my shoulders sag. My expedition partner and I have been inching our way over ice and water for over a month now, trying to become the first ever expedition in history to reach the North Pole in summer. It hasn't been easy. The farther north we've traveled, the more severe the conditions. Large sections of open water (leads), thin ice, soft snow, pressure ridges and white outs have sapped any initial optimism. Nearly gone is any aspiration of the North Pole. Now, we measure success in hour and a half increments.

I parallel a five-foot wide crack hoping to find a spot where the ice narrows enough to cross. Suddenly, my sled-canoe slips, falls off the ice ledge, and then pulls me backwards with enough force to snap me out of my ski bindings. I fall back in soft wet snow and flounder for a full minute trying to get back up. A silly mistake. Had I slipped a few feet to my right, I would have fallen into an icy slushy ocean. What then? Hypothermia at best. I try to reflect on my apparent luck, but all I can muster is a perfunctory glance at my watch. Forty minutes left in the day.

Later in the tent I wearily page through a small dictionary to pick out a word to describe my day. “Brinkmanship,” I find. “Pursuing a course to the point of disaster.” Just another typical day on an expedition, I think.

Clearly, physical factors of the environment such as the cold and wind, low humidity, winter darkness, and physical isolation have a considerable influence on human performance. Severe cold significantly affects motor and cognitive performance. Physiological processes related to heat loss/conservation are also highly influential because different body mechanisms function to maintain thermal balance. When there is a decrease in temperature on the periphery (fingers, hands) and other areas of the skin surface, the

---

body's response to cold is to conserve heat (Burton & Edholm, 1955; Koscheyev, 1981). Vasoconstriction of the blood vessels of the fingers is the initial response, in conjunction with a shift of blood to the body core as a self-preservation mechanism. Shivering, increased urination, dehydration, and frostbite all occur at different levels of heat loss from the body.

Hypothermia is always a consideration when individuals are exposed to severe cold conditions for prolonged periods of time. Humans are in thermal balance when core body temperature is maintained at approximately 95--96.8 °F (35--36 °C). In mild hypothermia, core temperature drops by approximately 1.8--3.6 °F (1--2 °C), and is accompanied by mild to strong shivering and numbness of the hands (Burton & Edholm, 1955). In moderate hypothermia, there is a 3.8--7.6 °F (2--4 °C) drop in core body temperature, and shivering becomes more intense. Body movements become sluggish, and there is a decline in muscle coordination. In severe hypothermia, core body temperature drops below approximately 89.6 °F (32 °C), shivering ceases, and motor and cognitive function are severely affected. Unless immediate rewarming occurs, major organ failure ensues, resulting in death.

In the cold, impairments in finger dexterity and muscle strength result in performance degradation, noted particularly in carrying out fine motor tasks (Phetteplace, 2000). The effects on cognitive performance have been shown to be both negative and positive. Negative effects include increased response time and decreased accuracy and efficiency, likely due to the distracting influence of the cold (Mäkinen et al. 2006). The cold also serves as an arousal mechanism, enhancing performance on some cognitive tasks. However, as the body suffers from prolonged time in the cold, the metabolic cost of the physiological mechanism of thermogenesis (heat production), such as shivering, results in increased fatigue and eventual debilitation. Over time, the effects of a combination of physiological and deteriorating cognitive factors can impair judgment. For example, the disastrous decision by Robert Scott to push on to the South Pole when supplies were dangerously short prior to reaching the next supply depot, with several of the men injured and in poor physical condition, was likely a combination of ambition, fatigue, malnutrition, and the generally debilitating effects of the severe cold (Huxley, 1977).

Wind and low humidity are additional environmental factors that have a significant detrimental impact on physiological processes and performance (Armstrong, 2000). The term wind chill refers to the combination of low air temperature and the movement of air across the skin; these factors greatly accelerate heat loss from the body. Low humidity or dry air combined with the cold is also an environmental stressor, affecting the lungs and bronchial airways, and making breathing difficult, particularly during physical exertion.

The bulky clothing personnel need to wear to protect against extreme cold and wind is another factor affecting human performance. Dexterity and motor flexibility are impaired due to the necessity of wearing highly insulated clothing ensembles.

### Darkness

The winter-over period of total darkness has a significant effect on sleep and overall well-being. Light intensity has been associated with suppression of melatonin, affecting physiological rhythms related to arousal, energy, mood, and cognitive performance (Lewy, Wehr, Goodwin, Newsome, & Markey, 1980) Studies of polar work personnel have demonstrated that the normal circadian sleep rhythm often is disrupted, resulting in difficulties in falling asleep, less deep sleep and dreaming, periodic awakening during the sleep cycle, and in some cases, seasonal affective disorder (Bhargava, Mukerji, & Sachdeva, 2000; Natani, Shurley, Pierce, & Brooks, 1970; Palinkas, Houseal, & Rosenthal, 1996). For example, a survey by Palinkas (1992) found that 64.1% of personnel at McMurdo Station in Antarctica reported some sleep difficulties in winter. During the long winter-over period in Antarctica, personnel have also reported increases in depressive mood, lethargy, somatic complaints, interpersonal conflicts, and a decrement in work performance (see Sandal, Leon, & Palinkas, 2006 for an overview). According to Palinkas and Suedfeld (2008), these problems can be attributed primarily to the absence or reduction of daylight during winter in conjunction with cold and psychosocial stress. Yet, it should be noted that other studies have found no evidence of these symptoms (e.g. Palinkas & Houseal, 2000).

Rohrer (1961) described a transient staring behavior observed in some Antarctic personnel, which he termed the “long eye”, or a “twelve-foot stare in a ten-foot room.” Staring when alone and with others occurred after the onset of the isolation and darkness of the winter, and was associated in some individuals with subjective reports of depression (Popkin, Stillner, Osborn, Pierce, & Shurley, 1974). It is possible that the staring behavior is due to a combination of sleep deprivation and boredom, along with the social isolation of a small group setting. A process of “drifting”, i.e. the experience of being dissociated from the ongoing flow of events, was also described and viewed as possibly associated with increased alcohol use. However, there are obvious individual differences in sensitivity to the darkness during the winter-over. As one participant commented, she felt like she was in a cave and she loved it (Rothblum, Weinstock, & Morris, 1998).

### Physical isolation

The experience of physical isolation has both positive and negative aspects. For many polar personnel, the possibility of spending time away from the time pressures and other stressors of their usual existence is a very attractive feature of their current environment. One participant commented that she wanted to go to Antarctica because she was attracted by the barrenness and remoteness from civilization, thus avoiding distractions and everyday stress (Rothblum et al. 1998). On the other hand, many find the separation from family and friends to be increasingly difficult. Feelings of guilt and ambivalence may arise in case of illness or death of loved ones occurring while the sojourner is in the polar region.

Access at present to the Internet and satellite telephones has both positive and negative features. Personnel are now able to keep in contact with and updated on events at home, thus lessening the psychological isolation; however, feelings of helplessness or responsibility to intervene in some way if problems occur may also result. Clearly, each individual is in a different situation, and what may be a benefit for one person may be a negative feature for another.

## Psychological factors

### Time phases

Participants who described their experiences during polar treks or at research stations often emphasized how their adaptation changed over time. Observations of winter-over groups on Antarctic research stations suggested that decrements in mood and the quality of social interactions were likely to occur during the third quarter, when personnel begin to think about the time remaining before returning to their usual environment (Bechtel & Berning, 1991; Rohrer, 1961; Stuster, Bachelard, & Suedfeld, 2000).

However, empirical findings are inconsistent in regard to the existence of a specific third-quarter critical phase (Palinkas &

---

Houseal, 2000). Sandal (2000) found that land-based personnel who operated under harsh and unpredictable conditions showed a decline in optimism and well-being and a rise in anger at approximately the third quarter; however, scientists living in more comfortable conditions on a ship showed high levels of adaptation during the entire period. A third-quarter increase in need to associate with other personnel although at a lesser level of intimacy has been reported (Paul, Mandal, Ramachandran, & Panwar, 2010).

Other studies of polar expeditions have shown increases in negative behaviors in the first quarter (Sandal, Bergan, Warncke, Vaernes, & Ursin); a decline in both negative and positive mood and negative events during the winter-over midpoint (Leon, Atlis, Ones, & Magor, 2002); a prevalence of positive over negative mood across the entire trek (Atlis, Leon, Sandal, & Infante, 2004, Kahn, & Leon, 1994, Leon, 1991, Leon, Kanfer, Hoffman, & Dupre, 1991, Leon, McNally, & Ben-Porath, 1989); and an increase in positive mood over the course of a short expedition (Palinkas, Suedfeld, & Steel, 1995).

Clearly, the data do not support the thesis that there is a predictable time period in all polar settings characterized by negative mood and conflicts. It is possible that individual differences in personality in members of a particular group, the cohesiveness of the group, leadership styles, and environmental factors differentially influence the emergence and timing of mood changes and social tensions.

### Salutogenesis

Sojourns in polar regions have significant positive effects for many individuals. Suedfeld and Steel (2000) highlighted the numerous positive features of living and working in an isolated and confined environment, and referred to Antonovsky's concept (1979) of “salutogenesis”, that is, a sense of positive growth and meaning in one's life. Palinkas and Suedfeld (2008) have suggested that these positive effects can be divided into two major categories: (a) the inherently pleasurable aspects of the situation, and (b) the positive reactions to having encountered and successfully surmounted the challenges of the environment. Whereas most research conducted in polar environments has focused on negative reactions and consequences, this orientation directs attention to the favorable experiences and characteristics of the situation. Many participants choose to return to Antarctica over a number of seasons, and experience personal growth and transformations of their personal values as a result of their polar experience.

### Personality

The personality traits of individuals participating in expedition teams and in polar work environments have received considerable attention. Evaluation of the first documented group to reach the North Pole without resupply indicated that for each of the six team members, the personality traits that were predominant were achievement motivation and low stress reactivity (Leon et al. 1989). Scores on a scale measuring the tendency to take risks was at an average level, compared to population norms. The combination of high achievement motivation and low stress reactivity was replicated in studies of a number of other expedition teams, both mixed gender and single gender (Atlis et al. 2004, Kahn, & Leon, 1994, Leon et al. 1991, Leon, & Scheib, 2002, Leon, & Scheib, 2007).

Of interest, the majority of expeditioners evaluated in the above studies scored relatively high on the personality trait of absorption, i.e. the tendency to become so engrossed or focused on particular perceptions or thoughts that he or she does not pay attention to other ongoing events or experiences. In post-expedition interviews, descriptions by team members reflective of this trait centered on becoming so immersed in the beauty of the environment that he/she did not pay attention to fatigue or physical pain, and time seemed to pass quickly. This state of mind, characterized by an energized immersion and narrowing of attention while one is coping with a challenging task, has sometimes been labeled “flow” (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990). The phrase “being at one with things” is another metaphor of this state.

The “sensed presence” phenomenon (Suedfeld & Mocellin, 1987) may reflect a personality characteristic that is a component of the absorption trait; i.e. becoming so engrossed in cognitive processes that thoughts appear as reality. Several expeditioners reported the feeling of a benign person or power looking over them. In one example, an expeditioner indicated that as she was skiing across difficult terrain and feeling extremely fatigued, she felt her deceased grandmother looking over her shoulder and urging her to continue on (Atlis et al. 2004). Another commented that she thought there were spirits on the continent who were taking care of them (Rothblum et al. 1998).

Studies of polar work groups using a variety of personality measures have found characteristics of openness to experience (perhaps similar to absorption) (Grant et al. 2007, Steel, Suedfeld, Peri, & Palinkas, 1997) and high stress resistance (Weiss, Suedfeld, Steel, & Tanaka, 2000). Antarctic scientists had high scores on achievement orientation, low anxiety, and low somatic preoccupation (Butcher & Ryan, 1974). Antarctic technical personnel indicated narrow interests and a low need for stimulation (Biersner & Hogan, 1984), characteristics that are highly adaptive in the confinement and isolation of polar work stations. Similarly, a prospective study of Antarctic winter-over personnel that included workers in a range of occupations found that those rated highest in performance had low scores on measures of neuroticism, boredom, need for order, affection from others, and achievement motivation (Palinkas, Gunderson, Johnson, & Holland, 2000). In addition, polar workers judged to be exceptionally well adapted scored relatively higher on the NEO-FFI Openness to Experience and Emotion-Focused Coping than did workers rated as poorly adapted (Grant et al. 2007). Suedfeld and Steel (2000) proposed that in general, traits of high conscientiousness and agreeableness would be optimal for adaptive functioning in isolated and confined environments.

Using the Personality Characteristics Inventory (PCI), studies have focused on identifying the optimal personality profile of members of aircrews and astronauts (Chidester, Helmreich, Gregorich, & Craig; 1991, Musson, Sandal, & Helmreich, 2004). The profile that has sometimes been referred to as the “right stuff”, predictive of performance and coping in a number of different environments, has been characterized by a combination of high positive expressiveness and high positive instrumentality (Sandal, Bergan, Warncke, Vaernes, & Ursin, 1996, Sandal, Endresen, Vaernes, & Ursin, 1999, Sandal et al. 1998). Assessment of the personality characteristics of members from six different polar expeditions indicated that the majority of those evaluated exhibited a profile consistent with the “right stuff” profile (Sandal et al. 1996). These results suggest that many individuals who volunteer to participate in polar expedition teams or are preferred teammates on such expeditions possess personality traits reflective of interpersonal sensitivity, in addition to motivation and physical ability.

Personnel in polar work group settings also need to be socially skilled, and traits that might be adaptive in one situation and with a particular group might not be adaptive in other groups or situations. Clearly, there are individual differences among polar personnel as in expedition teams, not only in personality, but in age, gender, education, occupational level, and nationality. Therefore, one cannot expect that there will be consistent findings across studies on personality and behavior, or that the same characteristics would be adaptive in different environments.

Social factors

My partner and I had planned for our North Pole trip for almost four years. In that time, we trained together nearly every day, traveled the country on fundraising tours and much more. Throughout those experiences, we gained a better understanding of each other's personality quirks and foibles. In my opinion, it was our interactions prior to starting the expedition that allowed us to be successful partners on the ice.

What ended up being the most stressful interpersonal experience on our journey was more a philosophical issue than anything else. The style in which we did things, our personal view of the world and how we presented our selves continue to strain our relationship to this day. For my partner and me these fundamental differences in our personalities manifested themselves ultimately in our physical struggle to get to the Pole and back. At a certain point in the expedition, my partner (as leader) deemed that the expedition would not be able to complete our goal of making a return journey from the Pole back to land. As the second in charge of two people, I was able to give my opinion but felt that it was of little use as the decision was already made. In the end, I simply had to come to terms with a decision that I didn't agree with.

The understanding among teammates as to who holds decision authority is crucial in avoiding major confrontations on the ice. Furthermore, acceptance of each other's personal characteristics promotes bonding, which is particularly important because of the teammates' high interdependence. Sensitivity toward the needs of others and a positive group climate facilitate individual adaptation. One member of an expedition commented on how teammates tried to support each other despite considerable language problems, writing in her diary that: “She was easy to understand. When it was one of those days when her energy was really low, we sang Stenka Rasina, the Russian song she had taught us. After the expedition, she explained to us how much it had meant to her” (Leon & Sandal, 2003; p. 263).

Group size, composition, experience, and culture all have an influence on interpersonal processes and the cohesiveness of the group. Group cohesion has been shown to vary from group to group (Palinkas, 1989), and across national stations (Palinkas et al. 2004). In a French Antarctic station, younger scientists expressed a greater need for privacy while technicians preferred the social leisure area (Weiss, Feliot-Rippeault, & Gaud, 2007). While studies of Antarctic groups found that the greatest source of stress was interpersonal conflicts and tension (Stuster et al. 2000), overall interpersonal conflicts and negative experiences may be relatively low (Wood, Hysong, Lugg, & Harm, 2000). Sarris and Kirby (2007) surveyed returned Australian Antarctic personnel and found that ratings of the overall profile of their station reflected a satisfaction-oriented culture. The majority viewed the station as open and friendly, although the small minority of women were more likely to refer to conformity, rules, and pressures to fit in. An analysis of the male sample found that those who described the station as exhibiting a satisfaction-oriented culture reported less role conflict in their work role (Sarris, 2007).

The literature generally indicates that single-gender compared to mixed-gender groups function in different ways. While all-male expedition teams tend to exhibit a high degree of competitiveness with each other and little sharing of personal concerns (Bishop, Grobler, & Schjøll, 2001; Koscheyev, Lartzev, Rode, & Malakhov, 1992; Poles Apart, 2000), a recent study found that interactions were predominantly cooperative and there was a marked concern for each other's welfare (Leon, Sandal, Fink, & Ciofani, 2011). Women in mixed-gender and all-women's groups seem to assume a more nurturing role, and reported that the greatest source of stress was concern about the welfare of other group members (Atlis et al. 2004; Kahn & Leon, 1994; Leon et al. 1989; Leon & Sandal, 2003; Rothblum et al. 1998). Moreover, women in mixed-gender groups often assumed the role of “peacemaker” and avoided direct involvement in conflicts among the men in the group (Leon, List, & Magor, 2004; Sandal, Vaernes, & Ursin, 1995). The inclusion of women in winter-over groups on Antarctic stations was judged to have a positive influence on interpersonal relationships in terms of reducing rude behavior (Rosnet, Jurion, Cazes, & Bachelard, 2004). However, there was an increase in stress related to rivalry, sexual harassment, and frustration.

Cultural factors also play a role in the relationships between men and women in isolated and confined environments. For example, a study of a mixed-gender Soviet and American Bering Bridge expedition team found that Soviet men expedition members viewed their American men teammates as being insensitive to the differences in strength and thus the physical demands placed on the women on the team (Leon, Kanfer, Hoffman, & Dupre, 1994). However, the American women reported feeling equal to the men and viewed the Soviet men's solicitousness as patronizing.

The leadership of the group is highly important in maintaining task focus and group cohesion. Evaluations of polar expeditions indicated that task leadership was most important in the beginning stages of the trek, while supportive leadership was of greater importance in later stages (Stuster, 1996). Group members perceived the best leader as being a role model, interested in the advice of subordinates when appropriate, sensitive to the needs of crew members, and clearly communicating roles and responsibilities (Nicholas & Penwell, 1995; Stuster, 2005). Still, cross-cultural research in other settings suggests that people from different cultures may have different prototypes or “mental pictures” of what efficient leadership entails. Unless these issues are articulated and dealt with, such differences may result in frustration and tension both for the leaders and the other participants.

## Coping strategies

During my 2006 expedition to the North Pole, easily the biggest stress was traveling through increasingly worsening conditions. While we had initially assumed that ice would improve the closer we got the pole, the opposite was true. We continually encountered a puzzle of small pans, open water, thin ice and unstable brush ice. Every new vista unfolded into a fresh series of problems to negotiate.

Compounding our growing worry was the dilemma of traveling across a relatively featureless terrain (much of the time in complete whiteout). Each day was eerily like the next. Wake up, eat breakfast, pack up, ski all day, set up camp, eat, go to sleep, ad infinitum... So little distinction between moments added to the futility of our endeavor. It was difficult, at times, to find the motivation to keep moving forward.

Neither my partner nor I could cope with the physical (breaking trail) or mental (choosing an efficient safe route) strain of skiing in lead position all day. Our first easy solution was to break the day up into thirds then divide each third in half. That simple division meant that during a typical travel day, my partner would travel in lead for nearly five hours and I would travel in lead the same amount of time. Calculated further, we would switch lead skiers every hour and a half with every three hours bringing the relief of a ‘sit down' break and a slight 10-minute rest.

Working hard for a specific amount of time, then having a break allowed for manageable amounts of stress. When skiing in second, we also relinquished decision making control to the lead, further eliminating unnecessary anxiety. This proved so effective that we often referred to our hour and a half stints in second position as ‘vacation'. Eventually, I realized that I didn't need to ski all the way to the pole, only half. My partner would ski the rest.

---

The strategies that expedition members use in coping with different challenges during expeditions can have a significant influence on their ability to maintain health and well-being over time. In the general literature dealing with stress and coping, a distinction is often made between problem-focused and emotion-focused strategies (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984). Whereas problem-focused coping refers to dealing with the situation directly, emotion-focused coping involves reinterpreting or redefining a stressful situation. The above excerpt demonstrates that a core characteristic of individuals who participate in polar expeditions is their active approach in dealing with challenges, and the positive results that often ensue. While this attitude is highly important for the success of the expedition (Leon et al. 1989), it also seems essential to be able to engage in more emotionally oriented strategies. Recognizing and accepting personal feelings may be particularly important in situations where the stressor is chronic or not under personal control; for example, in the case of injuries or difficult weather conditions during treks, or the constant darkness of the Antarctic winter.

In reports from several expeditions, a number of team members felt that cognitive strategies were an effective means of overcoming declines in motivation, in particular around the midpoint of the trek. One expedition member commented that she built things in her mind- she built a lot in wood. She always seemed to be constructing something that seemed to keep her busy because she could then be absorbed with the details (Rothblum et al. 1998). The same expeditioner indicated that the most difficult days were the ones when she had a hard time thinking of anything and her mind felt numb. When that happened, she would simply focus on her breathing.

Sharing one's emotional feelings with others is another means of coping with difficult situations, although with mixed results in terms of interpersonal interactions. Individuals often seem to adapt to extreme environments by refraining from relying on their fellow team members for support, primarily because the others are facing the same stressors (Palinkas, 1992). Many expeditioners have expressed a concern that they not be seen as a burden to the others, for example, after being injured. Relevant to this point, seeking social support as a coping mechanism was related to poor psychological adjustment during polar treks (Leon et al. 1991) and long-duration submarine missions (Sandal et al. 1998). While the psychological literature suggests that seeking social support is a positive means of coping, these behaviors may have a negative impact on relations with others if the support initiatives are more reflective of or interpreted as an expression of dependency needs. An expedition leader indicated her frustration when one of her teammates repeatedly asked about the distance they had left to traverse on that day (Leon & Sandal, 2003). Another said that she felt overburdened because others kept coming to her with their problems, while at the same time, she felt that trying to perform well on her own tasks was a hard enough job (Rothblum et al. 1998).

Of interest, participants have also commented that completing psychological questionnaires assessing how they dealt with stressors in different situations helped diffuse some of the stress that built up. Perhaps the focus on and documentation of stressors provided an opportunity to communicate various concerns and feel that “someone out there” had an interest in how they were doing, without overburdening their teammates.

### Lessons learned about performing/functioning in the best way possible

In my opinion exceptional human performance cannot be attributed to one main characteristic. There are natural athletes and super intelligent people, but I am neither. Yet, I was able to complete a fairly formidable journey. In the end, I feel that the ability to push one's self beyond the norm is more mental than anything else. While extreme human performance may ultimately rest in the power of the mind, it can only happen within the confines of one's physical ability. Knowing your body, having it be well trained, and understanding how your muscles will react when stretched to their breaking point is critical. Pushing your body beyond the limits of its physical ability is about using your mind to discover systems that help cope.

This excerpt is a powerful illustration of a key element of expedition polar psychology: the influence of strong achievement motivation on the capacity of the human body to go beyond usual physical limits.

## Research challenges

Despite advances in our understanding of the human challenges in polar environments, a number of issues regarding psychological functioning require attention, particularly the integration of psychosocial and neurobehavioral adaptation. These two domains are usually examined separately and considered individually as risk factors for poor performance (Health Sciences Policy Board; 2005). Nevertheless, both are interrelated. For example, changes in thyroid and catecholamine function in polar environments have been linked to poor mood and impaired cognitive performance (Reed et al. 2001). Similarly, stress and mood are believed to influence endocrine, immune, and cardiovascular function. Ultimately, solutions to human challenges in these environments will require multidisciplinary efforts.

Aggregation of data from the Australian National Antarctic Research Expeditions (ANARE) collected over a 25 year period indicated a rate of morbidity for mental health disorders below 4% (Lugg, 2005). This incidence is lower than in the general population. However, given the problem of evacuation from polar stations during the winter, there is a need for additional research on personnel selection to more effectively identify individuals at risk for developing psychological problems and psychiatric disorders. Further research to identify the factors associated with the emergence of negative mood and conflicts in isolated and confined groups is also warranted.

Preventive countermeasures or interventions are highly important, and include training in interpersonal relations and social dynamics, leadership skills, individual coping strategies, and more effective and less bulky protective clothing to deal with the severe cold. Moreover, research is required to facilitate the positive aspects of living and working in polar environments. It is still unclear whether people can be trained to experience such benefits, or whether this positive orientation is an inherent feature of particular personality types.

Intrapersonal processes, interpersonal relationships, and work performance in polar regions have been viewed as an analog to inform about the types of interactions that might occur on space missions. In fact, there are a number of commonalities as well as differences in comparing findings from studies of polar, chamber simulation, and short and long-duration space mission groups (Sandal et al. 2006). With international planning in progress for extended planetary exploration missions, it is important to continue extrapolating information from analog groups. Although each group is small in number, a positive feature of the study of polar expedition and small work groups is the aggregation over time of a larger number of participants. This will provide more robust statistical power in evaluating findings, and greater ease in maintaining confidentiality than with the limited number of space sojourners. In particular, the interactions and decision making processes of expedition teams provide an attractive analog for

---

anticipating some of the positive and negative relationships that might occur in exploring planetary surfaces.

Furthermore, the information gained from the larger number of personnel functioning in polar work groups provides the opportunity to analyze in greater depth the mix of personality, leadership styles, and environmental impact of extreme environments on work performance and adaptive group cohesion. Through the cooperation of groups from different countries, as well as the inclusion of a greater number of women and members of ethnic minorities, it will be possible to gain a better understanding of the interaction of gender, culture, and occupational status on human performance in extreme environments. This information can be applied to improve the psychological and work functioning of future long-duration space crews.

## 8. Conclusions

Both environmental conditions and human relationships influence performance in polar regions. In the scientific and popular literature, polar activities have been considered primarily in terms of their negative impact. Comprehensive studies of winter-over polar work groups demonstrate that many sojourners experience decrements in mood, sleep deprivation, problems in concentration, interpersonal tensions, and a decline in the quality of their work performance. However, the many positive aspects of living and working in an extreme environment for an extended period of time are often ignored. The personal growth aspects associated with successfully meeting different types of challenges can result in greater self-confidence and an enhancement of work performance. Therefore, it is important to consider both aspects of living and working in polar regions and other extreme environments such as space, and develop training strategies and countermeasure tools to optimize performance in these conditions.
