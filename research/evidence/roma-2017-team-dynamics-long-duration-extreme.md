# KEY FACTORS AND THREATS TO TEAM DYNAMICS IN LONG-DURATION EXTREME ENVIRONMENTS

Peter G. Roma and Wendy L. Bedwell

# ABSTRACT

Purpose — To better understand contributing factors and mediating mechanisms related to team dynamics in isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE) environments.

Methodology/approach — Literature review.

Findings — Our primary focus is on cohesion and adaptation — two critical aspects of team performance in ICE environments that have received increased attention in both the literature and funding initiatives. We begin by describing the conditions that define ICE environments and review relevant individual biological, neuropsychiatric, and environmental factors that interact with team dynamics. We then outline a unifying team cohesion framework for long-duration missions and discuss several environmental, operational, organizational, and psychosocial factors that can impact team dynamics. Finally, we end with a discussion of directions for future research and countermeasure development, emphasizing the importance of temporal dynamics, multidisciplinary integration, and novel conceptual frameworks for the inherently mixed work and social setting of long-duration missions in ICE environments.

Social implications — A better understanding of team dynamics over time can contribute to success in a variety of organizational settings, including space exploration, defense and security, business, education, athletics, and social relationships.“Originality/value — We promote a multidisciplinary approach to team dynamics in ICE environments that incorporates dynamic biological, behavioral, psychological, and organizational factors over time. Keywords: Team cohesion; extreme environments; biopsychosocial factors; temporal dynamics; key factors and threats to team dynamics in long‐duration extreme environments”

In this chapter, we provide a non‐exhaustive review of various contributing factors and mediating mechanisms related to team dynamics in long‐duration extreme environments. Our specific focus, at the team level, is on cohesion and adaptation — two critical aspects of team performance in such environments that have received increased attention in both the literature and funding initiatives. We begin by describing the conditions that define isolated, confined, and extreme (ICE) environments and briefly review relevant individual biological, neuropsychiatric, and environmental factors that interact with team dynamics in ICE environments. We then outline a unifying team cohesion framework for long‐duration missions and discuss several environmental, operational, organizational, and psychosocial factors that can impact team dynamics. Finally, we end with a discussion of directions for future research and countermeasure development, emphasizing the importance of temporal dynamics, multidisciplinary integration, and novel conceptual frameworks for the inherently mixed work and social setting of long‐duration missions in ICE environments.

## ISOLATED, CONFINED, AND EXTREME (ICE) ENVIRONMENTS

Long‐duration mission‐oriented operations in ICE environments, such as space exploration, military and security deployments, and remote work sites, pose a wide variety of behavioral health and performance risks. Among them, as recognized by NASA and most operational communities, are certain “team” risks that interact with individual neurobiological, behavioral health, and psychiatric risk factors, and are typically discussed within the broad psychosocial construct

---

of group “cohesion” (Landon, Vessey, & Barrett, 2015; Schmidt, Keeton, Slack, Leveton, & Shea, 2010). Before considering any factors and threats to team dynamics in long-duration extreme environments, we must first clarify and define exactly what we mean by “extreme.” A common phrase used in the operational community, particularly in space exploration, is “isolated, confined, and extreme” (ICE) environments, which we use henceforth. For our purposes, space certainly qualifies as the prototypical ICE environment, but we also include military and security deployments, remote work sites, and other long-duration team-based work settings. We limit our discussion of ICE environments to operational settings involving some combination of personal choice and professional assignment rather than the extreme but exceptional scenarios of forced captivity such as criminal incarceration, kidnaping, hostage-taking, and political or war imprisonment (see Leach, 2016).

Isolation in operational settings can take several forms, but is mostly understood as the physical separation from civilization, such as working and living in a remote location, outpost, military installation, basecamp, or transport vessel (e.g. submarine, spacecraft) at a distance that is either impractically or impossibly far from the crew members' homes to permit a standard workday commute with separate locations and people for professional and personal lives. However, isolation is not limited to physical proximity, as operational environments can have inherent or required constraints on external communications. For example, military craft and installments often operate on restricted or otherwise secure networks with limited accommodations for civilian communications. For future space exploration crews, all communications between Earth and Mars for example are subject to an approximately 20-minute delay each way due to the distance the signals must travel. Taken together, physical separation combined with limited or lack of ability to communicate in real time with social support systems can amplify feelings of isolation.

The “confined” component of ICE environments refers to both the nature of the habitat as well as the social environment. Operations in ICE settings rely on constructed environments, and as such are designed with a focus on operational goals and basic life support, but still subject to budget and logistical constraints that can potentially compromise team performance and social dynamics. Some of the physical confinement issues relevant to team dynamics in operational settings include inadequate privacy, limited space for both work and social activities, and a lack of configurability (Kearney, 2016). Even with the most accommodating and luxurious habitat design, teams that work and live in isolation surrounded by an otherwise hostile environment (e.g. polar, sea-bound, space) are largely confined to the habitat, or at least subject to permissions and procedures for utilizing resources outside scheduled work activities. In everyday terms, one cannot spontaneously step out for some fresh air or go for a walk to get away from it all in a true ICE environment. Similarly, confinement is not limited to the physical environment, as the team members isolated from the rest of the world physically and in communications are also socially confined to

---

each other as the primary -- if not only -- source of collegiality, friendship, affection, and social support for the duration of the mission.

As suggested earlier, the “extreme” portion of ICE typically refers to a dangerous external geophysical environment incompatible with human physiology, health, and well‐being, including the lack of or toxic atmosphere, extreme altitude (above or below sea level), extreme heat or cold (or rapid shifts between the two), non‐24‐hour light‐dark cycles, reduced gravity, wildlife threats (e.g. predatory animals, microorganisms, toxins), or potential exposure to radiation and extreme weather phenomena (e.g. solar flares, high winds, dust storms, rough seas, blizzards, volcanism). However, even without imminent physical danger from the environment surrounding the habitat, isolation and confinement themselves can make an operational environment extreme. This is particularly true considering perhaps the most critical overarching contributor to the extremity of ICE environments with potential impact on team dynamics: time itself. For long‐duration missions on the order of months and years, biological and psychosocial stress effects can accumulate, and many of the factors relevant to teamwork and social dynamics become increasingly interactive, salient, and potentially threating to mission success over time.

## Individual Biological, Neurobehavioral, and Psychiatric Factors Contributing to Team Dynamics in ICE Environments

Before discussing the role of cohesion and adaptation, as well as factors and threats to team dynamics, we first consider some of the individual biological, neurobehavioral, and psychiatric factors affected by ICE environments that underlie, interact with, and contribute to team dynamics in long‐duration missions. The general framework we apply is that of biopsychosocial “stress” (Lloyd & Nemeroff, 2011; McEwen & Karatsoreos, 2012). Stressors can be subjectively perceived psychological experiences (e.g. heavy workload, time pressure, social conflict), physical challenges (e.g. exercise, combat, noise), or in operational environments, often a combined physiological and psychological experience (Dietz et al. 2010). The brain is central to the stress response in that it interprets environmental and physiological stimuli as threatening or potentially threatening, orchestrates biological and behavioral responses, and mediates the cyclical learning and adaptation processes (McEwen & Gianaros, 2010). The neurobiology of the stress system is quite complex, involving multiple brain circuits, peripheral organs, and psychoactive chemicals, but is classically associated with the hypothalamic‐pituitary‐adrenal (HPA) axis and the release of the endogenous biochemicals cortisol and epinephrine as part of the stress system's defining “fight or flight” response.

---

It is important to note that stress and stress responses are not inherently negative. Indeed, as McEwen and Karatsoreos (2012) describe, stress can be positive, tolerable, or toxic. This idea stems from the work of Lazarus and colleagues (Lazarus & Folkman, 1984) who argued that individuals appraise stressors as either (a) threatening, which act as a hindrance that elicits negative emotions and behaviors, or (b) challenging, which elicits positive emotions and promotes a desire for personal growth. Acute stress can boost immunity and enhance performance on physical and cognitive tasks, ultimately providing a challenge and potential sense of mastery (Suedfeld, 2001). Tolerable stress from adverse life events may be more intense or enduring, but can be buffered by social support mechanisms and provide an opportunity for coping and resilience. Chronic stress over time is potentially toxic, where adverse events are some combination of extremely intense or traumatic, frequent, or persistent, all with limited buffering. Chronic stress can lead to alterations in brain architecture and dysregulated biobehavioral and cognitive systems associated with myriad physical and neuropsychiatric dysfunctions, including cardiometabolic disease and mood disorders (Byers & Yaffe, 2011; Gianaros et al. 2015). In addition to interacting with the HPA axis stress system, circulating levels of psychoactive androgen (e.g. testosterone) and estrogen (e.g. progesterone, estradiol) hormones vary by gender (and by phase in naturally cycling women) and can also interact with other endogenous neurobiological systems and exogenous medications, thus potentially conferring differential vulnerability to behavioral health conditions that should be considered by mission planners and support. Although these and related gonadal hormones are usually associated with reproductive behaviors and mood, recent evidence also suggests they join the peptide hormone oxytocin (De Dreu, 2012) in playing a moderating role in social dynamics, group stability maintenance, and team effectiveness (Derntl, Hack, Kryspin-Exner, & Habel, 2013; Edwards, Wetzel, & Wyner, 2006; Eisenegger, Haushofer & Fehr, 2011; Eisenegger, Naef, Snozzi, Heinrichs, & Fehr, 2010; Zyphur, Narayanan, Koh, & Koh, 2009).

A factor of increasingly recognized importance to individual neurobehavioral function and team dynamics in long-duration ICE environments is sleep and circadian rhythms (Barger et al. 2014; Miller, Matsangas, & Kenney, 2012; Roma, 2014; Whitmire et al. 2009). Empirical research on the effects of sleep and circadian factors on team dynamics and performance in ICE environments is sparse, so we offer Fig. 1 as a conceptualization of the interactive relationships between sleep and circadian factors, individual neurobiological mechanisms, individual and team neurobehavioral processes and cohesion, and individual and team performance outcomes in the task and social domains.

The most superficially obvious sleep risk relative to team dynamics is asynchronous sleep/wake/work schedules, as team tasks, social activities, and professional and personal relationships are difficult to develop and maintain without all team members being physically present or somehow able to interact in real time. Even if team task effectiveness is largely maintained, the potential influences of sleep and circadian factors on social processes may constitute a significant threat to team dynamics and mission success, particularly when compounded with the chronic stress of working and living over long durations in ICE environments.

Given the potential physical, neurobehavioral, and psychosocial stresses of working and living in ICE environments, the development of maladaptive neurobiological, cognitive, and behavioral patterns and neuropsychiatric disorders is considered a major risk for long-duration missions (Herian, Vanhove, Harms, & Luthans, 2015; Slack et al. 2016). International Space Station (ISS) astronauts confirm greater perceived stress compared to shorter shuttle missions, but apparently nothing in either case that escalated to clinically or operationally significant levels (Myers et al. 2015). This is in stark contrast to prevalence rates of behavioral health disorders reported post-deployment by US military personnel (∼18%; Riddle et al. 2007) or in the general public (∼26%; Demyttenaere et al. 2004). However, combining many of the defining features of ICE environments along with duration, Palinkas, Glogower, Dembert, Hansen, and Smullen (2001) found that 5.2% of 313 Antarctic winter-over personnel developed clinically diagnosable disorders in-mission, most commonly mood and adjustment disorders (31.6%), sleep disorders (21%), substance use disorders (10.5%), and personality disorders (7.9%).

An important point to consider is that insofar as a team is composed of individuals, that which affects any one individual can potentially affect any or all other team members and the crew as a whole. This axiom is particularly true given the interdependence inherent to small teams on long-duration missions in complex ICE environments. If any individual's neurobiological, cognitive, or behavioral systems become dysregulated to the point of clinical significance, it automatically becomes operationally significant, as it will almost certainly impact team dynamics in both the task and social domains. As such, even with an emphasis on team performance and social dynamics as critical for long-duration mission success, mission management and support personnel must understand and consider individual neurobiological and behavioral mechanisms as part of a comprehensive mission planning, behavioral health monitoring, and countermeasure development strategy to reduce the risks to both individual

---

behavioral health and team dynamics in the high‐stress setting of long‐duration missions in ICE environments.

With an appreciation of individual neurobiological and behavioral health vulnerabilities established, we now continue our review with a focus on team dynamics couched within a unifying framework of team “cohesion” incorporating the overlapping and bidirectionally influential components of task cohesion and social cohesion.

## DYNAMIC RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN TASK AND SOCIAL COHESION OVER TIME

Group cohesion can be conceptualized as a desire of members to remain in a particular group to achieve an identified group goal (Festinger, Back, & Schachter, 1950), or “a dynamic process that is reflected in the tendency for a group to stick together and remain united in the pursuit of its instrumental objectives and/or satisfaction of member affective needs” (Carron, Brawley, & Widmeyer, 1998, p. 213). Cohesion is often conceptualized as two overlapping sub‐domains of task cohesion and social cohesion, that is, taskwork and teamwork, or commitment to the mission and commitment to each other (Carron, Widmeyer, & Brawley, 1985; MacCoun, 1993; MacCoun, Kier, & Belkin, 2006), to which we would add working well together and living well together.

Both task cohesion and social cohesion are particularly salient and interactive in the mixed professional/social context of complex long‐duration missions in ICE environments. Recent operationally oriented theoretical frameworks (Salas, Wilson, Murphy, King, & Salisbury, 2008; Wilson, Salas, Priest, & Andrews, 2007) have parsed cohesion into more tractable processes of relevance to high‐performance teams, depicting the construct as one requiring cooperation, communication, coordination, and commitment applied at multiple organizational levels (Bell, Kozlowski, & Blawath, 2012). Frameworks such as this, as in most of the Industrial/Organizational (I/O) Psychology and Organizational Behavior (OB) literatures, quite rightly focus on task cohesion and task performance based on the premise of space exploration and other operational settings as primarily work‐ or mission‐oriented environments (Castaño, Watts, & Tekleab, 2013; Salas et al. 2015b). However, one of the inherent challenges of long‐duration missions in ICE environments, and to some extent a gap in the organizational science knowledge base, is the overlap and interaction between task and social cohesion and the mechanisms mediating them. The importance of team processes relevant to social cohesion is underscored by a recent long‐duration space exploration (LDSE) crew job analysis conducted by NASA, where a panel of astronauts and spaceflight subject matter experts identified or projected the most critical behavioral competencies

---

for various mission profiles of relatively short, medium, and long durations (Barrett, Holland, &amp; Vessey, 2015). Among the 18 factors included in the final rankings, small-group living, teamwork, and adaptability ranked highly for all mission types, with small-group living and teamwork scoring particularly high for the longer-duration, autonomous missions (NASA Human Exploration &amp; Operations Mission Directorate, 2015).

At one extreme, for most brief or simple tasks conducted in the comfort and safety of regulated workplaces such as customer service interactions, professional consultations, and one-time meetings, the knowledge, skills, and abilities that constitute task cohesion are effectively all that matter. Social cohesion may compromise success if the team includes members with exceptionally poor interpersonal skills or who manifest strong biases based on surface-level demographic variables, but the limited interaction time functionally prevents these social issues from overpowering the team's focus on the acute task at hand, and precludes the influence of deeper shared values, activities of daily living, recreation, and other interdependencies beyond those encompassed by task cohesion. Finally, at the far right end of the spectrum are all‐encompassing high‐risk operations in ICE environments such as extended military combat deployments and LDSE missions. Again, technical competence and task cohesion are always important, especially in safety‐critical environments, and at no point does social cohesion displace task cohesion. However, as task cohesion behavioral patterns and work/rest/leisure routines plasticize and the behavioral health impacts of isolation and confinement accumulate over time, so increases the salience of social cohesion and its interaction/overlap with task cohesion as a potentially significant contributor to mission success. It is also worth noting that as mission duration and concomitant isolation and confinement increase in LDSE, the definition of “mission success” itself expands beyond simply the completion of operational tasks. Indeed, the long‐term post‐mission health and well‐being of operational personnel are publicly acknowledged priorities (Milliken, Auchterlonie, & Hoge, 2007; Worth, Manning, & Longnecker, 2004). Although there are currently no true LDSE missions in profoundly extreme environments (e.g. Mars) from which to draw empirical evidence, data from Antarctic winter‐over campaigns, long‐duration space analog studies, and other capsule environments reveal myriad biobehavioral risks in the social domain (Basner et al. 2014; Palinkas, 1990; Suedfeld & Steel, 2000; Wang et al. 2014). Simply put, the longer the mission and more extreme the environment, the more mission success may be affected by team‐level behavioral health, neuropsychiatric,

and psychosocial phenomena that receive less attention in the science of organizations. Moreover, processes that are typically ascribed and best understood under the auspices of task cohesion in work teams may also apply to social cohesion processes.

## FACTORS AND THREATS TO TEAM DYNAMICS IN ICE ENVIRONMENTS

Team cohesion is thought to be important to team functioning in ICE environments as success often hinges upon maintenance of team effectiveness despite the influence of a dynamic and potentially dangerous operational environment. Long-duration missions in ICE environments are characterized by periods of low workload coupled with intense, highly coordinated planned activity as well as necessary responses to unplanned stress-inducing emergencies. This need to transition between highly interdependent tasks requiring coordinated precision and individual tasks that rely solely on functional expertise (Bedwell et al. 2011; Rosen et al. 2011; Salas et al. 2015) coupled with results from recent analog studies (Bedwell, Roma, Ramsay, & Binsted, 2016) suggest that both interpersonal and functional flexibility (Leary, 1957) are critical to mission success. Thanks in large part to sponsoring agencies like NASA and the Department of Defense, team research in ICE environments has made significant progress in recent years, particularly with regard to cohesion, teamwork (including cooperation and coordination), and adaptation. However, significant gaps remain that threaten these important factors, which can cause a demise in team effectiveness over time. In this section, we highlight some of these issues in a review of selected team factors that have the potential to negatively influence cohesion, adaptation, and ultimately performance and mission success. Much of this evidence is presented from the perspective of astronaut crews as the archetypal long-duration ICE environment mission team, but other operational contexts are highlighted when possible.

### Selection

Astronaut candidates were originally drawn from military test pilot applicant pools as they were thought to have “the right stuff” that would contribute to performance through high levels of cohesion as all candidates were considered similar in functional expertise and personality. However, later research revealed that personality traits did not predict selection into the astronaut corps (Musson, Sandal, & Helmreich, 2004). Yet, this does not suggest that personality is irrelevant, rather, it reflects the fact that validation efforts are limited due to the dearth of available performance data.

---

Given the characteristics of long‐duration missions, researchers have emphasized a need to focus on team‐oriented factors, including communication skills, teamwork skills, group suitability, and intercultural competence, abilities that have been met with reported success within the European Space Agency (ESA) and the Japanese Aerospace and Exploration Agency (JAXA; Inoue & Tachibana, 2013; Maschke, Oubaid, & Pecena, 2011). However, such efforts are based on shorter duration missions than what are anticipated for LDSE, so caution must be taken when attempting to generalize these findings to other ICE environments of extreme duration. Supporting evidence for the idea that different selection criteria are required for longer‐duration missions comes from a job analysis conducted for low earth orbit explorations as compared to deep space (Barrett et al. 2015). Teamwork skills were considered the most important competency in shorter, low earth orbit missions, followed by communication skills and adaptability. However, results change when considering the longer, deep space explorations such that self‐care was rated the most valuable, with similarity in small‐group living competencies and adaptability also ranking among the most valued skill sets. This suggests that the desired personal characteristics of team members may differ depending on the timing of the performance episodes and the operational demands associated with the work in any given ICE environment, and adaptability is critical in any context.

In terms of individual selection criteria, research also suggests a strong risk factor that could otherwise undermine the beneficial effects of team competencies -- specifically, emotional instability, or neuroticism, which is a propensity to respond negatively to threat, frustration, or loss (Goldberg, 1993). Research suggests this trait is of broad and significant importance (Lahey, 2009) because it is (1) relatively stable, (2) significantly correlated with various negative behavioral health conditions (e.g. depression, anxiety; McCrae, 1990), and (3) inversely associated with positive outcomes, including resilience (Campbell‐Sills, Cohan, & Stein, 2006), adaptability (Martin, Nejad, Colmar, & Liem, 2013), perceived availability of social support (Swickert & Owens, 2010), and positive emotional contagion (Ilies, Wagner, & Morgeson, 2007).

### Team Composition

Selection of adaptable individuals who (1) place team success above their own, (2) have relevant functional and social expertise, and (3) are low in neuroticism may indeed contribute to maintaining team cohesion and ensuring team effectiveness. But the team composition literature suggests that the interaction of these individual level characteristics over time plays an important role in team functioning, and as such, can mitigate or exacerbate situations that can lead to performance decrements (Bell, Villado, Lukasik, Belau, & Briggs, 2011; Landon et al. 2015). In studying anonymous astronaut journal entries, Stuster

---

(2010) found that group interaction issues were one of the most frequently mentioned topics; more specifically, and consistent with the task-social cohesion relationship described in the previous section, that over time, even small differences in individual characteristics can create problems, such as member isolation, trust, and cohesion decrements.

Nonetheless, a focus on team composition and compatibility is not complete without consideration of multiple levels of analysis, specifically including dyads (Humphrey & Aime, 2014). Kenny and colleagues (2006) argued that a single dyadic relationship within a team “is arguably the fundamental unit of interpersonal interaction and interpersonal relations” (p. 1) and, therefore, the foundation of teamwork and team performance. As the foundation of a team (Salas, Dickinson, Converse, & Tannenbaum, 1992), the dyadic relationship can be defined as the specific behaviors one exhibits in the presence of a partner (Kenny, Mohr, & Levesque, 2001). The unique one-on-one relationships between individuals within a team influence the corresponding thoughts, feelings, and behaviors of all members. The very nature of the dyadic relationship can positively or negatively affect the two involved and even influence the entire team as a whole. From a beneficial standpoint, social support has been shown to positively influence well-being and job satisfaction (Humphrey, Nahrgang, & Morgeson, 2007). Furthermore, increased interaction enables dyadic pairs to build trust and engage in helping behaviors that improve team performance (De Jong, Van der Vegt, & Molleman, 2007; Van Der Vegt, Bunderson, & Oosterhof, 2006). However, there are potentially harmful effects of dyadic relationships over time in teams. For example, increased interaction can also reveal deep-level characteristics, such as differing values and beliefs (Bell et al. 2011), which can result in interpersonal conflicts (Jehn, 1995). Such conflicts have been reported in analog studies of long-duration mission in ICE environments (Bedwell et al. 2016). Further, negative moods or emotional expressions have a strong contagion effect, spreading between dyadic members and potentially across the entire team (Barsade, 2002). Such emotional contagion effects have also been demonstrated in long-duration ICE analog studies (Binsted et al. 2017).

### Autonomy, Communication, and Multiteam Systems

A variety of organizational and operational characteristics can also impact team cohesion and performance in long-duration ICE environments. Among them is autonomy, a job characteristic that has been widely tested as part of the popular Job Characteristics Model (JCM; Hackman & Oldham, 1976). This well-validated model suggests that autonomy can lead to increased motivation and greater job satisfaction (Humphrey et al. 2007). As with traditional command-and-control military operations, current space crews experience limited

---

amounts of autonomy due to the design of the ISS, which is largely operated directly by the Mission Control Center (MCC; Landon et al. 2015). Given that the crew acts as the eyes and hands to the brain of the MCC, coordination between the two teams (i.e. a multiteam system) is of paramount importance and requires high levels of task cohesion (e.g. effective communication) within and between the two teams. Fortunately, communication is nearly instantaneous on such missions. However, as noted above, long‐duration missions and ICE operational environments often experience constraints on communications. For the most remote operational teams on Earth and space exploration crews subject to communication delays up to ∼20 minutes each way (Landon et al. 2015), mission success requires successful management of unprecedented levels of crew autonomy.

There have been no published studies to date looking at shifting levels of autonomy over extended periods of time, but research suggests it will likely impact adaptation to spaceflight demands (Kanas & Manzey, 2008). Recent laboratory experiments in experienced work teams showed that performance in an interdependent simulated planetary surface exploration task was improved under autonomous conditions and robust to the loss of communications, although the loss of communications elicited the same level of physiological stress reactivity as highly scheduled mission operations (Roma et al. 2011, 2013). Similarly, a recent short‐term study on the ISS (Palinkas, Chou, Leveton, & Vessey, 2013) found that a 50‐second, one‐way communication delay (vs. no delay) led to increased time to complete tasks as well as elevated levels of perceived stress/frustration. Further, the delays led to misunderstandings. The increased autonomy was positively associated with crew performance, but it was not a significant mediator of the relationship between communication delay and the various outcomes of interest. This suggests that these two variables uniquely affect performance and well‐being (Landon et al. 2015).

One interesting outcome of the communication delay was less interaction with MCC. In analyzing astronaut journals, Stuster (2010, 2016) found that communication often tapered during the fourth quarter of a mission. Astronauts reported greater tension with MCC, but also greater discussion of and appreciation for positive interactions with MCC. Stuster also noted that astronauts' comments about workload, a lack of understanding about time required on task, and overscheduling increased during this time. There was also general consensus about the positive feelings associated with being able to control their own schedule (although those opportunities were rare), which led to greater crew cohesion, including a stronger in‐group/outgroup feeling among the crew relative to MCC. In other words, disconnect with the ground enabled the crew to bond, but at the expense of cohesion among the larger multiteam system. Given that the current model of space exploration is based on low crew autonomy, constant communication, and mission control, training efforts will likely need to dramatically change to accommodate such dramatic shifts in job characteristics associated with long‐duration missions, and more research is

---

needed to determine the degree to which these changes will affect cohesion among crew members and the larger multiteam mission support system on Earth.

## FUTURE DIRECTIONS FOR RESEARCH AND PRACTICE

### Temporal Dynamics

Although the effects of isolation, confinement, and various psychosocial factors and operational constraints can be meaningfully examined in laboratory experiments, analog environments, and field studies, the marquee feature of long-duration missions -- the passage of time -- cannot be modeled in human research. Developmental psychobiologists studying rodents, primates, and other non-human species can examine the entire lifespan to better understand the development of basic biopsychosocial processes (Callaghan, Sullivan, Howell, & Tottenham, 2014), but the ability to compress a long-duration operational team's lifecycle is not a luxury afforded to those studying human performance. Indeed, as Salas and colleagues (2015a, Salas et al. 2015b) note, organizational and military psychology research on team lifecycles and dynamics is largely limited to cross-sectional designs and rarely longitudinal (Bartone & Adler, 1999), but if mission duration and the cumulative stress it entails are as important as theory and the limited empirical database suggest, particularly in ICE environments, then the need for truly long-duration studies on the order of months or years is all the more pressing.

Emerging preliminary evidence suggests the possibility of six months in ICE settings as a minimum threshold for threats to team cohesion to achieve a critical mass warranting intervention and focused countermeasures even in high-performing and highly motivated teams (Bedwell et al. 2016; Bedwell, Roma, & Fletcher, 2017; Binsted et al. 2017; Roma, Waggoner, Hienz, Binsted, & Hursh, 2017), but naturally limited sample sizes make these conclusions extremely tentative. This underscores the importance of mission duration itself as a research variable, highlights the limitations of extrapolation versus interpolation of presumably nonlinear functions (Kozlowski, 2015), and emphasizes the practical benefits of conducting multiple simultaneous studies of team dynamics in long-duration ICE environments. To the latter point, we encourage continued work on team temporal dynamics with high-fidelity space analog missions (Keeton et al. 2011) in NASA's Human Exploration Research Analog (HERA) facility, the Russian Mars-500 chamber, the Hawaii Space Exploration Analog and Simulation (HI-SEAS) project, various polar outposts (e.g. Concordia and Neumayer stations), and complementary collaborative projects with military, first responders, elite athletes, medical units, and other high-performance teams in long-duration operational settings. We also support

---

focused use of the ISS itself as a high-fidelity long-duration ICE analog (Keeton et al. 2012; Leveton et al. 2012), and even the somewhat radical notion of developing permanent outposts on the Moon and near-Earth objects (e.g. large asteroids) as ultra-high fidelity biomedical and behavioral research analogs of long-duration deep space missions that allow investigation of interactive effects of the space environment (e.g. microgravity, radiation) on the brain, behavior, and performance at the individual, crew, and multi-team levels (Goswami et al. 2012).

### Multidisciplinary Integration and Countermeasure Development

As evident even from our limited review, numerous variables across multiple levels of analysis and scientific disciplines can contribute to a fuller understanding of the factors and threats to team dynamics in ICE environments, and potentially inform the development of countermeasures to predict, build, maintain, and repair team task and social cohesion. Unfortunately, for mostly practical reasons, multidisciplinary research is more the exception than the rule. For example, NASA systematically supports research in a number of behaviorally relevant risk areas considered critical to long-duration missions, including team dynamics, sleep and circadian rhythms, radiation exposure, human factors and technology interactions, immunology, sensorimotor and perceptual systems, behavioral health and clinical psychology, and pharmacology and medications management. Yet, funding programs typically focus on one topic area, or allocate separate funding streams by discipline with little formal support to reinforce comprehensive multidisciplinary research endeavors from the broader scientific community. This is by no means unique to agencies interested in operational performance (Börner et al. 2010; Stokols, Hall, Taylor, & Moser, 2008), and by no means arbitrary, but nonetheless still creates an unfortunate division of labor that limits understanding of the combined effects of various risks to team cohesion and long-term health, well-being, and mission success in ICE environments (Goswami et al. 2013). Focused multidisciplinary efforts that integrate multiple theoretical and methodological approaches could lead to novel techniques for quantifying individual and team constructs (Salas et al. 2015a), novel insights on the nature of team task and social cohesion, more accurate multivariate predictive models (Hursh et al. 2004; Lazer et al. 2009; Van Dongen, 2004), and more effective and personalized in-mission countermeasures ideally suited to high-performance teams in long-duration extreme environments.

### Biological Variables

Given the potential effects of ICE environments, operational and human factors constraints, and chronic psychosocial stress on the brain over long

---

durations, it is likely that neurobiological factors can also contribute to mission risk in the task and social cohesion domains. A growing body of observational work is exploring genetic and physiological biomarkers and predictors of individual vulnerability and resilience to stress-induced neurobehavioral dysfunction and behavioral health disorders (Goel & Dinges, 2011; Satterfield, Wisor, Field, Schmidt, & Van Dongen, 2015; Schmidt, Shelton, & Duman, 2011; Toker, Shirom, Shapira, Berliner, & Melamed, 2005; Walker et al. 2017; Yehuda, Neylan, Flory, & McFarlane, 2013), as well as brain imaging to assess structural changes and functional circuitry signatures of chronic stress (Admon et al. 2013). Aside from animal models of space radiation and neurobehavioral performance (Davis, DeCicco-Skinner, Roma, & Hienz, 2014; Davis, Roma & Hienz, 2016), laboratory and operational assessments of fatigue countermeasures (Caldwell et al. 2009; Faber, Häusser, & Kerr, 2015), and some of the aforementioned laboratory experiments on neurohormonal systems underlying team social cohesion (de Dreu, 2012; Eisenegger et al. 2010), experimental behavioral pharmacology is a remarkably underutilized approach for the study of team performance and social dynamics. In addition to the ability to target and establish causal contributions of specific neurobiological systems, compounds with known safety profiles and proven efficacy may also potentially serve as in-mission treatments. In all cases, we encourage continued exploration and integration of objective biological predictors, mediators, and moderators of team performance, cohesion, and biopsychosocial adaptation to accelerate the maturation of a genuine “organizational neuroscience” (cf. Butler, O'Broin, Lee, & Senior, 2016; Foxall, 2014; Wiltshire & Fiore, 2014; Woolley et al. 2007) and enhance selection, composition, training, monitoring, and customized individual- and team-level in-mission countermeasures to critically endangered task and social cohesion processes.

### Experimental Analysis of Behavior

Moving from the biological to the behavioral level, we consider the value of behavior analytic approaches for team measurement, research design, and integration with operations. Traditionally, discussions of team cohesion in operational environments were rooted in anecdotes describing a fundamental lapse in communication, coordination, or cooperation; reliance on anecdotes is largely due to the fact that there are so few critical incidents from which to derive normative data (Shepanek, 2005; Stuster, 2010, 2016). Such stories are emotionally compelling and often underscore the tragic consequences of poor task cohesion and the need to address social cohesion issues; however, their inherently subjective and unstandardized nature limits their scientific value. A more rigorous alternative is the analysis of astronauts' personal journals, which has proven valuable for systematically identifying and prioritizing a wide range of professional and psychosocial issues of apparent importance to ISS astronauts over the course of their missions (Stuster, 2010, 2016). However, journal entries are

---

still subjective and unstandardized, as analysts can only examine the topics which the participants choose to discuss. Moreover, regardless of the topic, expert quantitative content analysis is extraordinarily time‐consuming and language‐dependent, and thus impractical as the sole in‐flight tool for diverse multi‐national teams. Alternatively, the objectivity and unobtrusive nature of real‐time behavioral ecology measures such as proximity detection (Kozlowski, 2015), computerized facial expression recognition (Dinges et al. 2005; Dinges, Venkataraman, McGlinchey, & Metaxas, 2007), and linguistic and speech analysis (Fischer, McDonnell, & Orasanu, 2007; Greeley et al. 2013; Wu, Rye, Miller, Schmer‐Galunder, & Ott, 2013) are promising technologies for quantifying variations in task and social cohesion in stressful operational environments, but are also heavily context‐dependent and thus preclude standardization over time as a singular measure of cohesion. Comprehensive computerized team tasks (DeShon, Kozlowski, Schmidt, Milner, & Wiechmann, 2004; Roma et al. 2011, 2013) provide rich metrics capturing multiple aspects of task cohesion in a standardized fashion and have proven valuable in ground‐based research and training applications, but are prohibitively time‐intensive for operational use and minimally informative for social cohesion. The final and most common method of assessing team dynamics in laboratory, field, and operational environments is the use of psychometric tools, such as the popular Group Environment Scale (GES; Moos, 1994) or any of the dozens of other questionnaires designed to capture variations in the many constructs relevant to team functioning (Podsakoff, MacKenzie, Lee, & Podsakoff, 2003). Questionnaires offer distinct advantages because they can directly address perceptions of cohesion in a standardized form with easily computed results and established outcome metrics, thus making it suitable for tracking changes in the social environment over time. However, even with demonstrably valid measures (Spector, 1994), repeatedly completing static questionnaires can be time‐consuming and boring, if not operationally disruptive, which limits the practical temporal resolution with which changes can be tracked in long‐duration missions, especially under autonomous operations. Moreover, members of high‐performance teams' sense of professionalism and duty may inhibit their willingness to document unflatteringly honest answers to questions about their crewmates. Finally, as with anecdotes and personal journals, self‐report assessments remain purely subjective, and curiously individualistic for something intended to capture critical features of a group.

As a complement to the approaches described earlier, we remind the reader of the potential value of the experimental analysis of behavior for understanding and enhancing team dynamics in ICE environments. Although stereotypically radical behaviorist dogma is far less prevalent in the scientific community today than it was prior to the cognitive revolution and the decade of the brain (Jones & Mendell, 1999), behavior analysis' core emphases rooted in positivist philosophy of objective measures, methodological rigor, and replicability of effects is applicable (indeed, pervasive) across the behavioral and social sciences

---

regardless of concomitant emphases on biological, cognitive, or psychosocial variables (Roediger, 2004). Organizational behavior management takes an applied behavior analytic approach to I/O psychology, but is mostly limited to conventional workplaces rather than operational environments (Mawhinney, 1984; Nolan, Jarema, & Austin, 1999). However, recent advances in behavioral economics derived from the operant behavioral psychology tradition (Hursh, 1980, 1984, 2014; Hursh & Roma, 2013, 2016; Hursh & Silberberg, 2008) provide a novel conceptual, methodological, and analytical framework for objective, standardized, and metric‐rich assessments and potential countermeasures relevant to how individuals and teams “value” altruism, productivity, and fairness processes underlying team cohesion over time in social‐context specific settings and ICE environments (Emurian, Canfield, & Brady, 2010, 2011; Hursh & Roma, 2013). In addition, drawing from pioneering interdisciplinary work on “behavioral engineering” in confined environments (Brady, 1990, 2005, 2007; Brady & Emurian, 1978; Brady, Bigelow, Emurian, & Williams, 1975; Emurian, Brady, Meyerhoff, & Mougey, 1983; Emurian, Brady, Ray, Meyerhoff & Mougey, 1984), we encourage mission planners and human factors specialists to further investigate and incorporate operant behavior analytic principles that actively and passively promote and sustain team task and social cohesion into mission scheduling, habitat design, and instrumentation of the fully constructed environments of autonomous long‐duration ICE mission operations.

Philosophical, measurement, and environmental design issues aside, the experimental analysis of behavior by default offers a discipline‐agnostic framework for operational research that can address many of the design concerns expressed by the biomedical research community (Ploutz‐Snyder, Fiedler, & Feiveson, 2014). Although randomized, double‐blind controlled clinical trials are considered the gold standard for empirical evidence in the biomedical sciences (Grossman & Mackenzie, 2005), such designs are often impossible, impractical, or simply logically inappropriate for longitudinal studies in complex operational and ICE environments. Defining features of the prototypical behavior analytic research approach almost perfectly mirror long‐duration mission architecture: small sample sizes (including single subject designs), within‐subject repeated measures over time, and control (or at least measurement of) extraneous variables in a fully constructed environment (Sidman, 1960). Even with single or very few subjects inherent to high‐performing teams, the effects of many operationally relevant variables can be determined through “A‐B‐A” designs where the outcomes of interest are consistently and repeatedly measured under alternating conditions of differing levels of the independent variable(s). Although the experimental analysis of behavior traditionally eschews complex inferential statistics, they are not mutually exclusive approaches. We argue that the nature of team dynamics and operational research with small groups makes it that much more imperative to employ powerful objective analytical tools to account for variance not just at the individual

---

and team levels, but over time across the repeated measures of the mission(s). To this end, researchers across disciplines could combine behavior analytic designs with techniques such as nonlinear mixed‐effects modeling, which is akin to hierarchical linear modeling but for repeated‐measures data where the individuals and teams over time become nested factors and nonlinear trajectories and individual differences at the person and team/mission levels can be accounted for (Olofsen, Dinges, & Van Dongen, 2004; Van Dongen, Maislin, & Dinges, 2004). These analyses can inform the temporal dynamics of team cohesion and other variables as part of comprehensive multidisciplinary research and countermeasure evaluation efforts.

### Family Systems Theory

A final consideration that takes us from the purely behavioral level to the intersection of psychosocial and clinical domains focuses on a novel framework for both understanding team dynamics in long‐duration ICE environments and developing training and treatment countermeasures. Specifically, if one accepts the conceptual task‐social cohesion framework we offered earlier, then what becomes clear is that as missions increase in duration and extremity, the most salient team risks weighing against mission success gradually shift from task cohesion issues dominated by organizational and human factors psychology to increasing overlap with social cohesion issues in the social and clinical psychology and neuropsychiatry domains. But is there a theoretical framework that is appropriate for the less work‐oriented nature of social cohesion while maintaining relevance to the mechanisms underlying task cohesion and team performance in long‐duration ICE environments? We suggest family systems theory as a viable approach. Family systems theory holds that families are goal directed, self‐correcting, dynamic, interconnected systems that both affect and are affected by their environment and by the properties of the family system itself (Klein & White, 1996). This definition is strikingly similar to general definitions of teams and organizations within the I/O psychology literature (cf. Kozlowski & Ilgen, 2006), and seems particularly relevant to teams in long‐duration ICE environments. Indeed, the influential Circumplex Model of Marital & Family Systems (Olson & Gorall, 2003; Olson, Russell, & Sprenkle, 1989; Olson, Sprenkle, & Russell, 1979) emphasizes cohesion and adaptability as the core dimensions of family function (with communication as a mediating factor for both). The circumplex is primarily conceptual, but can also be quantified for research and diagnostic purposes through a self‐report questionnaire, the Family Adaptability and Cohesion Scale (FACES; Olson, 2011). However it is measured, a social system at any given time will function as the product of its cohesion and flexibility

Although formal application of family systems theory within operational teams (as opposed to their families; Saltzman et al. 2011; Sipes &amp; Vander Ark, 2005) is less common than organizational psychology approaches, it is not unusual to see highly cohesive military units colloquially adopt some family concepts and terminology (e.g., "brothers in arms"), a sentiment that has also

---

been described by NASA astronauts (“space brothers”; C. C. Anderson, personal communication). Anecdotes notwithstanding, LDSE teams, military units, traditional work groups, and family systems also share the critical social network feature of a lack of control in “teammate” selection -- as siblings are born, so teammates are typically assigned, thereby inviting selection and team composition factors as potential threats to team cohesion and performance. Moreover, a major strength of a family framework over the traditional organizational science approach is the presumed cohabitation of the team, and all the associated shared space, resources, activities of daily living, and recreation that come with it. Simply put, family relationships are personal relationships inherently dependent on social cohesion to succeed. Teams in long‐duration ICE environments work and live together, so a family systems approach like the circumplex model seems especially appropriate for framing at least the social cohesion aspect of long‐duration missions or as an overlapping complement to the organizational science frameworks used for task cohesion.

It would not be inappropriate to consider a family paradigm as a relevant “shared mental model” (Cannon‐Bowers, Salas, & Converse, 1990; Fiore, Salas, & Cannon‐Bowers, 2001; Mathieu, Heffner, Goodwin, Salas, & Cannon‐Bowers, 2000; Mohammed, Ferzandi, & Hamilton, 2010) underlying social cohesion in ICE environments. Indeed, considering cognition from a family framework perspective is not a new concept, as the entire notion of transactive memory within teams was drawn from literature that first identified this cognitive system within married couples (Wegner, 1987). Interestingly, the literature on family‐run businesses suggests that social cohesion may outweigh task cohesion in achieving desired outcomes, providing some support for our task‐social cohesion conceptual framework introduced herein. Specifically, social cohesion is significantly related to continuity of the organization (Jiménez, Martos, & Jiménez, 2015) and profitability (Adendorff, Venter, & Boshoff, 2008; Venter, Boshoff, & Maas, 2005). This is likely because the family unit eliminates the typical agency theory problem (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) associated with organizations that operate based on different individuals holding the roles of the agent (i.e. manager) and the principal (i.e. owner). In family businesses, the agents are the principals, and therefore the decisions they make as managers are naturally aligned with the best interests of the owners. However, this does not come without costs. Given the overlapping roles and family ties, when social cohesion crumbles, family organizations tend to fail as well (Jiménez et al. 2015). If a family systems approach to long‐duration teams in ICE environments is appropriate, then perhaps training, monitoring, and intervention tools rooted in empirically supported family therapy approaches (INSERM, 2004) could be adopted, developed, and applied to operational teams as a social cohesion countermeasure, either on its own (cf. Craske et al. 2009; Rose et al. 2013; Rose, 2014), in conjunction with pharmacological treatments for behavioral health or other medical conditions (Wotring, 2015), or as a complement

---

or integrated component of otherwise task cohesion-oriented team debrief methods (Tannenbaum & Cerasoli, 2013).

## SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION

In this effort, we discussed aspects of team dynamics relevant to long-duration isolated, confined, and extreme environments. We not only described ICE, but also reviewed relevant biological, neurobehavioral, psychiatric, and operational factors that may impact team cohesion and adaptation. We also highlighted selected critical aspects of team effectiveness in such environments within a unifying framework of team cohesion that integrates the task and social domains over time. Finally, we offered a multidisciplinary perspective reviewing factors from parallel and emerging disciplines outside the dominant group cohesion and operational team performance approaches, a perspective that we believe has the potential to inform the scientific knowledge base and enhance development of empirically supported countermeasures for team cohesion and biopsychosocial adaptation over time in extreme environments.

By presenting a view of where we currently stand with regard to ICE environment science, we suggested avenues for future research that draw on various conceptual, methodological, and analytical approaches. This is important, as we argue that to really move forward, the research and operational communities must reach beyond our respective niche disciplines. Such efforts can not only provide greater insight into the constructs and outcomes relevant to team cohesion, but can also lead to novel solutions to inherently complex issues that require true systems thinking, well beyond the traditional boundaries of individual investigators' primary disciplines. Such efforts are beginning to take shape formally with funding initiatives requiring multidisciplinary collaboration, but more can and should be done informally as well. The power to address the mission critical issues of team dynamics in long-duration ICE environments is in our hands -- we only need to move beyond the orbits of our own disciplines to begin harnessing it and truly move onward and upward.
