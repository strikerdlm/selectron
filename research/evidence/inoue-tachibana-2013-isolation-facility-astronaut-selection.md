# An Isolation and Confinement Facility for the Selection of Astronaut Candidates

NATSUHIKO INOUE AND SHOICHI TACHIBANA

INOUE N, TACHIBANA S. An isolation and confinement facility for the selection of astronaut candidates. Aviat Space Environ Med 2013; 84:867-71.

Background: In 2008, JAXA conducted its fifth astronaut candidate recruitment. The final stage involved 10 of 963 applicants undergoing the first and second examination stages based on physical checks, written tests, and interviews. The third (final) stage focused on evaluating the behavioral attitudes required for ISS crewmembers. Since it is difficult to assess these attitudes by paper testing or interview alone, JAXA has established an environment in which the assessors could observe the behavior of examinees on a continuous basis. Methods: The 10 examinees stayed in the isolation and confinement facility for 1 wk. During their stay, several group and personal tasks were assigned along with predetermined daily schedules covering aspects of leadership, teamwork ability, productivity (performance), and so on. Subsequently, psychologists, psychiatrists, and JAXA officials/managers experienced in practical human space development evaluated them. Results: During the confinement, no examinees showed any excessive character traits or psychopathologic behavior. Repeated observations led to a convergence of opinions among the experts. Three examinees ultimately passed this assessment and were assigned as new Japanese astronaut candidates. Discussion: This unique assessment enabled the assessors to conduct longitudinal evaluations, evaluations based on observing detailed behavior, and easy control of test conditions and safety. Although "selection of the right stuff" may differ among each space agency, this method offers the unique advantage of allowing experts in various fields to evaluate the competencies of examinees both synthetically and longitudinally.

Keywords: astronaut selection, isolation and confinement facility, International Space Station, long-duration flight.

In February 2008, THE Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) issued a call for astronaut candidates to cover its planned operations until 2015 onboard the International Space Station (ISS). A total of 963 applicants applied for 3 candidate positions. The applicants underwent up to three screening examinations before final evaluation by the JAXA Astronaut Review Board, which centered on their competence as astronauts, physical fitness, and psychological aptitude. To evaluate behavioral aspects, JAXA set up the Psychiatric/Psychological Review Subcommittee under the Astronaut Review Board. The subcommittee members were mainly experts who had previous experience with JAXA astronaut selection. To ensure the subcommittee's fairness and impartiality, it also included some independent experts, such as clinical psychologists/psychiatrists and a recruitment expert from a commercial company. The examinees were evaluated on many aspects of competence, medicine, and psychology during the first and second examination stages before being subject to the confinement examination, which mainly involved performance evaluations in individual and group tasks. The examinees

were confined to the isolation and confinement facility at the Tsukuba Space Center (TKSC) for 7 d. This unique screening method has not been applied by any other space organization participating in the ISS program nor JAXA's previous astronaut selection reported in this journal (5). To accomplish ISS missions successfully, psychological stress management to maintain mental health and excellent crew performance is becoming a major challenge. Mental adaptation and positive performance ability within an isolation and confinement environment had to be properly evaluated when selecting new ISS crew candidates. The authors prioritize the selection method using the isolation and confinement facility, and discuss its characteristics and usefulness in this paper.

# METHODS

## Subjects

The examinees were 10 healthy Japanese adults, consisting of 9 men and 1 woman, with ages between 30 and 39. This article was not applicable for a JAXA IRB review since the astronaut selection described here was not for any research proposal, but only for operational purposes, and no personal data are described in this article. The facility, located at the TKSC, is known as the Confinement Environment Adaptation Training Facility (shown in Fig. 1; hereinafter, "the confinement facility"). Constructed in 1996, the facility consists of an Experiment Module (to simulate the KIBO Japanese experiment module) and a Habitation Module. The confinement facility is designed for isolation and confinement training or experiments simulating long-term flight missions and contains five monitoring cameras and microphones. ## Procedure

This confinement facility was used to select astronaut candidates a decade ago (1998-1999). Eight applicants who had passed preliminary screenings were confined and subject to 1 wk of evaluations at the facility. The evaluation focused solely on psychiatric and psychological aspects at the time. The following post-selection questions were posed:

1. Are the psychological evaluation criteria useful for screening applicants?
2. Are psychiatrists and psychologists the only professionals needed to evaluate the competence and qualities of applicants?

To address the above questions and improve evaluation methods, repeated discussions were held. Consequently, JAXA decided to apply the eight behavioral competencies which the Multilateral Medical Operations Panel has defined as requirements for an ISS crewmember (Table I) (1,2).

The target items to be observed during the tasks were defined by the Psychiatric/Psychological Review Subcommittee. To balance out individual differences among the evaluators, behavioral markers for each competency were set in advance. An experienced psychologist who employed the half-structured interview method to standardize his own attitude and evaluation conducted individual interviews.

To solve the second question, evaluation by a different expert group (the Competence Review Committee) was introduced. These experts consisted of JAXA officials with experience in practical space operations and management, space development, control, and experiments and research, and included astronauts and experienced pilots. The experts were involved in evaluating the competencies of examinees relative to astronaut tasks or the "ability to accomplish tasks in space."

The examinees were evaluated from a psychiatric perspective through personal interviews conducted before and after confinement. Their behavior during confinement was also observed and rated from perspectives of mental stability, interpersonal relationships, group dynamics, and coping with stress (Table II).

Each examinee was subject to a preliminary and unstructured personal interview to identify the person's psychiatric baseline and avoid any behavioral problems during confinement. Five psychiatrists met with each examinee and preliminary interviews were held to acquire information on detailed personal data (family and growth history), behavioral patterns, and mentality in a daily environment. The examinees were also checked for any morbid tendencies to ensure that they could tolerate the stress of confinement. Significant behavior and utterances observed during the interviews were recorded for reference. During the confinement, five experts observed the behavior of examinees in turn to evaluate their psychiatric properties with respect to mental stability under stress, human relations in group activities, sleep disturbance, and somatization of mental stress. To evaluate decline in sleep quality often reported anecdotally, even during short-term shuttle missions (4), the examinees were instructed to wear an ActiGraph (a wristwatch-type motion sensor; A.M.I. Inc., New York) during confinement. Any tendency toward somatization was also checked by a JAXA flight surgeon during daily interviews.

Upon completion of the confinement test, five psychiatrists held unstructured interviews lasting 15 min with each examinee. If some specific behavior was observed during confinement, the post interview was a good chance to ask the examinee about it. The results of the preliminary interviews, evaluations during confinement, and postinterviews were then integrated, to assess the final rating by the psychiatrist group, which was then reported as an examinee's "General Stress Tolerance." The examinees were also subject to psychological evaluation based on personal and group interviews before and after confinement, as well as behavioral observation during confinement (Table III). In a preliminary personal interview, basic psychological information such as the emotional state, personal life history, character profile, pattern of behavior in the workplace, personal image of astronauts in general, and baseline communication skills were identified to clarify understanding for behavior observation during confinement. Six psychologists formed three pairs of interviewers and interviewed each examinee for 30 min. Noteworthy behavior and utterances were recorded and shared with other psychologists. Immediately after the start of confinement and at night on the third, fourth, and sixth days, one of the psychological experts conducted unstructured personal interviews using the video conferencing system in the Private Conference Room. Opportunities for self-promotion, making speeches, writing, keeping a diary, and making origami (a string of a thousand origami paper cranes) were given to evaluate the ability to express personal opinions and maintain motivation and concentration. In addition, a psychological emotion evaluation test, the POMS questionnaire (3) (Japanese version), applied in various space and ground studies (2), was repeatedly conducted to determine any change in an examinee's emotional state.

After confinement, two psychologists held unstructured interviews with each examinee. The post interviews mainly focused on fatigue, stress, and the psychodynamics during the 7-d cohabitation. The examinees were questioned about the reasons for or causes of any noteworthy behavior observed during confinement. Finally, two psychologists held an unstructured interview with all 10 examinees as a group. The examinees were questioned about any interpersonal incidents observed during confinement. This interview also focused on any changes in their attitudes to others after being released.

Six psychologists summarized all data collected during the remote interviews and behavioral observations for each examinee in terms of each psychological quality, with reference to information obtained during the preliminary and post interviews. For each of the psychological traits, a provisional "A" rating was given to the top five examinees deemed excellent, and a provisional "B" rating was given to the lower five examinees. The evaluations made by all the experts were compiled and provisional ratings summed up for each examinee. A definitive "A" rating was to be given to the top five candidates and a "B" rating to the lower five candidates, according to the final agreement of the experts. It was agreed that a "C" rating would only be given when the experts found some unforgivable personal defects (Fig. 3).

As the facility was originally equipped with 8 single beds, 2 makeshift beds were added for this confinement to accommodate all 10 examinees. To ensure equal test/ evaluation conditions for the examinees, two were assigned with "watch" duties (making bedtime checks and communicating with outside operators) and slept in the makeshift beds in turn. The examinees were continually observed not only during their tasks, but also throughout their "life" in the facility. Assigned tasks were classified as either individual or group tasks. Individual tasks were used to evaluate personal competence and qualities. Group tasks were designed to evaluate the patterns of examinee behavior and the "ability to accomplish tasks" in cooperation with, and surrounded by, other colleagues. Their logical thinking and communication skills were evaluated during debates and group discussions. The examinees were assigned tasks involving collective handicraft work and virtual company business projects to evaluate their leadership, followership, teamwork, and communication capabilities. The behavior of examinees during their "private" hours was also evaluated.

# RESULTS

Throughout the confinement, the psychiatrists/ psychologists group found no examinee who showed any excessive character trait or tendency that might adversely affect the psychodynamics of the group in confinement. Moreover, the ActiGraphs worn by the examinees revealed no tendency toward insomnia or sleep disturbance. During the psychiatric evaluation,

As for the evaluation of each examinee's psychological traits, behavioral observations during the performance of individual and group tasks made it possible to identify the typical psychological traits of each examinee. Details of the observations are not given here to avoid impairing the fairness of future selections. Evaluations of the examinees initially varied widely among the experts, but during later tests involving the performance of tasks, this variation diminished. Repeated evaluations resulted in a convergence of opinions among the experts.

Since no interpersonal problems occurred during their isolation and confinement period, the authors presumed that a certain social desirability might cause behavioral restriction among examinees to avoid impairing their rating during the evaluation. However, observation of the examinees during task hours, as well as while taking meals and rest, also revealed interesting cues for evaluation. Some examinees behaved without considering the effects on others (problems of emotional stability and self-management), while others became less social (aptitude for group living) during rest hours. These findings were considered useful as complementary information.

# DISCUSSION

Using the isolation and confinement facility in the selection process, we could identify the usefulness and limits of this facility to select astronaut candidates. The first merit is that we could easily conduct detailed observations as compared to evaluation in external accommodation facilities or open-air sites. The week of habitation in the isolation chamber with five cameras and sensitive microphone allowed evaluators to conduct more detailed behavioral observations to identify what brief interviews and written tests may not have revealed. These behaviors include, but are not limited to, social traits (e.g., teamwork, leadership, followership, etc.), self-management under a stressful environment, and pathological issues (e.g., claustrophobia, or immoderate habits such as heavy snoring). Despite initial differences in evaluation among the experts, these gradually diminished over time, which showed the ability of experts in respective fields to "judge the right stuff" and successfully select the ideal astronaut candidates based on extended and repeated observations. psychological perspectives revealed considerable information to evaluate examinees, not only while they perform tasks, but also during their free time. During such free time periods, differences in behavior apparently attributable to individuals' professional rank or role and like or dislike of others were observed. These findings were used as precious reference data to evaluate the examinees and make a final decision.

Moreover, the confinement facility could easily control test conditions and enable uniform observation conditions for evaluators during the confinement period. Given the proper arrangement of all tasks and work/ rest schedules, the physical loads on the examinees were easily controlled and balanced. Equalization was also achieved in terms of the conditions regarding the three groups of evaluators, who could observe the behavior of the examinees in the same operation room and from the same observational perspective using a large monitor for shared camera images, which actually helped standardize the expert evaluations.

Finally, from the perspective of safety management, the use of this facility also offers major advantages. During the period of 7 d and 6 nights, medical doctors were on duty in the same building in the daytime on weekdays, and on call overnight and on weekends. In addition, the confinement facility is constructed in an earthquake-resistant and steel-reinforced concrete building where the use of fire is prohibited.

Nevertheless, despite the acknowledged usefulness of the confinement facility for selection, it also has some defects and restrictions. One defect is the fact that the facility requires considerable personnel and costs for operation and safety management. Several dozen staff members had to conduct operations for 7 d, including 24-h monitoring of the 10 examinees. Another defect is the risk of flu or other infectious diseases, which could impair the selection process there. As the confinement was planned for January, this risk was taken into account with due care. Even promising examinees suffering from flu might have been rejected. Lastly, as stated

above, the JAXA confinement facility can accommodate up to 10 persons. For larger organizations such as NASA or the Russian Space Agency that typically select more than that at a time, using a facility of this size might prove rather difficult.

In conclusion, whether selection was conducted correctly or not may be judged from the flight mission results of the astronauts selected. Human space development is currently shifting toward longer-distance and longer-duration missions to the Moon and Mars. Nevertheless, the competencies required of crewmembers during such missions probably resemble those required on current long-duration ISS missions. Although the selection of astronaut candidates with the right stuff may differ depending on each space organization, the authors believe that the JAXA selection method as presented here offers the unique advantage of allowing experts in various fields to evaluate the behavior, performance, and competencies of examinees, both synthetically and longitudinally. The authors wrote this paper in the hope that it would help improve the astronaut selection methods applied by space organizations overseas.
