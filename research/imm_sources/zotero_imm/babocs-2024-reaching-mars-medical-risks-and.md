# ABSTRACT

Compared to lunar missions, Mars missions will pose additional challenges. This includes a longer duration (currently anticipated to take around three years for a return mission), and a hazardous Martian environment with 0.38 Earth gravity, a thin atmosphere, and a weak magnetosphere. These factors contribute to extreme weather conditions, and significant exposure to space radiation. Evacuation to Earth for medical treatment will be impractical, rendering the capability to provide surgical interventions absolutely necessary. This paper is part of an ongoing scoping review of relevant published scientific literature to identify medical conditions that might require operative or non-operative surgical solutions during long-duration spaceflight. In the context of potential future Mars missions, onboard acute conditions, or newly developed chronic diseases (Type 1), and Mars surface/environment-related surgical conditions (Type 2), provide relevant considerations for future mission planning and crew safety. (Type 1) During long-duration spaceflight, exposure to space radiation and microgravity affects every organ system. This may result in a broad range of medical events requiring diverse operative or non-operative surgical interventions. The likelihood of acute life-threatening events (Type 1a.) is increased, and newly developed chronic diseases (Type 1b.) may also occur. If asymptomatic, but untreated, secondary sudden surgical emergencies may result. On reaching Mars (Type 2), reintroduction of partial gravity (0.38 G), flight-related reduced bone mineral density and potential changes in muscle mass, might lead to increased risk of lumbar disc herniations and traumatic injuries such as fractures. Without adequate space radiation shielding, surface conditions will likely increase the risk for development of malignancies and eye diseases such as cataracts. Communication delays (as long as 24 min each way) will require any immediate medical emergency to be managed in-situ by the expeditioners. Remotely operated robotic surgery is not currently feasible, as any communication lag &gt;100 ms will cause a perceptible delay, potentially affecting surgical outcomes. The provision of healthcare for Mars missions will face unique challenges in terms of both a hostile and extreme environment, and a remote and isolated location without real-time Earth communication. The medical team will need to be equipped to manage a very wide range of health conditions, including low acuity, chronic, and high acuity life-threatening issues, with some potentially requiring surgical intervention. Issues for the treating team include appropriate skill sets and access to medical guidance, facilities, equipment and supplies, and available pharmaceuticals. These significant challenges underlie the importance of adequate anticipation, preparation and planning for the healthcare needs of Mars explorers.

# 2. Methods

A scoping review of current scientific literature was conducted to assess potential medical conditions necessitating surgical intervention, categorized according to their severity, in anticipation of forthcoming long-duration and Mars missions. This study examines pertinent scientific publications available in English language up to 2022 sourced from Pubmed and Google scholar search engines, utilizing the keywords "surgery", "space", "Mars", "mission", "microgravity", "long-duration", "spaceflight", "telemedicine", "radiation", "robot-assisted", "artificial intelligence", "traditional", "advanced", "surgical techniques". Furthermore, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) Integrated Medical Model (IMM) and the Human Research Roadmap (HRR) also provide a foundation for the research. Astronomical and paleontological information from academic resources is also included.

# 3. The red planet

# 3.1. Reaching Mars

With respect to prevailing policies, strategies, and technological capabilities, the anticipated duration of a Mars mission is estimated to be approximately 3 years. Despite thorough pre-screening and exhaustive evaluations of astronauts, the adaptations induced by the space environment on all bodily systems may lead to the emergence of life-threatening conditions. Nonetheless, during lunar missions, the extended evacuation durations necessitate specialized medical capabilities to maintain crew stability during the transition. Conversely, during Mars missions, evacuation to definitive medical care is not feasible, accentuating the need to reevaluate and adapt the medical support framework to effectively manage heightened risks. In such a context, making well-informed medical decisions becomes more complex, particularly in terms of accurate diagnosis in the face of limited resources.

Extended human space exploration necessitates careful and comprehensive planning due to heightened inherent risks. Pro

longed mission durations, restricted telemedicine capabilities, limited resupply opportunities, and the inability to swiftly repatriate an ailing or injured astronaut to Earth for definitive medical care all contribute to the elevated risks associated with such missions and the need for a different approach to medical decision making and management. # 3.2. Astronomical features of the red planet

Mars has a radius about half that of the Earth (approximately  $3397\mathrm{km}$ ), resulting in a surface area akin to Earth's land area. Its mass is around one-tenth that of Earth  $(6.419\times 10^{23}\mathrm{kg})$  [8].

# 3.2.1. The Martian surface temperature

On average, Mars has a cold surface environment. In regions of low latitudes, the daily temperature span ranges from  $-100^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  to  $+17^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ , with an average of approximately  $-60^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ . During the winter season, temperatures near the poles can fall to as low as  $-130^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$  [8].

# 3.2.2. Atmosphere

The strength of a planetary body's gravitational field is the most important factor dictating whether or not it can retain an atmosphere; the stronger the field, the stronger the gravitational attraction on the molecules in the atmosphere. Mars has an extremely thin atmosphere with a surface pressure of about 6 mbar, mainly composed of  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$ . As a result, the 'greenhouse effect' is much weaker than on Earth. Surface pressure varies greatly based on elevation, from 11.6 mbar in Hellas Planitia to 0.3 mbar on Olympus Mons. Seasonal temperature changes can lead to pressure variations of up to 2.4 mbar at typical altitudes [8].

# 3.2.3. Water on Mars: unravelling the evidence

A substantial body of evidence suggests that Mars previously held substantial water reserves. Water undergoes sublimation on Mars, transitioning directly from solid ice to water vapor. The low pressure on the planet prevents water from being stable in liquid form. Seasonal exchange of  $\mathrm{CO}_{2}$  and  $\mathrm{H}_2\mathrm{O}$  occurs between the polar caps through the atmosphere [8].

The space environment is regarded as an austere (low-resource) context due to restricted availability of medical expertise, equipment, and consumable supplies [9]. Furthermore, due to signal delays, beyond a certain distance from Earth, real-time communication with the Mission Control Center is not feasible. The constrained availability of medications poses distinctive risks, necessitating alternative strategies to manage medical issues. In a context where resources are constrained, and there are heightened risk factors affecting both individual and crew health, making evidence-based medical decisions becomes more complex, particularly in terms of diagnostic accuracy [11,12]. Currently, diagnostic options are limited, with ultrasound being the only available imaging modality, with X-ray technology still under experimental consideration. Returning to basic medical principles, a comprehensive history and physical examination, alongside the utilization of AI and robotic assistance and available diagnostic resources, will be pivotal for effective medical decision-making and surgical strategizing. Numerous advancements in robotic technology on the ISS have introduced various models to assist astronauts in space. These robotic systems, ranging from those that interact with computer systems to those that can grapple spacecraft and detect hazards such as leaks, are set to become essential in future space missions. By handling routine tasks, maintenance, and operational support, robots enable astronauts to concentrate on research activities. Their role on the ISS significantly enhances mission capabilities, making them a critical component of station operations and a key factor in the success of human space exploration [13-16].

# 3.4. Surgical conditions and the pathophysiological background

NASA's Human Research Program aims to develop technologies and strategies to manage critical "red" risks associated with spaceflight. These risks are not isolated; they are deeply interconnected, reflecting the overlap of the five main spaceflight hazards and the interplay between environmental factors and human biology. This interconnected web is referred to as the "space exposome"—a holistic view that encompasses all spaceflight and lifetime exposures in relation to genetic factors, influencing the body's overall response. Understanding the space exposome within a systems biology approach will be essential for revealing the complex impacts of space environments on human health [17].

The Blophysical ANalysis of Cell death and chromosome Aberrations (BIANCA) biophysical model, when integrated with a radiation transport code, can estimate the Relative Biological Effectiveness (RBE) of galactic cosmic rays for both cell survival and chromosome aberrations in blood lymphocytes. 'Radiation transport code' is a computational tool that models the propagation and interactions of radiation with matter, predicting dose distributions and assessing shielding effectiveness. The RBE for cell survival is used to calculate equivalent doses concerning non-cancer effects, while the RBE for lymphocyte dicentrics is applied to assess stochastic effects, such as cancer risk [19]. Although current radiation protection technologies are not yet fully optimized and still pose health risks to astronauts, they offer considerable protection for Lunar and Mars missions. These strategies include limiting exposure through shielding and controlled mission duration; countermeasures like radioprotectors, radiomodulators, radiomitigators, and immune modulators; and treatments and supportive care to manage radiation-related effects [20-24].

NASA-STD-3001 Volume 1, Rev B, the Career Space Permissible Exposure Limit [V1 4030] stipulates that the total lifetime radiation dose from spaceflight for any astronaut must remain below  $600\mathrm{mSv}$ , applicable uniformly across all ages and genders [7]. # 3.4.2. Type 1b: newly developed, chronic conditions

Space radiation represents a key health risk [25] that warrants emphasis. This includes solar particle events (SPE), cosmic galactic radiation (CGR), radiation trapped in the Van Allen Belts, and secondary radiation. Within the space environment, the specific risks of consequently developing related medical conditions are heightened, with management likely spanning a wide spectrum of surgical disciplines. Notably, malignancies like breast or GI cancer emerge as substantial concerns [28-30]. In addition to malignancies, the impact of space radiation extends to the visual system, posing a high risk of cataract development [31]. Exposure to radiation also influences factors crucial to skin structure and wound healing [32,33]. The accelerated atherosclerosis resulting from ionizing radiation-induced endothelial damage should be taken into consideration, along with its associated consequences.

# 3.4.3. Type 2: Mars surface-related conditions

There are notable parallels between the challenges encountered in providing surgical care during both long-duration space missions and those anticipated during Mars surface missions. Arrival at Mars will involve a reintroduction of partial gravity at  $0.38\mathrm{G}$ , coupled with decreased bone mineral density and potential changes in muscle strength and mass resulting from extended space travel. These factors increase susceptibility to orthopaedic conditions such as lumbar disc herniations and traumatic injuries, including fractures. Transition between gravitational fields and adaptation to the altered gravitational force necessitates careful monitoring and potential interventions to mitigate the strain on the musculoskeletal system. Furthermore, the thin and toxic atmosphere on Mars presents additional hurdles, including heightened space radiation exposure and a wide range of temperature fluctuations. These environmental factors have implications for the overall health of the crew and may lead to or exacerbate specific medical conditions. Thus, the implementation of adequate radiation protection measures and effective thermal regulation systems will be essential in mitigating these risks. Understanding and addressing these multifactorial challenges is pivotal in ensuring the well-being and safety of astronauts during prolonged missions to Mars [17]. Programmatically, this encompasses the development of strategies to prevent and manage musculoskeletal injuries, the formulation of efficient radiation shielding methods [7], and the provision of reliable temperature control systems to maintain a stable and secure environment for the crew.

# 4. Conclusions

The "NASA-STD-3001 Levels of Care for Exploration Medical System Development" presents five proposed levels of medical care for human spaceflight. Table 4 below summarizes these levels together with the key relevant points regarding their application [7]. This structure reflects the increasing challenges facing provision of medical care as missions move further from close proximity to Earth and definitive care. Levels IV and V would be particularly applicable for Mars missions.

An extended exposure to significant space-related health risk factors, including weightlessness and space radiation, increases the likelihood of crew medical issues. While performing surgery in space is thought to be possible, it is advisable to avoid major surgical interventions when conservative or alternative treatments are viable and available. Healthcare providers must take into account essential factors such as possessing the requisite skills, the practicality of accessing medical guidance, and securing suitable facilities, equipment, supplies, and medications. The scale of these challenges for any space mission, and particularly one involving long-duration spaceflight and off-world habitation, highlights the utmost importance of thorough anticipation, careful preparation, and comprehensive planning, to effectively meet the healthcare needs of future Mars explorers.
